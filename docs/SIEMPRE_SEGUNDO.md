# SIEMPRE SEGUNDO

*"He was born second. He died first."*

A multigenerational saga — **feature screenplay + companion novel, both titled *Siempre Segundo* ("Always Second")** — loosely inspired by the life of Frank Arellanes, the Californio pitcher from Santa Cruz who died in the 1918 influenza. The title is the irony the whole story turns on: a man the world kept calling *second,* who was — as the coda reveals — *siempre primero,* first all along. **Fiction. Names and events invented.** Our hero is **Cirilo Méndez**. A motivational small-town-Chicano-to-hero story, kept **period-real to teach history** — with a tragic twist and a victory deeper than any trophy.

---

## The thesis — *Siempre Segundo*

> **This is what the picture is about.** It is an argument for the dignity of the runner-up — that a life is measured not by the trophy you lift but by how you keep working when you are *always second*: second-class, second-looked-at, second-chosen, the second of your blood ever to reach the top. Cirilo Méndez spends thirty-six years second, working anyway, reaching anyway — and the one autumn he finally becomes **first**, it kills him. **The moment he is no longer second is the moment he dies.** And yet the real first — the one death can't take — is the root he planted and the people he lifted. *Keep working. Especially when you're second. Siempre.*

Three things the story turns on:

- **The win is real, and it's the trap.** He pitches the 1918 Boston Red Sox to a true World Series title — and the very crowd that finally lifts a Mexican kid onto its shoulders is the breath that carries the flu that fells him weeks later. Being first, being *embraced* at last, is exactly what exposes him.
- **It is the wound of the Mexican-American.** A people who had their world taken and were told to keep their heads down and keep working — made second-class on the very land that was first theirs. *Siempre segundo* is at once a virtue to live by and an injustice to rage against, and the film holds both without resolving them.
- **The curse, worked from the inside.** Don Aurelio carries an old, private belief: that since the land was taken, the Méndez family has been **cursed to second** — that to be first is forbidden them, and the world collects when one of them forgets it. Cirilo's death seems to prove the old man right. The coda flips it: the *true* first — the son, the name the town can't stop saying — is the first that endures, and that is how the curse is finally broken.

---

## Themes & the leather thread

**Leather is the visual through-line** — the single object the camera (and the prose) returns to across three centuries, the way we keep the thesis in frame:

- **Hides as wealth.** On the rancho, sun-dried cowhides are stacked like money — "the banknotes of California" — and the family tans its own leather on its own land.
- **Leather as wage.** After the land is taken, the same hands that owned the hide trade trim another man's hides for a Prussian's wages at Kron's tannery. The craft survives; the standing is gone.
- **Leather as escape.** A baseball is stitched cowhide; a glove is tanned leather. The boy's way out of the tannery is the same material that trapped his father — reshaped into a ball and a mitt.
- **Leather as legacy.** The film opens and closes on a single worn glove. The last image is that glove in the small hands of his son.

Underneath the leather: **dispossession & reinvention**, **the rooted treated as foreign**, the cruel arithmetic of **always second**, and **the body that betrays** — first the breaking labor, then the fever in the air.

## Principal characters

- **CIRILO MÉNDEZ** — our pitcher (b. 1882). Quiet, proud, watchful; a cannon for a right arm. Born on the tannery floor; raised on the boardwalk. The last hope of a fallen family. The Boston papers will not let him keep even his name.
- **DON AURELIO MÉNDEZ** — the abuelo. Last patriarch of a rancho that no longer exists. Keeper of the Mexican past, the family's conscience, and the old belief that the Méndez are cursed to second. Our prologue narrator.
- **ESTEBAN MÉNDEZ** — Cirilo's father. A hide-trimmer at Kron's tannery. Wants his son safe and small; love expressed as fear.
- **DOLORES MÉNDEZ** — Cirilo's mother. The rosary, the language, the kitchen that holds the family together.
- **REBECCA WHITFIELD** — Anglo, wealthy; meets Cirilo at the beach (~17), then goes up to **Stanford** — which puts her in the grandstand the day Chuck's Stanford nine faces Cirilo's Santa Clara. The woman who crosses every line she was raised inside, and marries him.
- **CHARLEY "CHUCK" ASHBY III** — old San Francisco money, Rebecca's intended. Plays for **Stanford**. The embodiment of the class that inherited the family's land.
- **LUIS & ABEL MÉNDEZ** — Cirilo's older brothers; the meat market, the sandlot, the Santa Cruz Beachcombers.
- **ED BARROW** *(real)* — manager of the 1918 Red Sox; the man who finally gives the thirty-six-year-old his shot. *(He really managed that club, then went on to build the Yankee dynasty — a quiet irony to play.)*
- **BABE RUTH** *(real)* — Cirilo's 1918 teammate; pitcher-slugger, force of nature. Caught the flu *twice* in 1918 and lived. Cirilo catches it once and doesn't. The contrast does its own work.

## The Cast — deep backstories, the everyman lens & the secondary world

### The lens (Steinbeck, Dostoevsky)

The book and the script are told, wherever possible, from the eye level of the people who do the work — the tanner, the broken catcher, the widowed landlady, the barred Black ballplayer on the bench — because that is where the history actually lived. No one in it is a villain and no one is a saint; everyone is carrying something, and acting, mostly, out of fear or love or both at once, which are harder to tell apart than anyone admits. The Anglos who took the land are people, not weather; Chuck Ashby's gilded world is its own kind of prison, and we go inside it. The morality is grey on purpose. The interiority is the point — we are always closer to what a character tells themselves than to what they do, because the gap between the two is the whole subject.

### The principals, deepened

**Cirilo Méndez.** His silence is armor and also a wall, and it costs the people who love him; Rebecca and his mother both, at times, need him to speak, and he cannot, because he learned before he could read that the soft underside shown to a hostile world gets cut. He is not sure his ambition is a virtue. Late at night he wonders whether wanting to be first is the very pride his abuelo warned of — a vanity the world will punish — and whether his father was right, and safe-and-second is the only love a man like him is allowed. He wins, and feels guilty for what winning costs other men, and cannot stop.

**Rebecca Whitfield.** She loves him truly, and the love is tangled, in the honest way real love is tangled, with a willful need to be the author of her own life. There are nights in the cold flats when she wonders, with a shame she never speaks, whether she chose the man or the refusal — whether she would have loved him so fiercely if they had simply let her. Her mother died young; her father turned her into an ornament; she spent a girlhood being looked at and never once *seen*, which is why a bottle boy who looked at her like a person undid her in a single afternoon. Her growth is the discovery that she meant it after all — that the daily grind of the sacrifice is a harder and truer thing than the gesture of it, and that she is equal to it.

**Don Aurelio.** Keeper of the curse, and — in a way he cannot face — its author. He was young when the land went; he watched his own father die broken in the courthouse fight, and somewhere in that watching he made a vow of safety that hardened, over ninety years, into a doctrine of second. His guilt is enormous and unspoken: he taught his sons fear, watched Esteban shrink into the tannery and called it wisdom, and does not know, at the end, whether the curse is a truth he discovered or the excuse he built for his own failure to reach. He loves Cirilo, and is secretly terrified the boy will succeed and prove him a coward. The coda's reversal is his to earn.

**Esteban Méndez.** The father who loves in the only language the world left him: fear. He had a gift once, too — the fastest, the strongest, a boy who might have reached — and somewhere a reaching cost someone he loved, and he made himself small and has called it love ever since. He envies his son and is ashamed of the envy; he wants Cirilo safe partly to vindicate his own retreat. His fear-love is real love and real cowardice at once, and he knows it, and that knowledge is the ache he carries down to the tannery and back every single day.

**Dolores Méndez.** The silent strong one; the kitchen is her pulpit, and her faith is the family's spine, not its decoration. She came from even less than the rest of them, and she has buried two infants in the Santa Cruz ground and carries the grief without ever once displaying it. Her rebellions are quiet and total: she teaches the children the language; she keeps the rosary out when Esteban, frightened, wants it put away. She is hard toward Rebecca at first — the Anglo girl can only bring trouble — and the slow softening of that hardness is one of the book's quietest, truest arcs.

**Chuck Ashby III.** Not a villain — a wound. His father measured him his whole life against a dead elder brother and found him wanting, and Chuck inherited everything along with the private certainty that he deserved none of it. His feeling for Rebecca is the one true thing in a life of arrangements, which is exactly why losing her to the bottle boy curdles into something that lasts for decades. His cruelty is the cruelty of the insecure, never the strong. And in the box seat at the Series, for one unbearable afternoon, he understands precisely what he won and what he did not — and tells no one, ever, which is the most human thing about him.

**Luis & Abel Méndez.** Luis, the eldest, took the meat-market life and the weight of the family — steady, tired, half-resenting Cirilo's reaching and half-living through it. Abel, the wild one, was the better natural athlete and drank and brawled the gift away before it could cost him the way reaching costs; Cirilo's shadow-self, the road not taken, proof that the curse is sometimes just a man getting out of his own way before the world can.

### Ten lives along the road (the secondary world)

1. **Otto Kron** — the Prussian who owns the tannery. Not a monster: a hard, fair-in-his-fashion immigrant who clawed up in a town that called *him* a foreigner too, and who treats Esteban with a gruff, grudging, complicated near-respect even as he grows rich on his hands. He lets us smell the tannery — the lime, the bark, the bate — and see the cruel arithmetic by which one outsider is permitted to stand on the neck of another. *(Santa Cruz, the tannery.)*

2. **Father Aloysius Fagan, S.J.** — the Jesuit who finds Cirilo's arm. A genuine idealist who believes in the boy's soul, and a college man who needs to beat Stanford, and not at peace about which of the two is doing the recruiting. He wrestles his faith against the worldliness of it and mostly loses gracefully. He opens the door to the college — the Latin, the chapel bells, the class threshold. *(Santa Clara.)*

3. **Edward Purse** — the Beachcombers' barred Black star (real, and ruinous). Good enough to play for any nine in the county, made the *mascot* because the line would not bend, carrying a dignity so total it indicts the whole grandstand. His friendship with Cirilo is real and grey: there are days the sight of the Mexican *let onto the grass* is more than Purse can bear graciously, and a bitterness slips out, and is regretted, and is human. He is the book's conscience and its hardest mirror. *(The Beachcombers; the color line.)*

4. **"Deacon" Mulqueen** — Cirilo's first battery-mate, a broken old catcher in the Class D bushes who had three weeks in the big leagues in 1894 and has been drinking them ever since. He teaches the boy the road — the milk-run trains, the four-to-a-bed hotels, which towns will feed a brown ballplayer and which will not — and the long art of surviving a game that has already discarded you. Self-pitying, tender, ruined, and the truest friend Cirilo makes in the low minors. *(The bush leagues.)*

5. **Sören "Swede" Lindqvist** — the blond kid in left the derby scout writes down instead of Cirilo. Decent, limited, quietly ashamed; he knows, the way you know a draft in a room, that he is getting a life that belongs to the older man across the diamond, and he cannot give it back, and he half-hates himself and half-takes it, because a man and his family have to eat. The face of how injustice actually moves through the world — not through monsters, but through ordinary, frightened, decent people. *(The PCL.)*

6. **Aoife Doyle** — the Brooklyn landlady, an Irish widow with a gold-star flag in the window and a son in the French ground. Hard as a frozen stoop and unexpectedly, wrenchingly kind in the small hours; she lets Rebecca's rent slide the month the baby comes and would die before admitting it. Through her we get the tenement city — cabbage and coal smoke, church bells, war telegrams, the whole grieving immigrant Brooklyn the rosters were stripped to fill. *(Brooklyn.)*

7. **Nunzio Sabatini** — a Bay Area Italian, kiln-burned hands and a cannery wife, who plays winter ball with Cirilo and shares the particular fellowship of men the country has decided to insult in two different languages. He worships Ping Bodie — the real one, the Pezzolo boy who hit thirty over the fence and made the bleachers swallow their *wop* — and he carries the immigrant's whole argument with America inside one cheerful, exhausted body. He lets us see the Italian Bay Area: the kilns, the fishing wharf, the feast days. *(California; the immigrant labor world.)*

8. **Harry Hooper** *(real)* — the gentleman of the outfield, Hollister-born, who carried Cirilo's name in his pocket for years. Decent to the bone, and not without his own quiet guilt: he knows what the game does to men like Cirilo and Purse, and he benefits from a ladder he did not build and cannot quite bring himself to burn. The good man inside the unjust house, doing the one small right thing he can reach. *(The Red Sox; the bridge to Boston.)*

9. **Eleanor Ashby (née Vane)** — the woman Chuck marries instead of Rebecca. Poised, handsome, lonely, and far sharper than her husband knows; she married the chair at the club with her eyes open and has been quietly accounting the cost ever since. She sees Chuck's emptiness and her own with equal clarity and names neither aloud. Through her, Chuck's gilded world becomes a cage with people in it, rather than a wall to throw stones at — the only honest way to write the people who win. *(The Anglo upper class, from the inside.)*

10. **Danny Halloran** — the Boston sportswriter who hangs *El Cid* on Cirilo. Irish, two generations off the boat and the *No Irish Need Apply* signs, careless with a Mexican's name and genuinely, helplessly thrilled by the arm; he Anglicizes Cirilo half out of deadline laziness and half out of the recent-immigrant's instinct to climb by standing on the next man down. Not cruel, not kind — a man on a ladder, doing what the rung beneath his feet requires. Through him we get the press box, the bleachers, and the exact machinery by which a country renames the people it will not quite let in. *(Boston.)*

**And, deepened inside Chuck's world: Horace Whitfield** — Rebecca's father — is not old money but a self-made man terrified of falling back down, which is why his daughter's marriage does not anger him so much as *frighten* him: it threatens the respectability he bled a lifetime to buy. His cruelty, like Esteban's caution and Chuck's spite, is fear wearing a hard coat. Almost all of them are.

These come into the story not as a roster but as the rooms Cirilo passes through — each a place to stop and breathe and *smell*, each a life with its own weight, each a small window onto the history the textbooks flatten. The expansion pass weaves their pasts directly into the chapters: Mulqueen's three lost weeks in a freezing Class D depot; Purse's bitterness flaring and healing across a season of Sundays; Eleanor's clear-eyed loneliness threaded through the box-seat scene; Aoife Doyle's gold star turning the Brooklyn winter from backdrop into grief.

## Structure — three acts + prologue

**PROLOGUE — "California Banknotes" (1833–1882).** The Mexican golden age and its theft, told as Don Aurelio's memory: the secularization grant, the rancho at its height (vaqueros, the *matanza*, hides as money, the fandango, Branciforte rowdy across the river), the false promise of Guadalupe Hidalgo, the slow strangulation by the Land Act of 1851 and the drought, the fall into wage labor — ending on a match cut from a wet new hide to a newborn: Cirilo, 1882, born on the tannery floor.

**ACT I — "The Boardwalk" (~1900–1903).** Cirilo, eighteen, works the brand-new pleasure pier — labor in the leisure economy built on the land his family lost. The Whitfields summer at the beach; Rebecca and Cirilo collide. Chuck humiliates him; Cirilo answers with the only thing the Anglo world can't argue with — his arm. The attraction to Rebecca becomes perilous. A Santa Clara man sees the arm. Cirilo leaves the beach carrying the family's buried hope of rising again.

**ACT II — "The Rematch & The Long Wait" (~1901–1916).** Santa Clara as a class threshold. The hinge: the **Stanford–Santa Clara game** — Rebecca in the grandstand, Chuck at the plate, Cirilo on the mound; the boardwalk paid back with a strikeout, and Rebecca chooses. Then the cruelty of the long middle: a decade-plus of being passed over, the exotic "Spanish" spitballer who can't get his call to the bigs while lesser, paler men do. *Always second.* The act ends ~1917 with the call finally coming — and Cirilo and Rebecca marrying in the teeth of both their worlds.

**ACT III — "Siempre Primero" (1917–1918).** The bigs at last — at thirty-six, the oldest rookie anyone's seen, on Ed Barrow's **Boston Red Sox**, in a clubhouse with Babe Ruth. On **January 1, 1918**, a son is born. Then the season of his life: in a year cut short by the wartime "Work or Fight" order, he leads the league and the press crowns him the people's ace, and he pitches Boston to the **1918 World Series championship** (begun Sept 5 — the earliest ever). For one autumn the man who was always second is *first*. Then the flu that is already loose in Boston — Camp Devens, the packed victory streets, the deadliest month in American history — takes him within weeks. He dies the moment he is no longer second; the root of life and the legacy he leaves are the only first that lasts.

*(History note: there was no Rookie of the Year, 1947, or MVP award in 1918 — so he is "crowned" by leading the league and by the press, not by a trophy. All the period facts above are real.)*

## PROLOGUE — *California Banknotes*

### Novel prose

Before this was a town for strangers — before the lights on the water and the laughing on the pier — it was ours. All of it. The old man said it the way other men said prayers, which is to say he said it until the words wore smooth and stopped meaning anything to anyone but him.

In the year the bells came down — 1833, when Mexico unlocked the mission and handed God's land back to men — a governor's pen made the Méndez family rancheros. The grant ran from the river to the redwood line, hills the color of a lion in August, and on it the family put cattle and a chapel and a low white house that held the cool like a cellar. They were *gente de razón*: people of reason, people of name, Mexicans and Catholics and, above all, owners.

The wealth came on the hoof and left on the hide. Each spring the vaqueros rode the cattle down out of the hills for the *matanza*, and the killing-ground ran red and then the hides went out to dry, staked in their hundreds to the ground like a field of dark windows. The Yankee ships stood off the cove and the Méndez men rowed the hides out and rowed back with silver and silk and iron, because a cowhide was as good as coin in those years — better; men called them *California banknotes*, and laughed, and meant it. And on the rancho, in a shed that smelled of oak bark and lime, the family tanned its own leather from its own herds on its own ground. Remember that. The leather was theirs before it was anyone's.

Across the San Lorenzo the pueblo of Branciforte gambled and brawled and drank — a town the Spanish had stocked, a lifetime before, with the contents of a Guadalajara jail — and on feast nights the guitars carried over the water to the rancho's own fandango, so that the sacred bank of the river and the profane bank of the river sang to each other in the dark. It seemed it would last forever. Nothing built on land ever does.

The thunder came from the north. A flag at Sonoma; a war; the stars-and-stripes run up over Monterey where the eagle of Mexico had flown that morning. And then, in '48, the paper that was supposed to save them: Guadalupe Hidalgo, which swore on the honor of the United States that the grants would be respected, that what was theirs would stay theirs. Don Aurelio's father believed it. That was his only crime, and it cost him everything.

For the conquest did not come with a sword. It came with a courthouse. The Land Act of 1851 made every Californio prove, in English, before a commission in a city three days' ride north, that the ground under his own chapel was his — and even the families who won bled to death paying the lawyers who won it for them. Squatters fenced the range. The cattle wandered and were not returned. Then the sky itself turned against them: the flood that drowned the valley, and after it the long drought of '64 that left the hills white with bone. They sold the rancho the way a man bleeds — not all at once, but a little at a time, until there is no more.

By the time Don Aurelio was an old man, the new Santa Cruz had risen on the grave of his: lime kilns smoking on the hill, the long wharf walking out into the bay, strangers' money everywhere and the family's name on nothing. The language went indoors. The rosary stayed out. And somewhere in those years the old man began to believe the thing he would never say aloud — that the family was *cursed* now, cursed to second, that the conquest had not only taken their land but their right to ever again be first at anything; and that the world would collect, hard, from any Méndez who forgot it.

And on a winter morning in 1882, his son Esteban walked down off the hill and through the gate of Kron's tannery to do for a Prussian's wages the one thing the Méndez had always known how to do — turn hide into leather — only now the hide was another man's, and the leather was another man's, and all that was left to the family was the knowing. *They left us our hands,* the old man would say. *So we gave them to the leather.*

That same morning, in the back room where the women waited, a child was born — wet and red and roaring, the way a new hide comes off the beam. They wiped him clean and the old man looked at the boy's hands, already big, already strong, and did not yet know what they would do. He only knew the family was not finished. Not while there was a Méndez with hands.

## ACT I — *The Boardwalk* (1900)

### Screenplay — the meeting

```
EXT. SANTA CRUZ BEACH -- THE NEW PLEASURE PIER -- DAY (1900)

Fresh-cut planks, white paint, electric bulbs strung against
a blue sky. The leisure economy, newborn -- built on the very
ground the prologue mourned.

CIRILO MÉNDEZ (18) hauls a crate of bottles down the boards.
He does not look at the summer people. He has learned not to.

A BANNER: "TEST YOUR ARM -- 3 BALLS A NICKEL." Cirilo takes
his post behind a pyramid of iron milk-bottles.

CHUCK ASHBY III (20), white flannels, steers REBECCA WHITFIELD
(17) to the booth. She is already bored of him.

CHUCK
Watch. I rowed stroke at the club.

He throws three. Misses, misses, clips the pyramid -- it holds.
```

### Novel prose — the same beat, interior

He had learned not to look at the summer people, which was a thing you learned the way you learned not to touch the lime vats — once, and never again. So he was looking at the bottles, and the worn ball in his hand, when the girl laughed.

It was not the laugh they used on the pier, the bright tin one the young ladies kept in their purses with their handkerchiefs. It came out of her sideways and surprised, as if he had knocked it loose along with the bottles. Cirilo had thrown a baseball through six iron bottles and felt nothing; one honest laugh from a stranger and his ears went hot.

The boy in the white flannels had felt it too, the shift in the air, the way a dog feels weather. *Aren't they weighted, boy.* Cirilo knew that voice. It was the voice that had bought the rancho at the courthouse and never once had to raise itself. He had been answering it with his silence his whole life, because silence was the only thing they couldn't take and couldn't use against you. But the girl was holding out a nickel and looking at him as though he were a person and not a fixture of the pier, and the old man's voice was somewhere in him saying *not while there is a Méndez with hands* — and Cirilo understood, with the flat cold clarity of a thing already decided, that the silence was going to cost him more than it ever had.

"Then here's a nickel," she said.

He took it. Her glove was kid leather, soft as a thing his father might have made in another life, when leather still belonged to them. He gave her a ball, and tried not to think about whose hands had tanned the hide of her glove, and failed.

## ACT II — *The Rematch & The Long Wait* (1903–1917)

### The shape of the act — five sequences

1. **The Rematch (1903).** The Stanford–Santa Clara game below: Cirilo strikes out Chuck, and Rebecca stands and claps in a sea of cardinal red — choosing, in public, the wrong boy.
2. **The Break (1903–04).** Rebecca defies the Whitfields. They hand her an ultimatum; she takes the disinheriting. Chuck, humiliated twice now (the booth, the mound), turns from careless cruelty to real enmity — the kind that waits.
3. **The Climb That Stalls (1904–1915).** Cirilo becomes a star of the **Pacific Coast League** (Sacramento, the Seals), masters the **spitball** (legal then), and is, by any honest measure, big-league ready for a decade. The call never comes. Scouts admire and pass; the reasons are never said aloud. *Siempre segundo* — best in his league, second-class in his country. Years compress in a montage of seasons, exotic press names, and Don Aurelio's warnings not to reach.
4. **The Marriage in the Margins (~1916).** They wed at **Holy Cross**, in the teeth of both worlds (scene below). Rebecca, the heiress, lives the grind by choice — cheap rooms, barnstorming towns. A low season (an injury; the old temptation to quit). And **Chuck resurfaces** — prosperous, respectably married, appearing trackside to twist the knife: *"You could have had everything, Rebecca."*
5. **The War Opens the Door (1917).** America enters the war; the **"Work or Fight"** order and the draft strip the young arms off to France. Ed Barrow's depleted **Red Sox**, desperate for pitching, finally call the thirty-five-year-old. The bitter truth under the dream: he gets his shot because the white boys are in the trenches — and he takes it anyway, because you take it anyway. Rebecca is pregnant; the baby is due on New Year's. The train goes east. → **Act III.**

She paid for it in full and on time. The allowance stopped; the name closed over her like water; the friends she had grown up beside learned, all at once, not to see her on the street. And Rebecca Whitfield, who had never in her life made a bed or counted a dollar twice, went to live in the margins of a country that had decided she no longer existed — and found, to the genuine astonishment of everyone who had warned her, that she had never once been happy until she did.

### Sequence 3 — The Long Wait: the climb, level by level (1904–1916)

*(Where the picture gets drunk on baseball — the smells, the rungs, the day-in-the-life — and where Don Aurelio, who has watched the game in California since the Gold Rush sandlots, becomes Cirilo's teacher and the audience's. Period-real throughout; the real figures are named.)*

He went out into organized ball the only way a brown kid could in those years — at the very bottom — and he climbed it a rung at a time, and the country let him climb exactly as high as it could stand him, and not one rung higher.

**The bush leagues — Class D, then C.** The floor of the professional game: valley towns baking under a white sky, cinder infields that took the skin off a slide, wooden grandstands leaning in the heat. A few dollars a month, four men to a hotel bed, the milk-run trains that stopped at every water tank between Stockton and nowhere, meal money that did not cover the meal. The *smell* of that life, which he would know in his sleep for the rest of it: wintergreen liniment and cut alfalfa, coal smoke and tobacco spit and the wet-dog reek of flannels that never fully dried. He learned the trade here by failing at it in front of nobody. And he learned the first hard law of the business, the one Don Aurelio made him repeat until it stopped making him angry — that **he did not own his own arm.** Under the **reserve clause**, the club held his contract for exactly as long as it pleased: it could sell him, bury him in Class D until he rotted, or cut him loose into nothing, and there was not one thing he or any other player, in the bushes or the big leagues, could do about it. *You are leather,* the old man told him, not unkindly. *They will buy you and sell you and never once ask.*

**Home — the Beachcombers, Beach Flats.** When the climb let him back to Santa Cruz it was to the real pro club, the **Beachcombers** of the Pacific States League, playing the ground behind the **Leibbrandt bath houses** — so close to the sand that the brine and the boardwalk barkers came over the outfield fence with the gulls (the same pier from Act I, watching him now from the other side of the rail). The **Daubenbiss brothers**, Ed and Charley of the old Soquel family, ran the nine with **Bill "Red Dog" Devereaux** of Oakland; a Sunday crowd came a thousand strong in its best clothes, and a local rhymer crowed after they beat the City: *Who killed the Bushnells? We — the Beachcombers — three-baggers and homers.* Cirilo's brothers Luis and Abel played beside him. And on the bench in street clothes sat **Edward Purse** — good enough to start for any club in the county, kept off the grass and called the team's *mascot*, because the color line would not let a Black man play, his heart breaking by inches in plain view every Sunday. Cirilo watched Purse and understood the thing no one ever said to his face: that there were lines drawn on this country no glove could carry you across, and that he himself stood with one foot on one of them — let onto the field only so long as the papers could keep calling him *Spanish.*

**The lessons the old man carried.** Don Aurelio had watched ball in California since men played it bare-handed on Gold Rush sandlots, and he doled out the game like a second catechism — sequencing, deception, the geometry of a hitter's fear — and he salted it with the real ones he'd seen come up. He'd watched **Hal Chase**, "Prince Hal," the Los Gatos boy, climb out of the Coast League and go east and go *crooked*, and he warned Cirilo that the game devours the ones who sell it. He'd known the Pezzolo family in the City and watched their boy **Ping Bodie** — a *wop* kid, they sneered, the way they'd sneer *greaser* at Cirilo — become the first man to hit thirty over a Coast League fence, in 1910, proof that an immigrant kid's bat could make the country choke its own slurs back down.

**The Coast League — the San Francisco Seals.** The highest rung he could reach and still be told no: the **Pacific Coast League**, graded Class A and then **Double-A from 1909**, the "third major league" — 180-game seasons in the soft coast light, the best ball west of the Mississippi, good enough that men turned down the East to stay in it. He starred there for years. He beat, to their faces, men who got the call he never got. The scouts came and admired and wrote one word in their books that he was never allowed to read, and they signed the paler arm. *Siempre segundo* — the best pitcher in the best league the East refused to call major. The one who got clear and stayed decent was **Harry Hooper**, the Hollister kid who'd played Soquel ball a ridge away, gone to Boston, made himself a star and a gentleman — and who, years on, would be the one to remember the old Coast spitballer and say the sentence that changed everything: *there's an arm out west nobody's using.*

**Brooklyn — the bottom of the top.** The call finally came, late, in his thirties — and it was to **Brooklyn**, Uncle Robbie's Robins, the National League's most dependable heartbreak, a ballclub that knew being second the exact way Cirilo did. The thing he'd chased fifteen years, and it nearly starved him. The reserve clause pinned an old rookie's pay to the floor; Brooklyn rent ate the floor; and with Rebecca beside him and a child on the way he stood in a major-league uniform and *still* could not make a month meet itself. So he did what his father did before him: in the winters he went home and back through the **tannery gate**, the championship-bound arm trimming another man's hides to cover the Brooklyn rent — leather to leather, the thread his great-grandfather started never once breaking. Thirty-five years old, in the major leagues, and second-class still. He nearly quit. Rebecca would not let him. And then, in 1917, with the war stripping the rosters bare — *work or fight* — a Boston man bought the old spitballer's contract for almost nothing, the way you'd buy a used glove off a nail, on the word of a Hollister boy who never forgot the ridge he came from. Cirilo packed for the Red Sox not knowing he was packing for the only October that would ever matter. → **Act III.**

## ACT III — *Siempre Primero* (1918) — THE CLIMAX

Sixteen years he waited. The boy from the tannery floor — passed over a hundred times, the wrong color for the call that kept going to lesser arms — finally stands in a big-league clubhouse at thirty-six, on **Ed Barrow's Boston Red Sox**, two lockers down from a wild twenty-three-year-old named **Babe Ruth**. On New Year's Day his son was born. And in a season cut to pieces by the war's "Work or Fight" order, the old man who was always second leads the whole league — and the Boston papers, who cannot be bothered to spell *Cirilo Méndez*, crown him anyway, under a name they invent for him.

### The season (1918) — novel prose

He got his season the way a drowning man gets a plank: because the boat had already gone down. The war had pulled the young arms to France under *work or fight*, and the rosters that came north that spring were old men and boys and Cirilo Méndez — thirty-six, and finally, absurdly, a rookie.

And he was magnificent.

He pitched the way Don Aurelio had taught him behind a backstop in Beach Flats a lifetime before: reading a man's feet before he read his swing; busting the deep-standing hitters up and in where their fear could not reach; feeding the white-knuckle sluggers the slow wet one and letting their own greed swing for them; never showing a pair of eyes the same height twice. The spitball he'd carried up from the Coast League dropped off the table like it had died in the air, and the American League — in the last summer before they would outlaw the pitch forever — had no answer for a thirty-six-year-old who threw a dying ball with a surgeon's mind behind it.

He led them all. Most wins. Fewest runs allowed. The Boston writers, who could not be troubled to spell *Cirilo Méndez*, reached for something grand and handed him **EL CID** — and the bleachers took it up, and for one summer Fenway chanted a conqueror's name at a tanner's son and never once heard the joke folded inside it. **Harry Hooper**, the Hollister boy who'd put his name in, watched from right field and told anyone who would listen that the best arm in the league had been standing out west the whole time, unused, because no one had thought to look at a brown man twice. Even **Ruth** — twenty-three, enormous, already a legend and already, twice that year, knocked flat by the same fever loose in the camps — would drift over between his own starts just to watch the old man throw the thing nobody could hit.

**Barrow** rode him hard, and Cirilo let him, because sixteen years of *no* had taught him exactly what a *yes* was worth. By September the second-place country had itself a first-place team, and the man who had been second his whole life had, for the first time, a clear road to first — at the one thing the whole country had stopped to watch.

### Novel prose — the real victory, and the curse broken

They buried him in Santa Cruz, because Rebecca understood that a man belongs to the ground that made him even when that ground was stolen, and she carried him home on the train across the whole width of the country he was never quite allowed to belong to.

And the town came. That was the thing none of them expected — the town came. The tannery shut its gates for the afternoon, the first time it had ever shut them for a Mexican, and Esteban's old crew stood with their cracked leather hands folded. The fishermen came up from the wharf. The families came down out of the flats and in from the orchards, the people who spoke the language only indoors, and they stood in the open and spoke it, because the boy in the box had made it so that for one season the whole country had to learn to say a name like theirs — even the wrong version of it, even *Cy the Spaniard* — and say it with its hat off.

Don Aurelio stood at the grave on the edge of land that had once run from the river to the redwoods, all of it theirs, none of it theirs. He had believed his whole life that the family was cursed to second, and that his grandson had broken the law and paid for it, and standing there he understood at last that he had read the curse exactly backward.

Because here is what a trophy is: a cup in a case in a city far away, and the men who carried Cirilo Méndez on their shoulders will be old men and then no men, and the cup will gather the dust of strangers. That first was real, and it was glorious, and it was borrowed, and it was brief — the way being first in *their* world always is. If that had been the only first he ever reached, then yes, the curse was real and it had won.

But here is what a root is. In the summers after, the boys of the flats played ball on the flat ground by the river where the rancho's cattle had once come down to drink, and when a kid threw one nobody could hit, the others went quiet and somebody said it — *throws like Méndez* — and not one of them had ever seen him pitch. They only knew the story: that one of theirs, a tannery son off this exact dirt, had stood on the highest mound in the country and been *first*, and had kept working when the answer was no for sixteen straight years, and that the working had been the man, not the cup. The town that had spent a century trying to forget the family could not, now, stop saying its name.

*That* was the first that did not gather dust. That was the first the courthouse and the conquest and the fever in the air could never staple a stranger's name to — and it was the one the old man had been too grief-blind, his whole life, to see was even available to them. The curse was never that the Méndez couldn't be first. It was that they kept believing they couldn't.

They had hung the name "El Cid" on him as a joke and an erasure — a Mexican made into a Spanish conqueror because the papers could not let him be a hero as himself. They were more right than they knew. In the old legend the Cid's own body, lashed upright in the saddle, had led one last charge and won a battle after his death — and so, in the only way that ever lasts, did Cirilo Méndez.

The boy grew up on that ground with his father's glove, the leather his great-grandfather tanned, soft now from a third generation of hands. He was the root. He was the legacy. He was the first that lasts.

The last image is his small hands closing around the ball.

Already big. Already strong.

## _Siempre Segundo_ — Prologue, novelized (full chapters)

### Chapter — The Grant

Before this was a town for strangers — before the lights on the water and the laughing on the pier — it was ours. All of it. Don Aurelio would say it the way other men say prayers, which is to say he said it until the words wore smooth and stopped meaning anything to anyone but himself; and yet it was true, and the truth of it was the whole foundation the family stood on, long after the family had nothing else left to stand on at all.

In 1833 the young Mexican republic — hungry, idealistic, and broke — did a thing both generous and ruinous: it unlocked the missions, broke the friars' long hold on the coast, and handed God's land back to men. A governor's agent rode down from Monterey with a leather folio of grants, and one of them, when the dust settled, made Don Joaquín Méndez — Cirilo's great-great-grandfather, a blunt, devout, illiterate man who signed with a cross and wept doing it — a *ranchero.* The grant ran from the river to the redwood line, some leagues of hills the color of a lion in August, and Don Joaquín stood at the edge of it the first evening and could not speak, because no man in his family, as far back as the family could count, had ever owned anything a king could not take back on a whim.

They raised the low white adobe that held the cool like a cellar, and a chapel beside it, and ran the first cattle down into the bottoms, and for one brief generation the valley belonged, more or less, to the people who worked it. *Gente de razón,* they called themselves — people of reason, people of name — Mexicans and Catholics and, before and above all of it, owners.

But the hands that built the adobe and worked the cattle were not, most of them, Méndez hands, and this is the part of the story Don Aurelio told less often, and more quietly, and only when he was being honest — which old age made him more, and not less. The vaqueros and the diggers and the women at the Méndez fires were Ohlone, Awaswas people, the ones who had been at the mission before there was a Méndez grant to work, who came out of the broken mission with their old world gone and their new one already owned by men like Don Joaquín. The finest of the Méndez riders was one of them — a lean, silent man the family called Esteban, the *first* Esteban, the one Cirilo's father would be named for three generations on — who could gentle a wild horse with his breath and his hands, and who had been born, the mission books said, in a village whose name no one living could any longer pronounce. He worked the land the Méndez had been given. He owned none of it. The family that would spend the next hundred years being dispossessed had been handed its one brief kingdom, in part, on the back of a man more dispossessed than they would ever be.

It is a particular thing, to be born into a family that owned the ground beneath its own feet, and then to watch that ownership carried off across the length of a single life. It teaches you a lesson the secure never have to learn — that everything is a loan, and that the lenders, when they come to collect, do not knock. But there is a harder lesson underneath it, the one Don Aurelio reached only at the very end: that his own people had, for one generation, been lenders too; that the wheel which ground them had ground others first beneath their own weight; and that the only hands in the whole long story that were ever truly clean were the hands of the people who had never been given anything to dirty them with.

### Chapter — California Banknotes

The wealth came on the hoof and left on the hide, and the whole bright machinery of it ran on blood and salt and the patience of men who owned nothing.

Each spring the vaqueros rode the cattle down out of the hills for the *matanza* — the great killing — and for a week the bottoms ran red and the gulls came inland in white storms and the work went on from dark to dark. The Ohlone riders did the killing and the Méndez men kept the count, and the women boiled the tallow in iron pots big enough to bathe a child in, and the hides went out to dry, staked in their hundreds to the earth like a field of dark windows turned up at the sky. It was filthy, holy, exhausting work, and the smell of it — tallow smoke and blood and salt and green hide — was the smell of money, exactly the way the tannery's stink would be the smell of money to the same family eighty years on, when the hides belonged to a Prussian and the family only worked them.

For in those years a cowhide was as good as coin, and better. The Yankee ships came round the Horn and stood off the cove, and their captains — lean, courteous, devouring men, the polite leading edge of an empire that had not yet bothered to announce itself — traded silver and silk and Boston boots and bolts of cloth for the *cueros* the rancheros rowed out to them. *California banknotes,* the traders called the hides, and laughed when they said it, and meant it, and wrote down in their ledgers, in a language no ranchero could read, the exact future price of everything. One of them, a Captain Bray out of Boston who came three seasons running, ate at the Méndez table and admired the chapel and learned to dance the jota badly and with great good will, and who would live long enough to file, in 1852, one of the squatter claims that helped take the grant apart. He bore the family no ill will at all. That was the terrible thing about the men who took it: so few of them bore any ill will. They wanted the land the way water wants the downhill, and they came, and they were charming, and they stayed for supper — and then one morning the supper was theirs.

And on the rancho, in a shed that smelled of oak bark and lime, the family tanned its own leather, from its own herds, on its own ground. Hold on to that through everything that follows: the leather was theirs before it was anyone's. The hands knew the work because the work had once been a kind of kingship.

Across the San Lorenzo, the rough little pueblo of Branciforte gambled and brawled and drank — a town the Spanish crown had stocked, a lifetime before, with the swept-out contents of a Guadalajara jail, petty criminals for whom a stretch of empty California coast had been a sentence and a mercy at once — and on feast nights the guitars from the Méndez fandango carried over the black water to meet the fiddles and the cursing of the pueblo, so that the sacred bank of the river and the profane bank of the river sang to each other across the dark, and a boy lying awake in the white adobe could believe the whole arrangement of the world was permanent, and benevolent, and his.

It seemed it would last forever. It lasted about as long as one strong man's memory. Nothing built on land ever lasts longer than that — the land outlives everyone, and keeps no one's name, and that is the first lesson it teaches and the last, and almost no one learns it until the deed is already in another man's hand.

### Chapter — The Fandango

There was one night Don Aurelio returned to all his life, though he had not been born in time for it; he had it from his father, who had it as a boy, and he told it so often and so well that it became, in the family, less a memory than a kind of scripture — the night of the great fandango, the high-water mark of everything, the last good year before any of them knew there had ever been a last good year.

It was the close of the *matanza,* the spring the hides ran to nine hundred, and Don Joaquín — the patriarch who had wept signing the grant — threw the whole rancho open. They roasted a steer and two pigs over pits dug that afternoon; they hung lanterns in the oaks; the women had been three days at the tamales and the women's mothers three days at the women. By dark the yard was full: Méndez cousins down from Branciforte, rancheros from three valleys, the vaqueros in their best with silver on their hatbands earned across a hard year — and the Ohlone families who worked the place gathered at the edge of the light, where the custom of the country kept them, near enough to the music to dance to it and far enough from the fire to remember whose fire it was.

A guitar started, and then another, and then old blind Cipriano's fiddle, and the jota went up into the oaks and the yard began to turn. And the finest dancer of the whole night — every account agreed, even the ones that wished otherwise — was the Ohlone vaquero the family called Esteban, the first Esteban, the one Cirilo's father would be named for three generations on: a man who could gentle a horse with his breath and who danced the way he rode, all stillness and sudden grace, his boots going like a drum on the packed earth while the rest of him stayed quiet as a held note. The Yankee was there too — Captain Bray, off the hide ship, three seasons a guest, learning the jota badly and laughing at himself and being loved for the laughing, a courteous, devouring man who admired the chapel and the dancing and the daughters, and who was, though no soul in that firelight could have known it, the polite advance scout of the very thing that would carry all of this off inside ten years. He danced with Don Joaquín's nieces. He toasted the old man's health in bad Spanish. And he noted, meaning nothing by it yet, the run of the creek and the line of the hills.

And a boy of nine — the boy who would grow into Don Aurelio's father, who would believe the treaty and lose the rancho and end with a measure of his mind gone — sat on the chapel step for half the night and watched it all: the turning yard, the leaping fire, his grandfather presiding like a king over a country that had not yet been told it was about to stop being a country. He carried that night intact through everything that came after, the way a man carries the one photograph he saved from a fire, and he handed it down to his son Aurelio, who handed it down the line — so that eighty years later, on a tannery floor and a Brooklyn ball field and a Boston deathbed, some buried Méndez cell still remembered, without knowing that it remembered, the smell of tallow smoke and crushed sage and the sound of a blind man's fiddle going up into the oaks on the last good night of the world that was theirs. It seemed it would last forever. It is always, in every life and every country, the nights that seem they will last forever that turn out, looked back upon, to have been the last.

### Chapter — The Courthouse

The conquest, when it came, did not come with a sword. It came with a courthouse, which is worse, because a sword at least admits what it is.

The thunder came from the north first, in 1846: a flag run up by a mob of American settlers at Sonoma, with a bear on it that looked, the Californios said, like a badly drawn pig; then a war that was over almost before the rancho had heard it begun; then the stars and stripes raised over the customhouse at Monterey on a July morning where the eagle of Mexico had flown the day before. Don Joaquín was an old man by then. He rode up to see it, and came home and said nothing for three days, and then said only: *They have planted their flag. Now we will see whether they keep their word.*

In 1848 came the word, on paper, signed and sworn: the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, by which the United States, upon its honor, guaranteed to the conquered Californios the full enjoyment of their property — that what was theirs would remain theirs. Don Joaquín's son, Don Aurelio's own father, a gentler and more trusting man than the old ranchero, believed it entirely. That was his single crime, and it cost him the rancho, his health, and at the last a measure of his mind.

For the new country did not take the land at gunpoint. It took it by *process,* which is slower, and leaves no scar a man can point to. The Land Act of 1851 required every Californio to appear before a commission — seated in San Francisco, three hard days to the north — and prove, in English, with documents, before American lawyers, that the ground beneath his own chapel was lawfully his; and the burden of proof lay on the man who already held the land. Don Aurelio went north with his father, a boy of fourteen carrying a leather folio of Mexican grants and *diseños* — hand-drawn maps with a sycamore and a creek-bend and a cattle skull for boundary markers — into a paneled room where a commissioner named Hollister, a decent and overworked man who privately thought the whole business a disgrace and approved the claim in the end, took four years to do it. Four years of an attorney named Quint, of San Francisco, who took his fee in land because the family had no cash: a quarter-league for a filing, a quarter-league for an appeal, so that the family won its case and lost its rancho in the same long breath, paying the men who saved the title with the very acres the title covered. Quint was not a wicked man. He had a wife and a pew and a reputation for fairness. He simply understood, as the rancheros did not, that in the new country the land itself was no longer the wealth — the *paper* was the wealth; and he dealt in paper, and the Californios came to him holding deeds in a dead king's hand and walked out holding nothing.

And then, as though the courthouse had not been enough, the sky turned. The great flood of the early sixties drowned the valley bottoms and the herds with them, and behind the flood came the long drought of '64, two years without rain, the hills gone white with the bones of cattle that had been California banknotes and were now not worth the cost of skinning. The cattle economy that had built the rancho died in a single dry season, up and down the whole coast, and the Méndez sold what the lawyers had left them the way a man bleeds — not all at once, but a parcel for a debt and a parcel for a winter, until there was no more to sell.

It was somewhere in those years that Don Aurelio, a young man watching his father fail, began to believe the thing he would carry the rest of his life and never quite say aloud: that the family was *cursed* now — cursed to second, that the conquest had taken not only the land but the right ever again to be first at anything — and that the world would collect, hard and without mercy and on its own schedule, from any Méndez fool enough to forget it. He was wrong. But he had earned the error honestly, in a paneled room in San Francisco, watching a decent man named Hollister take four years to hand his family back a kingdom that a decent man named Quint had already quietly carried off in his briefcase.

### Chapter — The Hearing

And here is the other night the family carried, the black twin of the fandango: the day in San Francisco, ten years on, when they began to lose all of it in a paneled room.

Don Aurelio was fourteen, and he had come north with his father on the coastal steamer — three days of seasickness and dread — carrying a leather folio that held the whole of the family's claim to the earth it had lived on for twenty years: the Mexican grant with the governor's seal, and the *diseño,* the map, drawn in his grandfather's own hand, a thing of beauty and uselessness, with a sycamore for one corner and the bend of the creek for another and a cattle skull, carefully inked, to mark where the land met the sea. In the country that had made that map, every man who mattered knew the sycamore and the creek-bend and the skull. In the country that had replaced it, the map was a child's drawing, and the room they carried it into did not run on knowing the land. It ran on paper, and on English, and on time — of which it had an infinite supply and the Méndez had none.

The commissioner was a man named Hollister, gray and overworked and — Aurelio decided, across the four years it took — not a bad man: one who privately thought the whole proceeding a national disgrace, and who would in the end rule for the family; but who had four hundred such claims stacked on his docket and one ordinary mortal lifespan in which to hear them, and who therefore let each grind forward at the speed of cold honey, asking through an interpreter the same question a fourth and a fifth time, requiring a boundary witness who had to be fetched three hundred miles, adjourning, adjourning, adjourning. And the family's own attorney — a courtly San Franciscan named Quint, who took the case because there was a fee in it, and the fee, since the Méndez had no cash, was *land* — sat at the father's elbow through all of it being, by the standards of his profession, scrupulously fair: a quarter-league deeded over for the filing, a quarter-league for the first appeal, a quarter-league when the rival claim (entered, as it happened, by a genial retired sea-captain named Bray, who bore the family no ill will at all) had to be answered.

Aurelio sat on a hard bench against the wall and watched his father — who had believed the treaty — come slowly to understand, across four years, that he had been robbed without anyone in the room ever once doing a thing a sheriff could arrest them for; that the robbery *was* the process, not a break in it; that he would win, and be ruined by the winning, and that there was no man he could call out, no door he could kick, no single villain anywhere in the whole patient machine. It was the having-no-one-to-hate, Don Aurelio would say eighty years later, that broke his father's mind. A man can survive being robbed. It is being robbed *politely* — lawfully, by decent men following the rules, with a receipt — that there is no surviving.

They rode home down the coast with the title confirmed and the rancho three-quarters gone, and Don Joaquín met them at the landing, very old now, and read his son's face before a word was spoken, and only nodded — the way you nod at news you have been expecting for years. *They kept their word,* the old man said at last, looking out at the hills that were no longer theirs. *That was the cruelest thing they could have done. If they had only lied to us, we would at least have had something left to forgive.* And he turned and walked up toward the chapel, and that was the last anyone remembered him saying about it, ever.

### Chapter — The Tannery Floor

By the time Don Aurelio was an old man, the new Santa Cruz had risen entire on the grave of the old. Lime kilns smoked on the hills where the cattle had grazed; the long wharf walked out into the bay; the Powder Works thumped down the river and the tanneries steamed along the creek, and strangers' money was everywhere, and the family's name was on nothing at all. The language went indoors. The rosary stayed out — that one act of defiance, kept up in the window where the street could see it, the last unsold acre of the rancho.

And on a winter morning in 1882, Don Aurelio's son Esteban — named for the silent Ohlone vaquero who had gentled horses on land the family no longer owned — walked down off the hill and through the gate of Otto Kron's tannery to do, for a Prussian's wages, the one thing the Méndez had always known how to do: turn hide into leather. Only now the hide was another man's, and the leather was another man's, and the lime that had once tanned the family's own kingship into boots and harness only ate, slowly, at a hired man's hands. *They left us our hands,* the old man would say in the years after, to anyone who would sit still for it. *So we gave them to the leather.* It was the closest he ever came to telling the whole story, and most people thought it only an old man's saying, and let it pass.

That same morning, in the back room where the women waited and Dolores held the basin, a child was born — wet and red and roaring, the way a new hide comes off the beam. They wiped him clean, and the old man, ninety years from the kingdom and twelve from his own grave, bent over the basket and took the small hands in his and turned them in the gray light coming through the tannery window. Already big. Already strong. And he did not yet know what they would do, only that whatever the courthouse and the conquest and the long bleeding century had carried off from this family, they had not, somehow, carried off *this* — the hands were unbroken, and they were the family's own, and no lawyer's briefcase and no squatter's fence and no commissioner's four-year patience had ever yet managed to carry a man's hands away from him while he lived.

The family was not finished. Not while there was a Méndez with hands.

And so the whole long story that follows — the boardwalk and the booth and the girl who laughed; the strikeout and the climb and the man in the derby; the cold flat in Brooklyn and the great roaring autumn and the fever that came in on the cheering — all of it begins here, on a tannery floor, in the shadow of a stolen rancho, with a dying old man holding a newborn's hands up to a gray window and refusing, one last time, against ninety years of his own hard evidence, to believe that his family had been born to be second.

His name was Cirilo. Remember the hands. Everything that matters in this whole long story, in the end, is in the hands.

## _Siempre Segundo_ — Act I, novelized (full chapters)

### Chapter — The Boardwalk

The town had a new face that summer, and it wore it down at the water.

Where for fifty years there had been only sand and the long wharf and the fishing smacks, a man named Swanton was building a dream on pilings — a pleasure pier of fresh-cut planks and white paint, a casino strung with a thousand electric bulbs against the blue, a bathhouse, a carousel coming by rail from the East: the whole bright apparatus of a town that had decided to stop making its money from hides and lime and redwood and start making it from joy. They drove the pilings into the exact ground the Méndez cattle had once come down to drink — which is to say they built it, like everything in that town, on land that had been taken — and to build it they hired the sons of the people they had taken it from, and the Italians off the lime kilns, and the Portuguese off the whaleboats, and anyone at all with a strong back and no choice in the matter. Cirilo had driven pilings beside a cheerful, exhausted young Italian named Nunzio Sabatini, who sang while he worked, whose hands were already shiny-burned from a season at Cowell's kilns, and who told Cirilo that in this country a man like them could have anything in the world he wanted, anything, so long as he did not mind being hated for it. Nunzio meant it as a joke. It was the truest thing anyone said to Cirilo that whole year.

By midsummer the pier was open and Cirilo had been promoted, if that was the word, from the pilings up to the planks, and he worked the bright deck of the very thing his own hands had built, among people who would never once wonder whose hands had built it. He was eighteen, and he had long since mastered the one essential skill of his position, which was not to look at the summer people. You learned it the way you learned not to touch the lime vats — once, and never again. The summer people were Anglo and moneyed and down from the City, and they moved through the world Cirilo swept as though it had been arranged for their convenience, which it had; and a brown boy who looked at them too long, or too directly, was asking for a kind of trouble that did not trouble itself to announce its arrival. So he kept his eyes on the work. He was looking, that afternoon, at a pyramid of iron milk-bottles and a worn baseball in his hand, behind a banner that read TEST YOUR ARM — THREE BALLS A NICKEL, when the girl laughed.

It was not the laugh they used on the pier, the bright tin one the young ladies kept in their purses beside their handkerchiefs. It came out of her sideways and surprised, as though he had knocked it loose along with the bottles — which he had, all six of them, blown clean off the shelf by a ball thrown without any windup at all, after the young man beside her in the white flannels had missed three times and then announced, loudly, that the game was rigged and the bottle boy a part of the rig.

He had not meant to throw it. That was the thing he would turn over for years afterward, lying awake: a lifetime of not-looking, of keeping small, of swallowing the Ashbys of the world whole and in silence — and then one young fool in flannels had called him a cheat in front of a girl, and something in Cirilo that he had kept on a short chain his entire life had simply, for the length of one heartbeat, slipped it, and picked up a baseball, and answered. The bottles were still ringing on the planks when he understood what he had done, which was that he had been *seen.*

"Do it again," she said.

"Costs a nickel," said Cirilo, and did not smile, and watched her hold his eyes a beat too long — longer than was safe, longer than was wise — and watched the young man in the flannels watch her do it, and watched the young man begin, in that exact moment, to hate him: with the particular hatred of a man who has just discovered that the thing he believes he owns has a mind of its own, and a preference, and that the preference is not him.

"Then here's a nickel," she said, and held out a coin in a kid-leather glove so soft it might have been sewn by his own father in another life, in another century, when leather still belonged to them. He took it. He gave her a ball. And he understood, with the flat cold clarity of a thing decided somewhere above his head, that the silence he had worn his whole life as the only armor the world could not use against him had just, for the first time, begun to cost him more than it had ever once saved.

### Chapter — The House in the Tannery's Shadow

Otto Kron's tannery sat where the creek met the flats, and you could find it in the dark with your nose: the green reek of the soaking pits, the lime that ate the hair off a hide and the skin off a careless hand, the oak-bark liquor steeping in its vats like the world's worst tea, and under all of it the sweet, rotten, animal smell of the thing itself — the smell of death being argued, slowly, into something a man could wear. Esteban Méndez had walked through its gate six mornings a week for the better part of thirty years, and he no longer smelled it, which is the saddest sentence anyone ever wrote about a man.

Kron himself was a Prussian, square and gray, who had come to California with nothing but a trade, and who had been called a foreigner in this town for forty years and would be called one in his grave. He was hard with his men and fair in the narrow Prussian way that keeps its word and counts every cent of it, and he and Esteban had between them the particular wordless respect of two men who both knew, without ever once saying so, that the work was honest and the world that bought it was not. Years back, when Dolores was sick and there was no money, Kron had paid a doctor and docked it from no wage and never mentioned it, and had been short and unkind to Esteban for a month afterward, as if the kindness embarrassed him — which it did. He grew rich on the cracked hands of men he would never have had to his table. Both of those things were true at once. Most things, in that town, were two things at once.

Esteban had been fast once. People forgot it, because he had spent thirty years making them forget it, but as a boy he had been the quickest thing in the flats, faster than any Anglo kid in the county — and there had been an afternoon, which he did not speak of, ever, not even to his wife, when his speed had drawn a crowd, and the crowd had drawn the wrong kind of attention, and a friend of his, a Black boy named Toussaint who could hit a ball into the next county, had answered for that attention with his life, off a low oak limb, while Esteban watched from the culvert where bigger hands had pushed him and held him down. He was twelve. He had been fast only in his sleep ever since. He had learned, that year, the great terrible lesson he would spend the rest of his life trying to teach his son: that the nail standing up is the one that gets hammered, that a man survives by being too small to aim at, that love — real love — sometimes looks exactly like making a boy small enough to live. He believed it the way you believe a thing that once saved your life. And the horror of him, the whole grey horror of him, was that he was not entirely wrong.

Dolores ruled the two rooms and the lean-to from the stove, and her faith was not the lace-collar faith of the mission's front pews but the iron thing underneath it, the thing that had carried her people across deserts and through fevers and out the far side of grief still somehow cooking supper. She had borne five children and buried two of them — a girl and a boy, a single winter apart, in the bad year — in the Catholic ground above the town, and she climbed up to them on Sundays and told no one, not even Esteban, what she said there. She kept the language alive in her children's mouths the way she kept the fire alive in the stove: by tending it when no one was watching. And when Esteban, frightened by some fresh sign of the world's notice, wanted the rosary taken down from the window where the street could see it, she left it up, every time, and he never quite found the words to make her take it down — because they both knew that the rosary in the window was the last acre of the rancho the family still owned, and she would not sell it.

Of the three sons who lived, Luis was the eldest and had taken the weight: he worked the meat market on Pacific Avenue, married young, counted the family's pennies, and carried his resentment of Cirilo the way a man carries a stone in his boot — quietly, and always, and without ever quite deciding to throw it away, for he loved the boy, and also knew that he, Luis, had folded his own small gift into a butcher's apron so that there would be a roof, and no one had ever asked him to, or thanked him for it. And Abel, the middle one, was the cautionary tale told in the next bed: the best natural athlete of all of them, a wrist like a whip and an eye like a hawk, who had discovered early that a brown boy's gift draws the kind of notice that wounds, and had set about, with a methodical fury, drinking and brawling it into the ground before the world could take it from him on the world's own terms. He would be dead of it before he was forty. In the family's private theology he was the proof of the curse — and also, if you looked at him straight on, simply a frightened man getting out of his own way before anyone else could get there first.

So when the family gathered at night around the single lamp, the whole argument of Cirilo's life was already in the room before anyone spoke a word. His mother at the stove with her two graves and her undefeated God. His father gray with another man's hides and the friend he had watched die. Luis with his stone, Abel with his bottle, the old man in the good chair with his ninety years of evidence. And the boy, eighteen, with boardwalk sawdust on his collar and a girl's laugh still going off inside him like a struck bell, and hands — this was the thing, this was always the thing — hands that did not smell of the pit.

"Kron will take you on in the fall," Esteban said one night, not looking up. He said it the way you offer a man a coat in a storm. "Steady work. A man can feed a family on it."

"I have work, Papá. At the beach."

"You have a season at the beach. Until the summer people go home and the planks belong to the gulls again." And then, lower, the old fear rising under the words like water under a floor: "I want you somewhere they can't put you out on a whim, mijo. Somewhere too small to aim at. That's all I want." And he meant Toussaint, though he would never say the name, and at the stove Dolores went still for a moment, because she could hear the dead boy in her husband's voice as plainly as if he had spoken it.

"You want him where you are," said Don Aurelio from his chair — not unkindly, which made it worse. "Safe. Small. Second."

The table went still. Esteban's jaw worked, and the thing he wanted to say — *I kept you all alive, old man, while you sat in that chair and dreamed about a rancho the lawyers ate* — he did not say, because he was a man who had buried his words a long time ago, beside everything else he had buried.

"Second is alive, viejo," was all he said. "I have buried men who reached." And only Dolores knew how literally he meant it.

The old man did not answer his son; he had learned, across a long life, that you do not win an argument with a frightened man by frightening him further. He looked at the boy instead. "Show me the hand," he said; and when Cirilo crossed and held out his right hand, the old man turned it in the lamplight — big, strong, unmarked by the lime, the only unbroken working hand in four generations of the family — and held it the way you hold a thing found again after you had given it up for lost.

"Your great-grandfather tanned hides with a hand like this and called himself a king," he said. "Your father trims them for a Prussian's wage and calls himself safe — and he is not wrong to, God forgive me; I taught him to be safe, and the teaching kept him breathing, and I have hated myself for it every day for forty years." His thumb moved once over the boy's whole knuckles. "But you. You have the first hands in four generations that don't smell of the pit. Whatever else is true — and much of it is terrible, and I have seen most of it — that is also true. Don't you dare give them back."

And Esteban, who loved his son in the only language the world had ever permitted him to learn, looked at the floor and said nothing, because there is nothing to say to an old man who has decided, against ninety years of evidence and the cooling body of a boy named Toussaint, to hope. But under the table, where no one could see it, his cracked and stained and ruined hand had closed, slowly, into a fist — the old shape, the fast boy's shape, the shape he had forbidden himself for a lifetime — and then opened again, and lay flat against his knee, and was still.

### Chapter — Dolores

The men of this story get the headstones and the box scores and the telegrams; the women get the kitchens — and the kitchens are where it is actually decided whether a family lives. Dolores Méndez kept the kitchen, and so Dolores Méndez kept the family, and almost no one ever noticed, which was exactly how she wanted it and exactly the injustice of it, both at once.

She had come from even less than the Méndez had fallen to: a laborer's daughter out of a Watsonville the history books do not trouble with, a girl who could not read and never learned, and who raised four readers anyway by the simple expedient of deciding that they would read and then making the world rearrange itself around the decision. She had married Esteban at sixteen, when he was already gray inside from the thing he had watched at the culvert, and she had spent forty years learning the exact dimensions of her husband's fear and building a life inside them — a life large enough to raise sons in, in the cramped room a frightened good man's caution allowed. She did it the way water does things: patiently, without announcement, finding the level, wearing the stone.

She had buried two children. A girl, Inés, of the diphtheria, at four; and a boy — little Joaquín, named for the old rancho king who had wept over the grant — of nothing anyone could name, at eleven months, in the same hard winter: two graves in the Catholic ground above the town that she climbed to every Sunday of her life, in every weather, and tended, and spoke to, and told no one what she said. The grief did not break her, because she did not permit grief the room to break things; she gave it its Sunday hour and its tended plot and not one inch more, and the other six days she cooked and washed and taught the language and held the line. People mistook this for hardness. It was the opposite of hardness. It was the most ferocious tenderness the whole story contains — a love so large and so disciplined that it had taught itself to keep functioning while carrying two small coffins, because the living children needed a mother whose hands did not shake; and so her hands did not shake, by an act of will repeated every single morning for forty years.

Her faith was the engine of it, and her faith was not the soft thing the word has since become. It was iron, and old, and entirely without illusion. She did not believe God would protect her children; she had two graves that disproved it, and she was no fool. She believed something harder and more useful: that you tend what is given for exactly as long as it is given, with your whole heart and no guarantees; that the tending is itself the prayer and itself the meaning; and that whether anyone is listening is not your business — your business is the pot, the language, the plot on the hill, and the rosary kept in the window where the street can see that this family has not disappeared and will not. When her son the ballplayer died, three thousand miles away, a champion, of the influenza, she was old, and she received the news the way she received all news: standing at the stove. She finished what she was cooking, because there were still people in the house who had to eat. And then she went up the hill, in the rain, to tell Inés and little Joaquín that their brother was coming home to lie down beside them at last, and that he had done a great thing out in the world, and that she had always known he would, and that she would tend the three of them now together, on her Sundays, for as long as God left her the legs to climb — which He did, a long time; because God, who had taken so much from this woman, seemed to understand that the one thing He could not take, and would not try to, was her refusal to stop tending what was hers.

### Chapter — The Hill

The first thing his mother ever taught Cirilo about being a Méndez, she taught him on the hill, among the dead.

He was six, perhaps seven, the first time Dolores took him up — not into the living town but above it, to the Catholic ground where the graves looked down on the bay, where two small markers stood that he had not, until that morning, known were his to mourn. *This is your sister Inés,* his mother said, in the language she would speak only indoors, and here, in the open among the dead, where the street could not hear and it did not matter. *She was four. The diphtheria took her. And this is your brother Joaquín. He was a baby. He did not stay.* She did not weep — he would learn, growing up, that she had a place for the weeping and it was not here; here was for the tending — and she set the boy to pulling the weeds from around the markers with his small hands, and showed him how, and they worked together in the wind off the water; and she told him, in the old language, the thing that was the whole of her religion and the whole of the family's survival folded into one instruction a child could follow:

*We tend them, mijo, because they were ours, and because tending is how you say a thing was real. The world down there* — she meant the living town below, the Anglo town, the courthouse and the boardwalk and the men who had taken the rancho — *that world wants us to disappear. Not to die, exactly. To disappear. To stop having ever been here. And the only answer there is to that is this* — and she laid his small hand on his dead sister's name — *you tend. You keep the name. You say it out loud, and you teach your children to say it, and you do not let the wind or the weeds or the men down the hill take a single one of us off the face of the earth without a fight. That is what it is to be a Méndez. We are the ones who do not disappear.*

He did not fully understand it at six. He understood it at the booth, and on the mound, and in the clubhouse where they handed him a name that was not his; he understood it best of all, perhaps, in a Brooklyn flat at the very end, folding his father's glove into his son's hands — that everything he had ever done, all the reaching and the climbing and the refusing to be made small, had been only the boy on the hill, grown large, pulling the weeds from around the family name so the world could not take it off the earth; that his whole life had been his mother's lesson, played on a bigger hill, before a bigger crowd, in the same wind. And when they brought him home at the end and laid him down, it was on that hill, beside Inés and the first little Joaquín, where his mother could tend all three of her reachers together — for she had decided, standing there, that the ballplayer had been a reacher too, the biggest of them, and that reaching was only another word for refusing to disappear, and that she would pull the weeds from around his name on her Sundays for as long as God left her the legs to climb. Which He did, a long time; because by then He surely understood that this was a woman who would have climbed it anyway.

### Chapter — The First Time He Threw

He was ten the first time anyone understood what was in his arm, and it frightened them, which was the correct response, and which he did not understand until much later.

It was a rock, not a ball — the family could not always spare a ball — a smooth river stone; and there was a tin can on a fence post a long way off, the kind of distance grown men missed, and Cirilo, ten years old and bored, picked up the stone and threw it without thinking and knocked the can spinning off the post on the first try, and then did it again, and again, until his brothers stopped their own game to watch. And Abel — Abel, the gifted one, the one who was *given,* who at twelve could already do things with a ball that drew crowds — Abel watched his little brother throw, and did not say *good arm,* and did not laugh, and did not challenge him to do it again. Abel went quiet: the particular quiet of a boy recognizing, in someone he loves, the thing that is going to hurt them — because Abel already knew, at twelve, having learned it the hard way, what a gift like that drew down on a brown boy in that county.

But it was the old man's face Cirilo remembered. Don Aurelio had been sitting in the shade, ancient even then, and had watched the whole thing — the stone, the can, the impossible accuracy of it, the casual murderous ease — and the boy looked over at his abuelo expecting the thing a boy expects, the pride, the smile, the *that's my grandson;* and instead found the old man regarding him with an expression he had no name for at ten and understood completely at thirty-six on a mound in Boston: fear and love and grief, all at once — the look of a man who has just watched his grandson hand him a thing that is both the family's only hope and its oldest danger, a man doing sums in his head that come out, every way he adds them, to a cost. *Put it down, mijo,* the old man said, very quietly, not unkindly. And then, contradicting himself, contradicting ninety years of his own hard-learned caution, in the same breath, because he could not help it, because hope is the thing in us that does not listen to evidence: *No. No — pick it up. Pick it up and throw it again. Let me see it once more, before I tell you to be careful with it the rest of your life.* And the boy threw the stone again and knocked the can spinning, and the old man closed his eyes — the first time, the first of many, the same closed eyes that would decline to watch a championship thirty years on — and Cirilo, ten years old, did not understand why his abuelo, having just seen him do a wonderful thing, looked for all the world like a man at a graveside. He understood it later. He understood it, in the end, completely: the gift and the danger were the same gift; they always had been; the old man had seen it in one throw of a river stone and had been right — and had told him to pick it up again anyway, because that too, the telling-him-to-pick-it-up-anyway knowing the cost, was love. Was, in fact, the whole of what the family had instead of safety. Was the only inheritance they had left to give.

### Chapter — The Dark Under the Pier

They could only have each other in the dark, and so the dark became theirs.

When the boardwalk lights went out and the surf came up to fill the silence, they met under the pilings, in the black wedge of shadow beneath the pier — the one place in that whole bright town where a brown boy and a white girl could stand together without it costing blood. Her father believed she was asleep in the rented cottage up on the bluff. His father believed he was a fool, and was right, and was also afraid, which Cirilo did not yet understand were the same thing in his father. They were deceiving everyone who loved them, those nights, and neither of them could make themselves care — and that frightened Rebecca more than the dark did, because she had been raised to believe she was a careful person, and here was proof, nightly, that she was not.

She had come to him, in the beginning, out of something she was honest enough to mistrust in herself. She was motherless — her mother had died when Rebecca was eight, of the same careless fever that took the poor, except that it was not supposed to take people who could afford doctors, and had taken her anyway — and her father had answered the loss by converting his daughter, across a decade, into an ornament: a beautiful, accomplished, marriageable thing to be set out at the right tables and married, in the fullness of time, to the right name. She had spent eleven years being looked *at* — at her dress, her posture, her prospects — and never once, not by her father nor by Chuck Ashby nor by any of the right names at any of the right tables, looked *into.* And then a bottle boy on a pier had knocked six iron bottles off a shelf and turned and *seen* her — not the dress, not the prospects, but the person rattling around loose inside all of it — and the thing it broke open in her was so large and so sudden that she mistook it, at first, for simple rebellion, for the satisfying scandal of wanting the one boy on the beach she had been forbidden. It was only later, much later, in cold rooms three thousand miles to the east, that she would be certain it had been love. On the nights under the pier she was not yet certain, and she knew she was not certain, and she came anyway — which is either the bravest thing a careful person can do or the most reckless, and is generally both at once.

"Tell me something true," she said once. "You never do."

He let the sand run through his fingers a while before he answered. "My family owned this," he said at last. "Not the pier — the land. All of it, the river to the redwoods, before there was a Santa Cruz to own it. Now I sell nickels on a plank built on top of it." He looked at her in the dark. "And I would rather be here, like this, with you, than have had a single acre of it back."

She took his hand — the pitching hand, the unmarked one — and held it like a thing she had decided to keep, and told him they would simply have to take the rest of it back themselves; and just then a whistle came down off the boardwalk and a watchman's lamp swung out over the rail. They froze together in the black while the beam swept the sand above their heads. It was only old Pereira, the night man — a Portuguese widower who had buried a wife and three of his four children, and who walked the empty pier until dawn because the rooms he went home to were louder, in their silence, than the whole Pacific. He did not want to find anyone. He swept the lamp along out of habit and not out of zeal, and the beam passed over the dark wedge where they stood pressed together and breathing, and moved on, and Pereira shuffled away up the planks humming something low in a language neither of them knew — a man wholly uninterested in ruining two strangers, because the world had done enough ruining of its own to last him the rest of his life.

They breathed again. But the fear was its own answer, the thing neither of them ever said aloud: that all of it — the dark, the nights, the held hand — was borrowed, and the lenders were cruel and patient and kept good books, and that somewhere a reckoning was being entered, in a ledger they would not be allowed to read until the day it was presented, in full, for payment.

### Chapter — What It Costs

The reckoning came, the first installment of it, on the bright planks in the full light of a Tuesday afternoon.

Cirilo was hauling crates when Chuck Ashby and two of his college friends stepped into his path — easy, grinning, hands in their pockets, with the loose insolence of men who have never once in their lives been outnumbered and so do not recognize that this, three on one, is precisely what outnumbering looks like from the other side. They had been talking about him, Chuck said. About how he'd been *seen.* At night. With a young lady of their mutual acquaintance.

"I don't know what you mean," Cirilo said, and set the crate down, slow, and kept his eyes at the level of the man's collar, which was the level the world permitted.

"You know exactly what I mean." And then Chuck Ashby, who was not, whatever else he was, a stupid man, told him how it went — pleasantly, conversationally, the way one man tells another the weather. Out here, for a fellow like Cirilo, with a girl like that, there was no court and no judge and no arm fast enough in the world. There was a rope, and a tree, and a crowd that went home afterward and slept the sleep of the just. He said it without heat, and that was the worst of it, because heat would have meant it was personal, and the absence of heat meant it was simply *true,* a fact of the country like the tide. He stepped in close. "Throw all the strikes you want, Mendez. You can't strike out a county." And he reached up and patted Cirilo's cheek — once, lightly, an insult measured with a jeweler's precision to be too small to answer and too large, ever, to forget — and turned and strolled off up the planks, laughing at something one of his friends had said, already, in his own mind, finished with the whole matter.

But he was not finished with it, and that was the thing Cirilo could not have known, standing there with his hands shaking. For Chuck had felt, in the half-second his palm rested on the other man's face, the muscle jump in Cirilo's jaw — the held thing, the leashed thing, the enormous and disciplined refusal — and somewhere under the easy cruelty a small cold voice had told Chuck the truth he would spend twenty years declining to hear: that the bottle boy had not answered the insult not because he *could* not but because he had *decided* not to; that the power in the transaction was not, in fact, entirely Chuck's; and that a man who can absorb a slap like that and merely stand there, deciding, is a far more frightening thing than a man who swings. Chuck walked away laughing to cover the fact that, for one instant, on a sunny pier, with two friends at his back and a whole county at his command, he had been afraid.

Cirilo stood very still. Then he picked the crate back up. His hands — the good hands, the unmarked ones, the first in four generations to smell of something other than the lime — were shaking, and not from the weight. He carried the crate into the cool dark of the storeroom and set it down and stood there a while; and he thought of his father, who came home gray every night and wanted only that his son be too small to aim at; and he understood at last, all the way down, the thing the old men had been trying to tell him in two different voices his whole life. The family was not merely cursed to second. It was that *reaching for first* — out loud, in the daylight, where they could see you do it — was a thing this country had ways of charging a man for that had nothing whatever to do with baseball. His father had paid that bill once, in a culvert, at twelve years old. Cirilo stood in the dark and felt the old fear settle over him like his father's own coat, two sizes too big and somehow still warm — and felt, underneath the fear and refusing it, the thing he had thrown that day without meaning to: the arm, the answer, the chain slipping its post. Both things lived in him at once. They would go on living in him at once for the rest of his life.

### Chapter — The Exhibition

He had only one answer to all of it, and one bright Sunday in the new century he gave it in front of the whole town.

It was a challenge game, got up the way such games were — the local nine against a touring club of college boys down from the City for the weekend, Stanford men in crisp flannels with Chuck Ashby among them, loose and certain and already, in his own mind, the hero of the afternoon. A holiday crowd came out to the ground behind the Leibbrandt baths: parasols and straw boaters, the smell of frying smelt and spilled beer, the boardwalk organ carrying its waltzes over the outfield fence. Down the third-base line the people from the flats had gathered in their own loose knot — the Italians off the kilns, the Portuguese off the boats, the Mexican families who spoke the language only at home — and among them Nunzio Sabatini, who had taken an afternoon off he could not afford; and when Cirilo walked out to take the ball for the locals and the whole college bench began to laugh at the bottle boy from the pier come out in spikes, Nunzio rose and bellowed something joyful and obscene in Italian that made the women swat at him and the men grin.

Then Cirilo threw, and the laughing stopped.

The first college man swung at a fastball that was already in the catcher's mitt. The second got the wet one — the spitball, the dying ball, the pitch his abuelo had pressed on him with a warning folded inside it — and it dropped off the table a foot in front of the plate and left him lunging at air, foolish, in front of his girl and his friends and the whole bright grandstand. And the third was Chuck Ashby, who was not laughing now, who dug in with his jaw set and fouled one back and flailed at two more and finally stalked off to the bench telling anyone who would listen that the ball was doctored — which it was, and which the rules of that year entirely permitted, and which gave him back not one shred of the thing he had just lost in front of the only people he had ever been taught to count. Down the third-base line the flats came apart with joy. Nunzio was weeping with laughter. It was, Cirilo would think much later, the first wholly happy hour of his life, and it was happy precisely because it was *witnessed* — because for one afternoon the thing he could do was larger than the thing he was, and the whole town had to sit in the sun and watch it be larger.

In the little grandstand, a spare man in a dark Jesuit coat had stopped writing in his book.

His name was Aloysius Fagan, and he had come to Santa Clara by way of a hard Limerick boyhood and a harder novitiate, and he carried inside his cassock two appetites he had never managed to reconcile and had stopped, by middle age, expecting to: a genuine and burning love of God and of the boys in his charge, and a frank, competitive, nearly worldly hunger to beat Stanford at baseball every single spring — which he confessed as a sin twice a year and committed the rest of the time with his whole heart. He had seen a thousand arms. He had not seen this one. He watched Cirilo work the way another man might watch a sunrise, and he was honest enough even in the watching to admit he did not entirely know which of his two appetites had stood him out of his seat — and Jesuit enough to suspect it did not finally matter, that God was perfectly able to work His purposes through a baseball coach's vanity, having so little else, in this fallen country, to work with.

After the last out he came down to the fence.

"Son," he said. "Have you given any thought to college?"

"We can't pay for college, Father," Cirilo said.

"No," said Fagan, with a small dry smile that had Limerick still in it. "But Santa Clara can pay for an arm. We play Stanford every spring." He let his eyes drift, just once, to Chuck, sulking white-flanneled beside the charabanc that had carried the college boys down. "Wouldn't you like to do that again — where it counts?"

And the door of Cirilo Méndez's whole life swung open on a single sentence; and on the far side of it, only an hour up the line, was every last thing he had been told, all his life, in two languages and by people who loved him, that he was not permitted to want.

### Chapter — The Leaving

He left from the little depot in his one good suit — Dolores had turned the collar and pressed it twice the night before, saying nothing, which was how she said the most — with a college letter buttoned into his breast pocket and a valise that did not need to be large.

The family came to the end of the lane but not onto the platform, because the platform belonged to the other Santa Cruz, the one with the money and the timetables, and they had learned where their welcome ended as surely as they had learned where every other line was drawn. Luis shook his hand hard, said something about writing, and looked away — his resentment and his love so thoroughly braided by now that even he could no longer have told you which was which. Abel, already a little drunk at nine in the morning, hugged him too long and whispered, with the terrible clear sight of the ruined, *don't come back like me* — and then laughed it off and was gone up the lane before Cirilo could answer. Dolores took his face in both hands and looked at him a long while and gave him the language one last time, low and fast, a blessing older than the mission, and let him go. And Esteban stood a little apart, gray already though the day was young, his ruined hands working at the brim of his hat; and when his son came to him he could not, at first, find anything to say at all, because everything he wanted to say was *don't,* and he had decided in the night, watching Dolores press the collar, that he would not say it. So instead he said, roughly, "They will try to make you small up there. In a different way than here. A politer way." And then, against his whole life — against the culvert and the oak limb and forty years of being careful — he reached out and folded his son's pitching hand into a fist inside his own two stained ones, the old man's gesture, the gesture he had watched his own father make and had sworn in his fear never to make himself; and he held it a moment, and let it go, and turned and walked up the lane after his sons without looking back, because if he looked back his son would see his face.

Rebecca came to the platform itself, against every rule, to the very edge of it and no farther, because she could not afford to be seen, and they both knew it, and the knowing was part of the goodbye. She told him Santa Clara was only an hour, a morning's train — as though either of them believed for a single second that distance was the thing that kept them apart.

"It's a hundred years," he said, "and we both know it. Your people, my people —"

"Then go," she said, fierce and low, the tears standing in her eyes and refusing, by force of will, to fall where the platform could see them. "Go and become a man so loud they cannot pretend not to hear your name. And come back for me when you have."

She pressed something into his hand: the kid-leather glove, the soft one, the one she had worn the afternoon at the booth. "So you remember," she said, "whose hand you're playing for." The train called down the line. He closed his fist around the leather — her glove, made of the one material his family had ever truly owned, tanned God knew where by God knew whose stolen hands — and he stepped up into the car, and he did not look back, because he had learned that very morning from his father, without either of them ever saying it, that there are partings a man can only survive by refusing to witness them.

Through the window the platform slid away. Rebecca stood in the wrong place a moment too long — a beat past safety, a beat that was its own small act of defiance — and then made herself turn, and walk, alone, into a life that had just, in the space of one train whistle, quietly become a waiting. And the boy on the train rode north toward the colleges and the cathedrals and the sons of the men who had taken everything, with a white girl's glove shut in one fist and his abuelo's voice in one ear and his father's two warnings in the other, and a silence in him that had finally, after eighteen years, hardened all the way down into a purpose: he would climb, and he would not stop climbing, and he would make them say the name. Whatever it cost — and it would cost him, in the end, everything, though he could not yet know that, and would not have stepped off the train if he had — he would not, ever, for any price they named, agree to be second to the men who had decided he had been born it.

## _Siempre Segundo_ — Act II, novelized (full chapters)

### Chapter — The Threshold

He went up to the College of Santa Clara on the strength of one sentence from a man in a black coat, and he spent his first month there learning that a door opening is not at all the same thing as being welcome on the far side of it.

The college was Jesuit and old — older than the state, older than the conquest, the oldest school in California — and it was full, that autumn, of the sons of the men who had taken his family's land. Some of them he could even have named, had he wished to: a Bray, descended from the Boston captain who had danced the jota at the rancho and then filed on it; a boy whose grandfather had bought a Méndez quarter-league off the lawyer Quint for the price of a good horse. They were courteous to him in the particular way the secure are courteous to the useful — he was the arm that beat Stanford every spring, and so they let him sit at the edge of the long table, and did not, on the whole, let him forget that it was the edge. He wore his one good suit until it shone at the elbows. He learned which fountains and which staircases and which pews were, by an arithmetic no one had ever written down but every soul in the place could perform without error, *his.* He learned to construe Cicero and to serve at Mass and to hold his knife the way the others held theirs, and he learned all of it the way he had learned everything — in silence, watching, giving nothing back that could be used.

Father Fagan watched over him, in his fashion, which was a complicated fashion. The Jesuit was proud of him the way a man is proud of a fine tool he has found and sharpened, and fond of him the way a man grows fond of anything he has bet on; and there were nights, walking the cloister, when Fagan examined his conscience on the matter and did not love what he found there — that he had brought a brown boy into a house that would use him for nine innings every spring and never once truly seat him at its table, and that he, Fagan, was the man holding the door. He took the boy's confession on Saturdays. He never quite found the courage to make his own to the boy.

Cirilo did not mind the edge of the table, or told himself he did not, which at nineteen is very nearly the same thing. He had not come for the welcome. He had come because Rebecca Whitfield was an hour up the line at Stanford, close enough that on clear nights he fancied he could feel her there like heat off a wall; and because his abuelo had folded his fingers into a fist on a tannery floor and forbidden him to give the hands back; and because somewhere under the silence he had worn like a second suit his whole life, a thing had finally hardened all the way into a plan, cold and clear and patient as a winter creek. He would make himself so good that they would have no choice but to say his name. And then he would go and get her. He confessed the pride of it to Father Fagan twice, and was absolved twice, and kept it anyway — because some sins a man is not actually sorry for, and both he and the old priest knew it, and that shared knowledge was the nearest thing to friendship the house allowed them.

### Chapter — The Examination

There was a professor at Santa Clara, a layman named Pruett who taught rhetoric and resented having to teach it to the likes of Cirilo, and who found, in the oral examinations, his chance.

The orals were public — that was the cruelty built into them — conducted before the assembled class, a boy standing alone at the front construing Latin and defending propositions while his fellows watched and the professor probed. For most of the gentlemen's sons it was a formality, a genial hazing they had been bred for since the cradle, the Latin half-learned at good schools and the confidence wholly inherited. Pruett ran Cirilo's examination differently. He ran it long, and he ran it hard, doubling back, setting traps in the ablative, putting to the scholarship Mexican the questions he put to no one else, with a thin courteous smile that invited the room to enjoy what was coming — the bottle boy exposed at last as the fraud a man like Pruett needed him to be, because if the tannery's son could construe Cicero as well as the banker's son, then something Pruett had built his whole small life upon was not true, and Pruett would sooner break a boy in front of a class than let that be so.

Cirilo construed the Cicero. He had spent four hundred nights on it, in the silence, by a borrowed lamp, the way he had spent four hundred afternoons on the spitball — because he had understood from the first week that for him there would be no formality, no genial pass, no inherited confidence to coast upon; that he would be examined harder than any gentleman in the room and would therefore have to be *better,* simply to be permitted to be equal. And so he was better. He answered the traps. He found the verb. And when Pruett, furious now beneath the courtesy, abandoned rhetoric altogether for open baiting — asking the boy whether he did not think his talents might be better spent in *manual* work, at which his people had shown such aptitude — the room went still; and Cirilo, who had taken the slap on the pier and not swung, looked at the professor a long moment and answered, in Latin, quietly, with a line of the very Cicero they had been construing: the observation that it is the mark of a small mind to mistake the accident of birth for the measure of a man. Which was not, in fact, anywhere in the passage. Which Cirilo had composed on the spot, in correct Ciceronian Latin, and attributed to Cicero — gambling everything on the wager that Pruett, for all his airs, did not know the text half so well as the boy who had spent four hundred nights inside it. And Pruett, caught, uncertain, not daring to challenge a citation he could not place, went red, and nodded stiffly, and ended the examination; and the gentlemen's sons, who were not bad fellows and who loved a winner the way the young always do, broke into a startled applause the professor could not stop. Father Fagan heard of it within the hour. He laughed until he had to sit down — and then went to the chapel, troubled, and confessed that he had laughed, and admitted to God that he was not, in truth, entirely sorry, which was the same thing he was forever having to confess about that boy, and the same thing God, he suspected, was forever declining to mind.

### Chapter — An Hour Up the Line

Stanford was an hour up the line, and for three years Cirilo Méndez crossed that hour the way other men cross themselves — furtively, devoutly, and at the peril of his soul as the world around him reckoned souls.

They had worked out a geography of the forbidden. There was a grove of live oaks on the back edge of the Stanford lands, out past where the gentlemen rode, where a Santa Clara boy who came up on the milk train and walked the last two miles in the dark could meet a Stanford girl who had told her housemother she was at her devotions — and in a sense was. They had perhaps two hours, once a fortnight, when his games and her chaperones and the train schedule and the moon all conspired to allow it, and they spent those two hours talking, mostly — which is the thing no one believes about the young and the forbidden: that what they were starved for, even more than the other, was simply to be *known* by one other person in a world that had agreed to know them only as types.

She told him about her mother, the careless fever, the long conversion into an ornament. He told her — slowly, across many fortnights, because he had never told a living soul — about the rancho, and the courthouse, and his father's silence, and the boy named Toussaint his father had watched die from a culvert, which he had pieced together over the years out of things half-said and never finished. He told her things in that grove he would not tell her again until they had been married a decade, and some he never told her again at all. And Rebecca, who had been raised among people who used candor as a currency and conversation as a kind of fencing, discovered in the oak dark the disorienting experience of being told the truth by someone who wanted nothing from the telling except to set it down, once, in front of a person who would not use it. It was, she understood much later, the actual beginning of the marriage — not the wedding, years off, in the half-empty church, but those stolen fortnights in the trees, where two people each profoundly alone in their separate prisons taught each other the slow foreign language of not being.

They were nearly caught twice. Once a pair of riders passed close enough that Cirilo pressed her back against an oak with his hand over her own mouth and his heart going like a fist on a door, and they stood like that, breathing each other's breath, until the hooves faded — and afterward neither could speak, because they had both felt the same thing in the same instant, which was not fear exactly but the cold clear arithmetic of what discovery would cost. And it was not the same arithmetic for the two of them, and they both knew it. If they were caught, Rebecca faced disgrace, a ruined season, a swift correcting marriage to someone like Chuck. Cirilo faced a rope. They never once spoke of the difference. It sat between them in the dark like a third person, and it was, in the end, the truest measure both of what she was risking and of what he was — and of the fact that she climbed up to the grove anyway, every fortnight, and so did he.

### Chapter — The Rematch

It came, the way these things do, on an ordinary afternoon the body remembers forever.

Stanford came down to Santa Clara in the spring, loud and cardinal-red and certain, and in the grandstand among the red sat a woman Cirilo had not seen in two years and had not stopped seeing for a single day. She had not expected to know anyone on the field. She was wrong.

At the plate, when his turn came, was Chuck Ashby, an S on his chest, tapping the corners as though he had bought them. "The bottle boy," he said down the line, and to his bench, loud enough for the red seats: "Three balls a nickel, fellas!" And the Stanford side laughed, the easy laugh of people who have never once had to wonder whether the laughter was about them.

Cirilo looked in. And there, half-risen from her seat in the sea of red, a kid-leather glove forgotten in her lap, was Rebecca. Their eyes met across two years. He set, and the first pitch was past Ashby before the laugh had finished. Strike one. The second buckled his knees, a wet ball that died in the air. Strike two, and the Stanford bench went quiet. He held the ball a moment — the stitched leather, an old friend — looked once more at the woman in the wrong-colored seats, and threw the best thing he owned.

Strike three. Ashby swung at air and nearly screwed himself into the dirt.

The Santa Clara side came up roaring, but Cirilo did not hear it, because Rebecca was on her feet — alone, in a sea of cardinal red, clapping for the wrong team and the wrong boy in front of every person she had been raised among, and not caring, which was the whole of her in a single gesture. *Costs a nickel,* he said to no one, under his breath. And up in the stands a woman who had everything to lose looked down at a man who had nothing, and chose, in public, out loud, and for good.

### Chapter — The Break

Her father gave her a choice the way men like her father give choices, which is to say he did not give her one at all.

It was a drawing room on Nob Hill, gaslight on good furniture, and Horace Whitfield stood at the mantel and addressed his daughter as though she were a board that had voted unwisely. A summer's foolishness, he said. A beach attendant. He had let it run because these things burn out. It had not burned out, and so now he would be plain: she would not see the Mexican again. Charley — Chuck, waiting by the window with the wounded patience of a man who believes he is owed something — had been a gentleman about all of it. More than she deserved.

"He throws a baseball, Rebecca," Chuck said softly, the knife slid in gently. "That's the whole of him. A strong arm and a borrowed suit."

"And what's the whole of you, Chuck?" she said, almost kindly. "Your father's name, your father's money, your father's chair at the club." A beat. "At least his arm is his."

Her father read her the terms like a sentence: walk out the door to the Mexican and she walked out of the family. No allowance, no name, no coming back when the romance of being poor wore thin — and it would wear thin, faster than she could dream. Rebecca looked around the warm, lit, comfortable cage of the room she had grown up in, and understood that she was being offered everything, and that everything was less than a man with one borrowed suit had already given her on a pier when he looked at her like a person and not an arrangement.

What she could not see, in her fury — and would understand only years later, when her own son was born into nothing — was that her father was not, in that moment, cruel so much as *terrified.* Horace Whitfield was not old money. He had come to San Francisco at seventeen with a dead father's debts and a cardboard valise, and clawed his way up through ship chandlery and railroad freight and a great deal he never afterward discussed, and the respectability he had bought with thirty years of that clawing was, to him, not a luxury but a seawall — the one thin thing standing between his family and the roaring cold water he had only just escaped. His daughter marrying a tannery hand, a Mexican, a *nobody,* was not breaking his heart; it was breaching the wall, and he could feel the water coming through it already. He was a frightened man bailing, and frightened men bailing will throw anything at all over the side, even a daughter, to keep the boat one more hour above the line. He never once put it to himself that way. He told himself, that night and for the rest of his life, that he was protecting her. The most dangerous men in this whole book are, nearly every one of them, protecting someone.

She walked to the door, and turned back once, to Chuck: "Find someone who wants the chair at the club. They're easy to find. They're the only people you've ever met." And she went.

She paid for it in full and on time. The allowance stopped. The name closed over her like water. The friends she had grown up beside learned, all at once, not to see her on the street. And the heiress who had never made a bed or counted a dollar twice went to live in the margins of a country that had decided she no longer existed — and discovered, to the genuine astonishment of everyone who had warned her, that she had never once been happy until she did.

### Chapter — The Bottom of the Ladder

To get to the top you start at the bottom, and the bottom of professional baseball in those years was a circuit of valley towns baking under a white sky. Cirilo Méndez went out into it the only way a brown kid could: at the very floor, a rung at a time, and never one rung faster than the country could stand him.

He learned the trade by failing at it in front of nobody. Class D, and then C — cinder infields that took the skin off a slide, wooden grandstands leaning in the heat, a few dollars a month, four men to a hotel bed, the milk-run trains that stopped at every water tank between Stockton and nowhere. He learned the smell of that life so deeply he would know it in his sleep for the rest of it: wintergreen liniment and cut alfalfa, coal smoke and tobacco spit and the wet-dog reek of flannels that never fully dried. And he learned the first hard law of the business — that he did not own his own arm. Under the reserve clause the club held his contract for as long as it pleased; it could sell him, bury him, or cut him loose into nothing. *You are leather,* the old man told him, not unkindly. *They will buy you and sell you and never once ask.*

His first battery-mate in the Class D bushes was a ruin of a man named Deacon Mulqueen, a catcher gone to seed at thirty-eight, with a gut and a thirst and a left hand missing the top of one finger to a foul tip in a town whose name he could no longer reliably produce. Mulqueen had had three weeks in the big leagues in 1894 — three weeks, eleven at-bats, one ringing double off a pitcher whose name he could recite in his sleep though he had long since forgotten his own mother's birthday — and he had spent the fourteen years since drinking those three weeks slowly, sip by sip, the way a miser spends a fortune he knows he will never replace. He taught Cirilo everything the abuelo's catechism had left out: which boardinghouses would take a brown ballplayer and which would set the dog on him; how to sleep sitting up on a night train and step off it able to throw; how to ice an arm with creek water and a flour sack; how to eat when the meal money ran dry on a Tuesday and payday was Friday. He was self-pitying and unreliable and frequently drunk and he wept easily, and he was, in that first hard year, the truest friend Cirilo had — and when he died, in a charity ward in Sacramento, of the thing that kills men who have decided to die, Cirilo was the only ballplayer who came, and he came a hundred miles to do it, and he understood, standing at the cheap grave, that the game he loved was also a machine for producing exactly this, and that loving it did not require him to pretend otherwise.

When the climb let him home, it was to the Beachcombers, his county's real pro club, playing the ground behind the Leibbrandt bath houses in Beach Flats, so close to the sand that the brine and the boardwalk barkers came over the fence with the gulls. His brothers Luis and Abel played beside him. And on the bench, in street clothes, sat Edward Purse — good enough to start for any nine in the county, kept off the grass and called the team's *mascot* because the color line would not let a Black man play, his heart breaking by inches in plain view every Sunday. Cirilo watched Purse and understood the thing no one ever said to his face: that there were lines on this country no glove could carry a man across, and that he himself stood with one foot on one of them, let onto the field only so long as the papers could keep calling him Spanish.

There was a Sunday — Cirilo carried it the rest of his life — when he had thrown a two-hit shutout and the flats crowd was chanting his name, and he came off the grass high on it and found Purse at the end of the bench not looking at him, and said, foolishly, something about how Purse would have caught that last man easy. And Purse turned and looked at him with a face like a shut door and said, very quietly, *Don't.* Just that. *Don't you stand there in your spikes — that they let you wear — and hand me your pity in my street clothes.* And then, a moment later, because he was a generous man and not a small one, and because the look on Cirilo's face was the look of a boy shown a wall he had not known he was standing on: *It's not your doing, kid. You didn't draw the line. But don't ask me to be glad about exactly where they drew it.* They were friends after that — real friends — but it was a friendship with a wall down the middle of it that neither of them had built and neither could take down, and the honesty of leaving the wall standing, of neither pretending it away nor blaming the other for it, was the truest thing in it.

And through all of it, behind every backstop, on an overturned bucket, sat Don Aurelio, who had watched the game in California since men played it bare-handed on Gold Rush sandlots, and who doled it out like a second catechism. *Read the feet before the swing, mijo. The deep stander is buying time; bust him up and in where his fear can't reach. Watch the hands on the bat — white knuckles is a frightened man, give him the slow one and let his own greed swing for him. Never show his eyes the same height twice.* And this — he would spit in his palm and work the ball — *this, while they still let you. It drops off the table like it died in the air, and they'll outlaw it in a few years, so learn it now and break their hearts with it while you can. Everything they give us has a clock on it, mijo. Throw fast.*

### Chapter — The Road

There was a town in the valley — Cirilo never afterward said which, because by the end there had been too many to keep straight, and because it was every town — where the only café open after the night game would not serve him.

It was past eleven, and the club had come in dead on its feet off a doubleheader, and the café by the depot had a lamp lit and a man behind the counter; and the ballplayers filed in out of a heat that did not break even at night that time of year in that country, and the counterman looked at Cirilo once — the way they looked, the quick flat appraisal that took in the brown and rendered its verdict before the eyes had finished moving — and said, not unkindly, the way they said it when they meant to be reasonable about it, that he could not serve the Mexican, but the rest of the boys were welcome. And the boys, who were nineteen and twenty and starving and not bad fellows, did the thing that nineteen-and-twenty-year-old starving not-bad fellows do, which is the worst thing, which is nothing: they shuffled, and studied their feet, and one or two of them muttered, and not one of them walked out — because they were hungry, and a man has to eat, and it was not, after all, *their* fight, and tomorrow there was another game.

Deacon Mulqueen was forty and fat and finished and three weeks of glory behind him forever, and he stood in the doorway of that café and looked at the counterman, and then at the boys finding their seats, and then at Cirilo standing alone in the heat outside the screen door, and he said, in the carrying voice of a man who had spent his life behind a plate making himself heard over a crowd: *Well, then. I guess none of us are hungry.* And he did not move, and he did not stop looking at the boys, one by one, until — shamed, sullen, grumbling, nineteen years old and learning something they had not come in to learn — they got back up, every one of them, and filed back out into the night; and the whole club walked a mile and a half to the only other place with a light on, a Chinese place by the tracks run by a man named Lim who had his own long acquaintance with the quick flat verdict, and who fed all of them, the brown one included, without a word, at a back table, for cash, at midnight. Mulqueen got drunk that night, drunker than usual, and wept, as he often did, and said something maudlin and self-pitying that Cirilo did not trouble to remember. But he remembered the doorway. He remembered the carrying voice. *I guess none of us are hungry.* It was the first time in his life, outside his own family, that anyone had stood in a doorway for him — and it had been a ruined drunk with a missing fingertip and nothing left to lose by it, who did it anyway. And Cirilo understood, walking the mile and a half to Lim's through the valley dark, that this too was the game: not the diamond, but this — the long road and the closed doors and the rare, unlooked-for, unreasonable man who stands in the doorway — and that he would spend the rest of his life trying to be, for someone, the thing Deacon Mulqueen had been for him that night, and would not always manage it, and would never once stop trying.

### Chapter — Nunzio

Nunzio Sabatini drove pilings beside Cirilo the summer the boardwalk went up, and sang while he did it, and told Cirilo the truest thing anyone told him that year — that a man like them could have anything in this country so long as he did not mind being hated for it — and then Nunzio went on and lived the whole of that sentence, cheerfully, to the end.

He never got out the way Cirilo nearly did. There was no arm in Nunzio, no gift the country would pay to watch; there was only a strong back and a good voice and an enormous uncomplicated heart, and he spent all of it where men like him spent themselves — at Cowell's lime kilns until the heat and the dust finished his lungs for finer work, then on the Monterey wharf hauling sardines, then, when his back went, at a fruit stand he and his wife ran on a corner of the flats, selling other men's harvests at a markup of pennies. He married a girl named Concetta off the same hill his people came from, and filled a small house with children — seven who lived, two who did not, the ordinary arithmetic of the poor in those years — and he was, by every measure the world keeps, a failure and a nobody, a wop who burned his lungs out for wages and died owing the grocer; and he was, by every measure that is real, one of the happiest and most successful men in this entire book, because he had wanted exactly what he had, and loved it without reservation, and never once mistaken the country's contempt for the truth about himself.

He followed Cirilo's whole career like a man following a saint. He could not always afford the trolley to the games and went anyway; he could not read the Eastern box scores and had his eldest read them to him at the fruit stand, and wept at the good ones, openly, among the oranges — an old man crying over a line of agate type about a friend three thousand miles away. When the word came in 1918 — first the championship, the whole flats lit up with it, *one of ours, one of ours made it all the way to the top* — and then, two weeks later, the other word, the influenza — Nunzio Sabatini closed the fruit stand for the day, the only day he ever closed it that was not a feast of the Church, and sat in the back among the crates and wept again, not openly this time, and said to Concetta the thing that was the whole eulogy Cirilo Méndez ever needed and never got from anyone the papers would print: *He did it, Cetta. They said we couldn't, and he did it. They'll forget. They forget everything that isn't theirs. But we won't. You hear me? We don't forget our own. As long as there's one of us left on this coast who remembers, that boy is not gone.* And he was right; and they did not forget; and that — the fruit-stand grief of a man who could not read the box score he wept over — was a truer monument than anything they carved, and it cost nothing, and it lasted, in the flats, in the mouths of Nunzio Sabatini's seven children and their children, longer than the bronze the rich men buy.

### Chapter — A Sunday at Beach Flats

There is no understanding the man Cirilo became without a Sunday at Beach Flats, because it was there, on a diamond behind the bathhouses with the surf for an outfield wall, that he learned what the game could be when it was allowed to be only itself.

The Beachcombers played their home dates on a scrappy field the Daubenbiss brothers — Ed and Charley, of the old Soquel pioneer stock, who ran the club on civic pride and a shoestring — had talked the town into letting them keep, hard by the Leibbrandt baths, so close to the water that a long foul into right rolled down onto the sand among the bathers, and a boy in a striped costume would fetch it while the game waited, good-natured, for him to. Bill Devereaux managed — "Red Dog," a barrel of an Oakland man who chewed and hollered and loved his ballplayers like a hard father — and on a good Sunday a thousand people came, which in that town was a multitude: the merchants in their straw boaters; the young ladies in their summer best, the romances of the season conducted in the stands as openly as the game on the field; the flats families down the right-field line; the whole loud democratic carnival of an American town at play. The boardwalk organ came over the fence. Somebody always sold tamales. And when the Beachcombers beat the City club a local rhymester would have a verse in the Monday paper — *Who killed the Bushnells? We, the Beachcombers — three-baggers and homers* — and the town would say it to one another on the street for a week, delighted with itself.

Cirilo pitched, and his brothers played behind him — Luis steady at first, Abel brilliant and unreliable in center, a whole inning sometimes a thing of careless beauty and the next a thing he had plainly poured out of a bottle the night before — and for nine innings on a Sunday the Méndez family was not a fallen house in a stolen town but simply the local nine, *theirs,* cheered by name. Nunzio came when the kilns let him, with a wife and a swelling brood, and bellowed his obscene Italian encouragement, and wept when they won and wept when they lost, an uncomplicated man in a complicated country. It was as close to belonging as the place ever let any of them come, and Cirilo knew even then to hold it lightly, the way you hold anything on loan.

And at the end of the bench, in his street clothes, sat Edward Purse. He kept the score book in a copperplate hand finer than any banker's; he warmed the pitchers; he knew more baseball than Devereaux and Cirilo and both Daubenbiss brothers put together, and everyone, Purse included, knew it. Once — only once — a visiting club came up a man short, some flu or some fistfight, and for ten minutes in the bright Sunday air it was actually proposed, half in earnest, that Purse fill in, just for the day, just this once; and the whole field went quiet while a thousand people in their Sunday best decided, without a vote, without a single word, by the simple gravity of what the country was, that it would not happen. And it did not happen. And Purse picked the score book back up and licked his pencil and entered the next batter in his beautiful hand, and his face did not change at all, which was somehow the worst of it. Cirilo, watching from the mound, understood that he was being shown the exact dimensions of his own permission — that the same town now chanting his name would, the day it decided he had become a Mexican again instead of a ballplayer, fall just as silent and just as unanimous, and pick up its own pencil. He threw the rest of that game in a cold fury, a two-hit shutout, and did not enjoy a second of it; and he and Purse rode the trolley home afterward in a silence that was, for once, entirely shared.

### Chapter — Abel

If the family had a ghost while it was still living, it was Abel.

He was the middle brother, two years above Cirilo, and he had been born with the thing Cirilo had to work for — the gift, the raw and careless gift, a body that did what the game asked before the mind had finished asking it. The old men of the flats, the ones who had watched ball since the sandlot days, said it plainly and often, to Abel's face and within Cirilo's hearing, which was its own slow poison: *that one, the middle Méndez, he's the natural. The little one works. But Abel — Abel was given.* He could hit a ball farther at sixteen than Cirilo ever would; he ran down everything in the outfield; he threw with a long whippy ease that made Cirilo's hard-earned mechanics look like exactly what they were, which was earned. And he drank it all into the ground before he turned thirty, methodically, almost on purpose — and the family told itself for years that the drink had ruined the gift, when the truth, which only Cirilo ever quite let himself see, ran the other way: the gift had ruined Abel, because the gift had drawn the notice, and the notice, for a brown boy in that county, was the thing that got you hurt.

Abel had learned that at fifteen, the way Cirilo learned it later on the pier — except that Abel had not had a girl's laugh or an abuelo's fist or a plan to set against it. He had only the gift and the fear and no language to hold both, and so he did the thing a frightened boy does with a gift that frightens him: he got there first. Before the world could take his arm on the world's terms — before some Chuck Ashby could decide that a Mexican who hit the ball that far had gotten above himself — Abel took it himself, with a bottle, on his own schedule, so that when the ruin came it would at least be a ruin he had chosen. He was, in the family's private theology, the proof of the curse. Cirilo came, late, to understand him as something harder and truer than that: not a man the curse had struck down, but a man who had read the curse correctly and decided, rationally, in his way, to disarm it by failing on purpose — because a brown man who fails hurts only himself, and a brown man who succeeds, out loud, hurts everyone who loves him. It was the most logical thing in the world. It was also a slow suicide. Both things. Always, in this family, both things.

There was one night Cirilo carried the rest of his life. He had come home from a good Beachcombers Sunday, still warm with the crowd, and found Abel on the back step in the dark with a bottle, and he sat down beside him without a word; and after a while Abel said, not looking at him, in a voice with no self-pity in it at all, only a terrible clarity: *You're going to make it, hermano. You've got the thing I never had. Not the arm — I had the arm. The other thing. The thing that lets you take the slap on the pier and not swing. I'd have swung. I'd have swung the first day, and they'd have hung me for it, and that is why God gave the arm to the wrong brother.* And then he laughed, and drank, and would not say another serious word, and was dead inside the decade; and Cirilo paid for the stone, and had carved on it, over the family's objection that it was prideful, the one true thing — ABEL MÉNDEZ · HE WAS GIVEN — because somebody, Cirilo thought, standing at the grave with his own arm still sound and his own road still open before him, ought to say it in stone where the county could read it: that the boy had been given everything, and that being given everything, in that place, in those years, had been the precise thing that killed him.

### Chapter — Luis

And if Abel was the family's ghost, Luis was its spine — which is a harder and less romantic thing to be, and gets no headstone worth the carving.

He was the eldest, and the eldest of a fallen family is not a child but an institution: from the time he could carry a quarter of beef he was carrying the family, and he never once set it down. He had a gift too — smaller than Abel's, smaller than Cirilo's, but real, a steady reliable line-drive bat and soft hands at first base — and he folded it, without drama and without being asked, into a butcher's apron at the meat market on Pacific Avenue, because somebody had to bring home the thing that was certain while the other two chased the thing that was bright. He married at twenty, had children at twenty-one, and bought, on a butcher's credit, the small ordinary respectability the family had not known since the rancho: a house with a number on it, a pew with their name penciled in the book, a future you could see the shape of. It was the least glorious life any of the three brothers led, and by a wide margin the most useful — and useful, in this world, is the thing nobody writes the song about.

He carried his resentment of Cirilo the way a careful man carries a knife: folded, pocketed, brought out only for work, and never, he told himself, for malice. He loved his brother. He also knew that he, Luis, had been just good enough to wonder, all his life, what might have happened had *he* been the one the family could afford to gamble on — and that no one had ever once asked him to make the sacrifice he had made, which meant no one had ever thanked him for it, which meant it did not, in the family's accounting, quite count as a sacrifice at all; it was just Luis, being Luis, being there. When Cirilo's name began to travel, Luis cut every clipping and pasted them into a ledger book from the shop and told no one; and when a customer at the counter said *say, Méndez, any relation to the ballplayer?* he would say, gruffly, weighing the chops, *my brother* — and feel two things so tangled he could not have separated them with his own boning knife: a pride so fierce it frightened him, and a grief, small and clean and permanent, for the line drive he never got to find out whether he could hit. Both things. He wrapped the chops and made change and went home to the house with the number on it, which his brother — the king of the family, the man whose name the country would one day learn to say — never had, and never would.

### Chapter — Purse

Edward Purse kept the Beachcombers' books for eleven seasons and never played an inning, and then the Beachcombers folded, the way small clubs do, and he was forty and out of even the bench he had been allowed. And this is the part of the story the country prefers not to be told: that there was no late triumph for Edward Purse, no door that finally opened, no scout in a derby who came around at last to the right conclusion. The line drawn around him was drawn all the way around, with no gate in it anywhere, and he lived his whole life on the inside of it, and was never once, on any diamond that kept a record, allowed to be the thing that everyone who ever saw him throw a ball knew he was.

What he did instead was this. He took the beautiful copperplate hand that had kept the score books and got work as a porter on the Southern Pacific; and then, because he was the kind of man he was, he started keeping other books — for a colored ballclub he helped get up in the years the barnstorming Black teams came west, an Oakland nine that played the white semipros on the off-Sundays when the parks could be had cheap. He managed them and taught them all the baseball he had stored across eleven years of not being allowed to spend any of it, and some of his boys were better than anyone the Beachcombers ever fielded, and a few of them, a generation on, would be among the men hammering at the closed door from the outside until it finally, decades after Purse himself was in the ground, came off its hinges. He did not live to see Robinson. He died in 1931, of a winter chest, in Oakland, respected in his own world and invisible in the larger one; and the Oakland paper that the Black community read ran a long and loving obituary, and the Oakland paper that the white community read ran nothing at all — because as far as that paper and that country were concerned, Edward Purse had never existed, had never crouched behind a plate finer than any man in the county, had never been told by a thousand people in their Sunday best, without a vote and without a word, that he could not.

Cirilo wrote to him to the end — the two of them, the let-on and the kept-off, the friendship with the wall down the middle that neither could remove — and Purse wrote back, in that copperplate hand, dry funny letters about his boys and their progress and the slow education of the world. The last letter Cirilo ever had from him, in the summer of 1918, said only, after the baseball news: *They are saying your name in Boston, I read. Even the wrong one. It is a start, Méndez. Somebody has to make them practice saying it. You go on and make them practice.* Cirilo carried that letter in his breast pocket through the World Series. It was on him, folded against his heart, the afternoon they carried him off the field a champion, and it was on him in the bed in October; and Rebecca found it there, after, and read it, and understood for the first time the whole shape of what her husband had been carrying, and why, and for whom, all those years. She kept it with the telegram, in the lining of the glove, where the boy would find them both when he was a man — the two truest things anyone ever said to his father, written in two hands the country had tried, and failed, and would go on failing, to erase.

### Chapter — The Best League They Wouldn't Call Major

He climbed until he reached the highest rung a man like him could reach and still be told no, and there he stopped, and stayed, for years.

The Pacific Coast League was the best baseball west of the Mississippi and very nearly the best anywhere — a hundred and eighty games a season in the soft coast light, big crowds at Recreation Park, a brand of ball so good that men turned down the East to stay in it. They graded it Class A, and then Double-A, and called it, when they were generous, the third major league; and the generosity was the whole point, for it was major in everything but permission. Cirilo starred there for years. He beat, to their faces, men who got the call he never got.

He learned why, in the end, though no one ever said it to him, because the day he learned it he happened to be close enough to hear. He had just two-hit the Angels and walked off to a roar, and up in a box a major-league scout in a derby lowered his field glasses to the bird dog beside him and said the best arm he'd seen all year, a spitter that fell off a cliff, a man who read hitters like a cardsharp. And the bird dog licked his pencil and said, so we take him. And the scout — not even unkindly, just certain — said: he's thirty next year, and he's a, you know; the crowds back east won't warm to it, ownership won't spend the ink. Write down the kid in left. The blond one. Half the ballplayer. *But he's the right kind of half.*

Down on the grass, Cirilo tipped his cap to the only crowd that was allowed to love him, and did not know, and would never be told, that a man in a derby had just decided his whole life in a single sentence, for a reason no one would ever put on paper.

The kid in left was named Sören Lindqvist, and he was not a bad man, which is the part of the story with no comfort in it. He was a decent, limited, sweet-natured Minnesota farm boy with a wife already and a baby coming, and he knew — the way you know a draft in a sealed room — that the call that reached him in September had been earned, on that same grass, by the brown man who did not get one. He tried, once, drunk, to say something about it in the runway under the grandstand, some clumsy half-apology that died in his mouth; and Cirilo, who could have made him pay for it and did not, said only, *Go. Play well. Send the money home.* Because what was the use. Lindqvist had not drawn the line any more than Cirilo had; he had simply been born on the right side of it, and a man cannot un-receive a thing, cannot hand his family's bread back across the counter. So he took what was Cirilo's, and hated himself the regulation amount, and went east, and had a modest, forgettable, perfectly real big-league career — and thought of Cirilo Méndez, with a small private wince, perhaps twice a year, for the rest of his life. That is how it works. Not monsters. Frightened, decent men, taking the bread, looking away.

The years went by like that. He led the league and the league pretended not to notice. He grew older in the one place that wanted him, and the door east stayed shut, and he learned the thing his people had been learning for seventy years on the land they used to own: that you can be the best in the room and still be made to stand second in it, and that the only thing they cannot take from you, if you refuse to hand it over, is the refusal itself.

### Chapter — Swede

Sören Lindqvist — Swede to everyone, including, eventually, himself — was the pitcher who kept getting the calls that Cirilo Méndez earned; and the slow education of Swede Lindqvist, who was not a bad man, into the knowledge of what he had been handed and at whose expense, is its own small chapter in the long account.

He was a big fair Minnesota farm boy with a good-enough arm and a wonderful disposition, the kind of teammate everyone is glad to have, generous with his chew and his money and his praise; and he came up the same ladder Cirilo climbed, a rung or two ahead — not because he was better, which he knew he was not, and which was the thing that eventually undid his peace, but because he was *legible.* A scout could write *Lindqvist* in a notebook; a front office could picture *Lindqvist* in a city uniform; a crowd could chant *Lindqvist* without choking on it. None of those things were true of Méndez, and all of them were worth more, in that country, in those years, than a ball that died in the air. The Coast clubs called Swede up. The East bought Swede's contract. Swede got the train ticket and the raise and the line in the paper, year after year, while the better man pitched on behind him in the fog — and for a long time Swede did not think about it at all, because that is the deepest privilege of all, the one lying underneath the train ticket and the raise: not having to think about it.

It dawned on him slowly, the way the worst things do. There was a game — he told it on himself, years later, to anyone who would sit still, because he had become a man who needed to confess it and never quite could to the one person owed the confession — when he had pitched badly, been knocked around, been lifted; and Méndez had come on in relief and put the fire out cold, inning after inning; and afterward the writer had come to *Swede's* locker, to ask Swede how *he* felt about the win the bookkeeping had credited to him — and Swede had looked across the room at Méndez quietly icing the arm that had actually done it, and had felt, for the first time in his comfortable life, the floor tilt under him. He understood, in one nauseous instant, that he was standing on a man; that he had been standing on him for years; that the warmth and the chew and the easy generosity he had always taken as proof of his own good heart had cost him exactly nothing, because the machine had been quietly carrying *him* over the body of a better man the entire time, and calling it Swede's merit. He did one small thing then, the only thing the moment left him the courage for: he told the writer the truth. *Don't write me up. Méndez won that one. Go talk to Méndez.* And the writer laughed, taking it for modesty, and wrote Swede up anyway — because the machine does not accept corrections from the men it benefits, having no slot for them — and Swede read the lie about himself over breakfast and understood the last hard thing: that even his confession had been absorbed, costlessly, into his legend, as charm. He was not a villain. He never once did Cirilo a wrong with his own hands. He simply lived — gratefully, then guiltily, then finally just quietly — on the near side of a line he had not drawn and could not, by any act of private decency, undo. And the small unbearable truth of Swede Lindqvist, the one the country least wants spelled out, is that the line did not need villains to hold. It only ever needed good men who were glad of the train ticket and learned not to ask whose seat it had been.

### Chapter — The Coast

He gave the best years of his arm to the Coast, and the Coast loved him, and the country went on pretending he did not exist.

The San Francisco of the San Francisco Seals was a city of fog and appetite, and Recreation Park sat in the thick of it down on Eighth and Harrison — a ramshackle wooden bowl that held fifteen thousand when the fog let them come, with a section along the right-field line the regulars called the Booze Cage: seventy-five cents bought you a seat, two drinks, and a deviled-egg sandwich, and by the seventh inning it was the loudest, most knowledgeable, most profane real estate in California baseball. They came in their thousands on a Sunday — the Italians and the Irish and the Portuguese and the Mexicans, the longshoremen and the clerks, a whole city's worth of people who had also been told, in one language or another, to know their place — and they came to Rec Park precisely because for two hours on a Sunday the only place that mattered was sixty feet six inches from a man with a bat, and a brown pitcher who could make a baseball die in the air was not a Mexican there but a god.

Cirilo pitched for them the better part of a decade, and he was, by any honest reckoning of arms, one of the dozen best pitchers alive — and everyone who watched Coast ball knew it, and the East did not care, because the East had decided the Coast was a minor league, and had decided it partly to keep its own salaries down and partly because the men who ran the East could not quite imagine that the best baseball in America might be played by Italians and Mexicans in the fog at the wrong end of the continent. He won twenty-eight games one year. He threw both ends of a September doubleheader, both shutouts, because the club asked him to and the reserve clause made the asking a formality; and the Booze Cage carried him off on its shoulders, drunk and adoring, chanting his name into the fog, and some partisan with a good arm threw a silver dollar onto the mound, and then another, until the infield grass shone with them — the Coast's own crude coronation of the king the country would not crown.

And it was wonderful, and it was a cage, and Cirilo came to understand across those years the particular cruelty of being beloved exactly as far as the fog reached and not one foot farther. The scouts came — they always came — and sat behind the plate with their field glasses and their notebooks, and admired, and wrote down the blond kid in left, and went home. He learned to spot them by their hats, and to feel nothing when they left, which was its own kind of dying — the slow administrative kind, the kind with no funeral. He would lie awake in a Seals boardinghouse with the foghorns calling to each other out on the bay, thirty, then thirty-two, then thirty-four, the best man in the best league no one would call major; and he would take out the kid-leather glove that had been Rebecca's and hold it in the dark, and remember he had promised her he would make them say his name — and understand, with the foghorns answering on the water, that he was running out of the one thing a pitcher cannot buy more of at any price; that the door east might simply never open at all; and that a man could pour his whole gift into a game and a city and still, at the end of it, be handed nothing but a hatful of other men's silver dollars and a line in a record book that no one east of the Rockies would ever trouble himself to read.

### Chapter — The Wedding

They married at Holy Cross in 1916, in the teeth of both their worlds, and the church told the whole story without anyone saying a word.

It was half-empty, and the half that was there had a fault line straight down the center aisle. On one side sat the Méndez family — Dolores and Esteban, the brothers, Don Aurelio — in Sunday clothes worn thin, wary rather than joyful, because they had learned that when the world noticed them it was usually to take something. On the other: almost no one, and a single empty pew where the Whitfields would have sat. Rebecca's people had not come. Rebecca's people were, as of a letter some years old now, no longer Rebecca's people.

She walked the aisle alone, in a plain dress — the gowns had belonged to the life she had traded away — because there was no father to give her, and so she gave herself, which she would have told you, had you asked, she had been doing her whole life anyway. The priest spoke in Spanish, and Rebecca, who had been learning it in secret for a decade, answered him in it.

Dolores had not wanted this wedding, and on the day, at Holy Cross, she still did not; she stood on her side of the fault line with her face composed and her heart withheld, because she had buried two children and survived a stolen century by trusting nothing the Anglo world ever offered, and a banker's daughter marrying her son looked to her, for a long time, like precisely the kind of gift this country gave her people — the kind with a hook in it, the kind you paid for later and forever. She had watched the girl for two years for the hook. It would be months yet — in a freezing Brooklyn kitchen, the morning she found the banker's daughter ruining a pot of beans with the grim determination of a woman who had never been taught to cook and meant to learn now or starve her husband trying — before something in the old woman's chest that had been clenched for two years quietly let go, and she crossed the kitchen and took the spoon, not unkindly, and said the first warm thing she ever said to her: *Not like that. Like this.* But that was months off. On the wedding day Dolores only watched the girl walk the aisle alone and give herself because there was no one to give her, and answer the priest in hard-won Spanish, and she filed it away, the way she filed everything, to be weighed later, when the world had shown its hand.

Afterward, on the steps, Don Aurelio took his grandson's arm — quiet, not unkind, afraid. *First the game. Now an Anglo man's daughter. You keep reaching over your head, mijo. Our family was not built to be first; I have watched it cost us for ninety years.* And Cirilo, gentle and certain, said the only thing there was to say to a curse he refused to believe in: *Then I'll be the one it stops costing.*

And behind them, in the thin October light, Rebecca laughed at something one of the brothers had said — the real laugh, the surprised one, the one from the booth, sixteen years old now and still knocking itself loose out of people who had forgotten how.

### Chapter — Two of a Kind

The other great star the San Francisco fog produced in those years was a thick, joyful, prodigiously strong Italian kid off Telegraph Hill named Francesco Pezzolo, who played under the name Ping Bodie because *Pezzolo* would not fit in a box score and *Bodie* would — and who, in 1910, hit thirty home runs over the Coast League fences, the first man ever to do it, in an age when thirty home runs was very nearly a supernatural number, and made the whole bay forget, for a season, to call him a wop.

He and Cirilo were not close — Bodie was loud where Cirilo was quiet, and their paths crossed only when the Seals played wherever Bodie was — but they had between them the easy wordless freemasonry of men who have both learned to play a game beautifully in a country that has decided their names are too foreign to say. They sat together once, rained out, under a leaking grandstand roof in Sacramento, two of the best ballplayers in the West watching the puddles spread on the tarp, and Bodie — who said out loud everything Cirilo only thought — said it: *You know what kills me, Méndez? They'll take the home runs. They'll take the strikeouts. They'll cheer themselves hoarse. They just won't take* us. *They want the ball to fly and the name to stay foreign. They want the show without the people.* And he laughed his big laugh, because Bodie's way of carrying it was to laugh, the way Cirilo's was to go silent and Abel's had been to drink: three different men, three different countries inside the same one, each carrying the identical weight a different way.

Bodie got his call east. The big leagues took him — for the Italian had got there first, in the long immigrant relay, the way the Irish had got there before the Italians and the Germans before the Irish, the door opening a crack wider for each new wave as it became, by the arrival of the next, no longer quite the bottom. The Mexican was still the bottom. The door that opened for Ping Bodie in 1911 did not open for Cirilo Méndez, who was a better pitcher than Bodie was a hitter, and they both knew it, and neither said it, sitting under the leaking roof — because there was nothing to say about it that the rain was not already saying, steadily, on the tarp, for both of them, in the only language the country ever spoke plainly to men like them: *not yet. Not yet. Maybe not ever. But not yet.*

### Chapter — The Dying Ball

Every pitcher has a pitch that is the truest expression of who he is, and Cirilo Méndez's was the spitball — the dying ball, the wet one — and it was no accident that the pitch which defined him was a pitch the rulebook was already planning to take away.

He had it from his abuelo, who had it from God knew where: the Gold Rush sandlots, the bare-handed men who played the game before there were rules enough to break. You wet two fingers; you gripped the ball just so, off-center; and you threw it hard, with a fastball's arm — and the moisture killed the spin, and a ball with no spin does not obey the air, it betrays it; it sails in flat and true, and then, in the last ten feet, having no spin to hold it up, it simply gives up and *dies* — drops off the table, falls out from under the bat like a thing that has decided, mid-flight, to stop pretending. It was legal in those years; every pitcher who could throw it did. Cirilo threw it better than almost anyone alive, because he threw it the way his abuelo had taught him — with a mind behind it, setting it up, hiding it, saving it, never showing the same eye level twice.

But the old man had folded a warning into the gift, the day he first showed it behind the Beach Flats backstop, and Cirilo carried the warning as long as he carried the pitch: *They will outlaw it. A few years, they say, and it's gone. So learn it now and break their hearts with it while you can. Everything they give us has a clock on it, mijo. Throw fast.* And the old man was right, as he was right about most of the hard things and wrong only about the one that mattered: in 1920 the rule-makers banned the spitball — grandfathering a handful of established big-league names to throw it to the end of their careers, and outlawing it for everyone else — and Cirilo Méndez was not on the grandfathered list, because to be on the list you had to have been *seen,* counted, recorded, allowed; and he had spent his best years throwing it in a fog the East had decided did not count. The pitch that made him was taken from the game two years after it took him. He died, in 1918, still legally able to throw the only thing that was wholly his — which was, his son Joaquín would think much later, perhaps the single mercy in the whole account: that the one thing they never managed to take from Cirilo Méndez before the flu did was the dying ball. The pitch with the clock on it. The pitch that was the truest picture of his whole people's whole history — thrown flat and hard and true into a hostile air, holding its line as long as it could, and then, having been given nothing to hold it up, falling. But falling, always, exactly where he aimed it. Falling on purpose. Falling like a decision. Dying, when it had to die, precisely where it would do the most good.

### Chapter — A Masterpiece

There was one afternoon in the fog when Cirilo Méndez pitched the best game of his life, and no one east of the Rockies will ever know it happened — and that sentence is the whole of what this country did to men like him, written small.

It was a Sunday at Recreation Park, the Seals against Los Angeles, and from the first pitch he had the thing that comes to a pitcher maybe a dozen times in a career if he is lucky and never at all if he is not: the day when the ball does exactly what the mind asks before the mind has finished asking it, when the catcher's mitt seems to pull the pitch into it, when the hitters walk back to the bench already beaten and knowing it. He did not walk a man. He did not let a man reach. Twenty-seven up and twenty-seven down — a perfect game, the rarest thing in baseball — thrown in the fog at the wrong end of the continent, in a league the East had decided did not count, in front of fifteen thousand people who would remember it the rest of their lives and not one sportswriter who could make the country remember it at all. The Booze Cage came apart. They threw the silver dollars. They carried him off. It was, by any measure of the thing itself, one of the great games anyone ever pitched, and it survives today in no record book that matters — because the record books that matter were kept by the men who had decided the fog did not count, and they were not watching, and would not have written it down if they had been.

He sat in the clubhouse afterward, thirty-three years old, perfect, beloved, and unwanted, and walked all the way into a thing he had been circling for years: that there was no game good enough. That was the cruel gift the perfect game gave him. He had believed, somewhere under the silence, that if he were only good enough — if he were *perfect,* if he left them no possible reason — the door would have to open. And he had just been perfect, as perfect as the game permits a man to be, and the door had not so much as rattled, because the door had never been about how good he was. It had been about *what* he was, and no number of strikeouts would ever change what he was; a man could throw a perfect game every Sunday for the rest of his life and still die a Mexican in a country that had already decided what that meant. He went home to Rebecca that night and she saw his face and said, *you won — you did something today,* and he said yes, and for a while could say no more, and then he said the truest and most desolate thing he ever said to her: *I was perfect today, and it wasn't enough, and now I know it is never going to be enough — and I think that's the day a man either quits, or decides to do it anyway, for some reason that isn't them.* And she waited. And he said: *I'm going to do it anyway.* And that — not the perfect game, which the world forgot, but the kitchen afterward, where a man looked the whole cruelty of his life dead in the face and chose to keep pitching into it — was the real masterpiece, the one no fog and no record book and no country could ever take from him, because he threw it where only she would ever see.

### Chapter — His Mother's Hands

He came home when he could, across the PCL years, and the homecomings blurred into one in his memory afterward, all but the hands — his mother's hands, which he watched the way other men watch the sea, because they were the one fixed thing in a life another man's word moved from town to town.

Dolores's hands were not beautiful and never had been; they were a working woman's hands, thick-knuckled, burn-scarred from forty years of stoves, the nails worn short, the skin gone to a leather of its own — for there was more than one kind of tanning in that family, and hers was the slow kind, done by the work itself, upon the worker. He would sit in her kitchen, a grown man, a ballplayer the fog threw silver dollars at, and watch those hands roll tortillas and tell beads and mend his brothers' shirts and never once stop moving; and he would think a thing he never said, because it would have embarrassed them both: that the whole country kept careful records of what men's hands did — his strikeouts, his wins, the box scores, the bronze — and kept no record at all of what these hands had done, which was *everything,* which was to hold the entire family up over the water for forty years by main strength, unpaid, unwatched, unrecorded, uncheered. There was no Hall of Fame for his mother's hands. There was no agate type. There was only the family, still alive, still speaking the language, still climbing the hill on Sundays — which was the whole of her monument, and which she would have told you, had you been foolish enough to call it a sacrifice, was not a monument and not a sacrifice but simply *Tuesday;* and Wednesday; and all the other days; the doing of the next needed thing, world without end.

She asked him once, on one of those visits, not looking up from the masa — the only time she ever asked him anything about the deeper matter: *Are you happy out there, mijo? In the fog? With the men who will not call you up?* And Cirilo, who lied to everyone, who had lied to the whole grandstand his whole life with his silence, found he could not lie to his mother's hands, and told her the truth. *No, Mamá. Not happy. But — right. I am doing the right thing with what I was given, even though it costs, even though they won't call me. That is not happy. But I think it might be better than happy. I think it might be the thing happy is only the cheap version of.* And Dolores nodded, and went on with the masa, and said the thing he carried to Boston and to the end — the benediction of a woman who had been doing exactly that her whole unrecorded life: *Yes. That is the thing. Happy is for people who were given easy lives. We were given this one. Do the right thing with it, and let them keep their happy. The right thing lasts. I have buried half of what I love, and I am telling you: only the right thing lasts.* And she handed him a warm tortilla, because the talk was over and the boy needed feeding, and that was that — the deepest conversation of his life, conducted over masa, by a woman who could not read, whose hands the country never once thought to record, and who had understood, all along, the entire thing this whole long book is about.

### Chapter — The Last Time Home

He did not know, the morning he left Santa Cruz after the wedding, that it was the last time he would ever see it alive. A man never does. The last time you see the place that made you, you almost always believe it is only one of many times — which is the small mercy and the great cruelty of the way a life is built.

The town had changed past his childhood and went on changing. The boardwalk he had swept as a boy was a vast electric thing now — a casino and a coaster and a million bulbs, the leisure-machine fully built at last on the ground his family's cattle had grazed — and the flats had filled and the kilns had slowed and the wharf had lengthened, and the new people did not know there had ever been old people, which is how a town forgets: not by deciding to, but simply by adding newcomers faster than it keeps rememberers. He walked it that last morning with Rebecca on his arm — his wife now, in the open, in daylight, on the very planks where once he had not been allowed even to look at her — and the small triumph of that was real, and was not nothing, and he let himself have it.

He had said his goodbyes the night before. Luis, gruff, with a clipping-book under his arm he was too proud to show and too proud not to mention. His mother, who held his face the way she always had, and gave him the language, and did not let him see her weep until he had turned away. And the old man — Don Aurelio, ninety now and more, gone to paper and bone in the good chair, his eyes the only young thing left in him — who took Cirilo's pitching hand in his two cold ones, the old gesture, the hundredth time, and held it, and said in a voice gone to a whisper: *I will not see you again, mijo. No — don't. We are too old for lies, you and I. I will not see you again. And I have been wrong, I think, about the family, about the curse; I have begun to believe I had it backward my whole life, and I am too near the end now to work out how — so it will fall to you to show me, after, whichever of us is watching, and from wherever we end up watching from.* And he had smiled, the old man, with his three remaining teeth, and let the hand go, and said the last thing he ever said to his grandson, the thing Cirilo carried onto the train and across the country and up onto the mound in October and down into the bed at the end: *Throw fast, Cirilo. Everything they give us has a clock on it. You always did listen to that part. Throw fast.*

And Cirilo went down to the depot — the same little depot, fifteen years on — and boarded the train east; and this time Rebecca boarded it with him, openly, his wife, and the town that had drawn every line in the world around them watched them go and could no longer do a single thing about it. And as the platform slid away he made himself, this once, look back — because he was not a frightened boy any longer, and because some partings a man has earned the right to witness — and he held the look until the bend in the track carried the whole of it away: the boardwalk and the wharf and the hills and the chapel-that-wasn't and the entire stolen, beloved, unforgetting town, gone around the curve and out of his sight for good.

### Chapter — The Margins

What the world remembers, when it remembers a marriage at all, is the wedding and the deathbed — the two days with the good clothes on. What a marriage actually *is,* though, is the thousands of ordinary days in between; and theirs had begun long before the church. She had walked out of her father's house and cast her lot with him the year she was twenty, and they had been together — in the eyes of the flats and of God, if not yet of the law — through a dozen hard years before Holy Cross ever made it official. Across all those days, the lean ones, it went like this.

They moved a great deal, because a ballplayer the reserve clause can sell goes where he is sold; and Rebecca learned to make a home out of a valise and a rented room in a strange town in under a day — to find the cheap market and the Catholic church and the one grocer who would extend a little credit to a brown man's family, to hang the single framed thing they owned (a tinted photograph of a mission chapel, bought off a peddler, that was almost certainly not the rancho's chapel at all) so that wherever they woke, they woke somewhere that was theirs. She had been raised to run a house with six servants. She learned instead to run a marriage with none, on a journeyman's pay, in towns that would not always serve her husband at the counter — and she did it, after the first hard year, not with the grim martyrdom of the convert to poverty but with a flat competent cheer that surprised everyone, herself most of all. The girl who had been an ornament turned out to have, underneath the ornament, an iron the Whitfields had never suspected and never deserved.

They were happy. That is the thing that does not make it into the kind of story the world tells about people like them — the world wants its dispossessed either nobly suffering or righteously enraged, and keeps no column in its ledger for *happy* — but they were, in the margins, on the cheap, in the rented rooms, more reliably and ordinarily happy than nearly anyone Rebecca had grown up among in the warm lit cages on the hill. They had their jokes, the long-running private ones no outsider could have decoded. They had a way of reading in the same lamplight, her head on his shoulder, that needed no words. They had the thing they had found in the oak grove and never lost — the being-known — and it turned out that being known, by one other person, all the way down, is the rarest and most expensive thing in the world, and that they had somehow, two of the least-moneyed people in America, contrived to own it outright, free and clear: the one piece of property no court could ever take. He still could not, often, say the soft things; the silence was too old in him. But he learned other languages for it. On the cold nights he got up first and warmed her side of the bed before she climbed in, and never once mentioned it; and she pretended, for years, not to notice, so that he would never stop. That was the marriage. That was the whole of it. And it was enough — it was more than the men on the hill, with everything, ever managed to buy.

### Chapter — The Letter

The letter came in the ninth year of her exile, forwarded twice, the expensive cream stock gone soft with handling; and Rebecca knew the hand before she had the envelope fully out of the box, and stood a long moment in a Sacramento boardinghouse hallway with it unopened in her fingers — because some letters you have been waiting nine years for, and dreading exactly as long.

It was from her father's lawyer, not her father, which told her most of what it had come to say before she read a word: that Horace Whitfield was dying — the heart, the doctors said, though Rebecca thought privately it was more likely the seawall finally giving way, the lifelong terror of the bottom drowning at last the man who had built his whole life as a levee against it — and that he wished, before the end, to see his daughter, and to discuss, the lawyer's careful phrasing ran, *certain provisions.* Provisions. Even dying, even reaching for her at the last, he could not help reaching through an attorney, in the language of estates, offering reconciliation and an inheritance in the same breath as though they were the same thing — as though they had ever, to him, been distinguishable. The hook was still in the gift. It would be in the gift in his coffin.

She went. Cirilo offered to come and she told him no, gently — this was the one country he could not cross with her — and she rode the train down alone to the great house on Nob Hill where she had been an ornament, and sat by the bed of the frightened old man who had thrown her overboard to keep his boat above the line. And she discovered, sitting there, the thing nine years in the margins had quietly done to her without her noticing: she was not angry anymore. The anger had needed him to be a monster, and he was not a monster; he was only small, and frightened, and dying, and sorry in the cramped way that was the most he had ever managed of any feeling at all — and you cannot stay angry at small and frightened and dying, not if the margins have taught you anything about what it costs to be a person. He took her hand. He could not, even now, say that he had been wrong; the words did not exist in him. Instead he said, looking at the ceiling, that he had heard her husband was *doing well in his line* — which was the nearest thing to a blessing the old man kept in stock, and which Rebecca, who had married a king and learned to read kings, accepted as one. He left her the money. She took it — not for herself, for she had discovered she did not need it, which was the freest she had ever felt, but for the boy who was coming, and for the glove, and because refusing it would have been a way of staying in the fight, and she was, she found, all the way done with the fight. She buried her father in the plot with her mother and the long line of clawed-up Whitfields, and she did not weep and she did not gloat, and she rode the train back north to her real life with the dead world's money in her bag and the dead world's hold on her finally, fully gone. That was the day, she would say later — not the wedding, and not even the boy — that she became entirely her own: the day she sat at her father's deathbed and felt the anger turn to pity and the pity to a clean and final freedom, and understood that she had not, after all, been the one in the cage.

### Chapter — The Winter Hides

He reached the major leagues at last, in his middle thirties, and it nearly starved him.

The call, when it finally came, came from Brooklyn — Uncle Robbie's Robins, the National League's most dependable heartbreak, a ballclub that knew about being second the exact way Cirilo did. It was the thing he had chased for the better part of two decades, and he stood in a big-league uniform in a cold-water Brooklyn flat and could not make the month meet itself. The reserve clause pinned an old rookie's pay to the floor, and Brooklyn rent ate the floor, and there was no other club he was permitted to ask, because his contract was owned, the way everything about him had always been owned.

They had two cold rooms on the third floor of a tenement that belonged to a widow named Aoife Doyle, and it was from Mrs. Doyle, more than from anyone, that Cirilo learned what the great roaring immigrant city actually was beneath its noise: a place where everyone had come from somewhere they could no longer go back to, and had buried something along the way, and was holding on by the fingernails to a ledge that the next bad winter might pry them off of. Aoife Doyle kept a gold-star flag in her front window for a son named Liam who lay in the French ground near a town she could not pronounce, and she ran her house like a top sergeant and her grief like a furnace she would let no one stand near, and she was, under all the iron of her, so kind that the kindness embarrassed her into rudeness. The month the baby was coming, when a ballplayer's wage somehow would not cover both a doctor and the rent, she let the rent go — and then barked at Rebecca for a week over nothing at all, and left a covered pot of stew outside their door with no note, and denied it to Rebecca's face. She had decided, somewhere in her ruined heart, that these two were hers to keep alive now, the way Liam was not, and she went about saving them with the ferocious, graceless tenderness of a woman who has learned the hard way that love is not a feeling you have but a series of unpleasant things you do for people while pretending to be put out about it.

So he did what his father had done before him. One winter night Rebecca saw his hands — the pitching hands, the good unmarked ones — come home raw and stained dark at the knuckles, the old tannery stain, and she understood before he said it.

"You went back to the hides," she said.

"A tannery in Newark takes on winter men. No questions. They don't care what a man's name is if his hands know the work." A laugh with nothing in it. "I pitch in the major leagues, Rebecca, and I can't make the rent on it. The club owns my contract and pays me what it pleases, and there's no other club I'm allowed to ask. They call it the reserve clause. My abuelo had a shorter word."

"Leather," she said.

"Bought and sold and never asked. Four generations, and the family business is the same. Only now the hide is mine."

She took his stained hands in her clean ones — the heiress's hands, that had chosen this — and did not let go. "Then we work the winter," she said. "We wait for the spring. We do not quit. Say it." And he said it, the family creed, worn smooth as a river stone: *We do not quit.*

### Chapter — The Newark Tannery

The tannery in Newark took on winter men, no questions asked, and that was how Cirilo Méndez — twenty-eight games a season at his peak, a name the Coast had thrown silver dollars at — came to spend the off-seasons of his major-league career trimming another man's hides in New Jersey for a wage that embarrassed no one to name.

It was a bigger, meaner operation than Kron's: a barn of a place on the Passaic, the river running whatever color they were tanning that week, the air a wall you walked into. The men who worked it were the bottom stratum of the great immigrant city — Poles and Slovaks and Italians, and a few Black men up from the South who had found the North's welcome warmer in the telling than in the having, and a Mexican ballplayer nobody recognized, because nobody in a Newark tannery in January was thinking about the Pacific Coast League. They did not know him and he did not tell them, and there was a deep, unexpected peace in that — in being, for three months a year, simply a good pair of hands on the beam, judged by nothing but the work; which was, he reflected once, scraping a hide in the steam with his own breath hanging white in the cold, the only place in the entire country where being a brown man with skilled hands was an unmixed advantage and not a problem to be managed. The foreman, a one-eyed Slovak named Hlavác who had lost the eye to a splash of lime years before and ran the floor with a profane tenderness, took to him because he was quick and quiet and did not complain, and gave him the better work — and never knew, until he read it in the paper the following October, *EL CID DEAD OF INFLUENZA,* that the silent quick Mexican who had trimmed hides beside him two winters running had been, all along, one of the best pitchers alive. Hlavác clipped that one, too. Half the country, it turned out, was keeping clippings of a man whose name they had never quite been given to say.

And here was the thing Cirilo carried home from the Passaic each spring, turning it over on the long train west: the leather did not care. The hide on the beam did not know whether the hands working it had once belonged to a ranchero king, or a tannery wage-man, or a major-league pitcher; it yielded to skill and to nothing else, honestly, the same for every man — the one perfectly fair transaction in his entire unfair life. There was a rest in that he never found anywhere else, not even on the mound. His great-grandfather had tanned hides as a kind of kingship and his father as a kind of defeat, and Cirilo, in the steam of a Newark winter with a championship still ahead of him that he could not yet see, tanned them as a kind of prayer — the only church, he sometimes thought, that had never once checked his name at the door.

### Chapter — Hooper

It was Harry Hooper who opened the door at last, and Harry Hooper spent the rest of his long honorable life not quite forgiving himself for how little it had cost him to do it, and how long he had waited.

Hooper was the gentleman of the Red Sox outfield and one of the genuinely good men the game produced — Hollister-born, a Saint Mary's man, an engineer by training who played ball because he was better at it than at engineering and because he loved it; a man who would, in the fullness of time, reach the Hall of Fame and deserve it. He had grown up a county over from the flats, on Soquel ground, and had seen Coast League ball as a boy, and had known — the way everyone who watched Coast ball knew — that the best pitcher in the West was an aging Mexican named Méndez the East would not call; and he had known *why* the East would not call him; and he had said nothing, for years, because it was not his fight and he had his own career to mind and the machinery of the thing was so large and so settled that one outfielder's opinion seemed a pebble thrown at a mountain.

That was the part he could not forgive. Not that he finally spoke — he did, in 1917, when the war had stripped the staff and Barrow was desperate and a word might actually land; he leaned on the front office, quietly, persistently, the way a respected veteran can, and said *there's an arm out west, an old one, but it's better than what you'll find, and I've watched it my whole life* — and the word landed, and the contract was bought, and the door came open. He did the right thing. But he did it in 1917, when it was safe and useful and cost him nothing, and he had known the truth since boyhood, and the distance between the knowing and the saying was sixteen years long; and on the far side of those sixteen years was a man who had given his whole prime to the fog because no Harry Hooper had spoken sooner. *I could have said it in 1909,* Hooper told a sportswriter once, decades on, an old man asked about the old days, and the writer did not even understand what he meant and printed it as a pleasantry. *I knew in 1909. I waited till it was easy.* He was the best of them, the decent man inside the unjust house — and the hard lesson of Harry Hooper, the one the country least likes to hear, is that the decent men inside the unjust house are not the same as justice, and the honest ones know it, and it is the honest ones it haunts: that a good man who waits until the right thing is also the safe thing has done a good thing, and has also let a man wait sixteen years, and both are true, and the second does not wash out, ever, however clean the first one looks in the telling.

### Chapter — The Call

It was the war that finally opened the door — which is to say it was a hundred thousand graves that finally opened the door — and Cirilo would never quite forgive himself for walking through it grateful.

In the winter of 1917 a telegram came to the cold Brooklyn rooms, and he stood in the doorway stamping snow off his boots with his face doing something it did not have a name for. Boston had bought his contract. The Red Sox. The major leagues wanted a thirty-five-year-old spitballer they could have for the price of the train. Rebecca rose, the joy breaking over her — and he stopped her, gently, because the truth had to come with it. They wanted him because the young arms were in France. Half of Barrow's staff was in a trench or under one. Sixteen years he had waited for the call, and it came at last because the country had run out of white arms to send up.

She took his frozen face in both her hands. "It does not matter why the door opened," she said. "It matters that you walk through it. You have waited your whole life for a door to open at all." And it was Hooper who had put his name in — Harry Hooper, the Hollister boy who had played Soquel ball one ridge from his brothers — so that after everything, the man who finally remembered him was a kid from up the road.

He lifted the worn glove off the table, his father's leather, and turned it once. His abuelo would say it was too high, he told her; he'd say the family was never meant to climb this far. "Then we climb it for this one," Rebecca said, a hand on her belly, where their son was already waiting for the first day of the year. "Let the curse take it up with a baby."

And from three thousand miles west, faint, a letter in the old man's hand seemed to follow them onto the train going east: *Mijo. They call you to the very top now. Be careful up there. The air is thin where they let us stand — and they have never, not once, caught us when we fall.*

## _Siempre Segundo_ — Act III, novelized (full chapters)

### Chapter — The First Day of the Year

The boy came in the first hour of the first day of the year, which Cirilo would decide later was the only wholly good omen the world ever sent him; and like all good omens, it arrived disguised as terror.

It was so cold in the Brooklyn rooms that the water in the basin had skinned over with ice by the time the midwife wanted it warm, and Cirilo — who had stood sixty feet from men paid to take his head off and never once felt his hands shake — stood in the doorway of his own bedroom and shook like a boy.

It was Aoife Doyle who had gone for the midwife — barked Cirilo out of the way and down the stairs and into the street herself, an old woman in a shawl thrown over her nightdress, running on the ice to fetch and then browbeat a doctor into coming on credit, because she had decided weeks before that this baby would arrive on her watch and would, by God, arrive properly. She came back up and put the water on and took the place by the bed that a mother might have taken, had Rebecca had a mother, or a mother-in-law nearer than three thousand miles. And between the worst of Rebecca's cries she stood a moment at the window by the little gold-star flag she kept there for a son named Liam who lay in the French ground — an old woman with one boy gone into the earth, listening to another fight his way out of it, in the same cold hour, in the same small rooms, the two facts sitting side by side without canceling and without comforting, simply both true, the way the best and the worst of a life will sometimes arrive together and ask you to hold them both at once. Then she turned back from the window and rolled up her sleeves and went to work, because the living were still here and needed her, and the dead, she had learned, did not.

Rebecca had stopped being quiet hours before. He had not known there were sounds like that in her, the heiress, the girl who laughed sideways, the woman who had walked out of a Nob Hill drawing room and into this; and each one went through him like a thrown strike he could not catch.

And then, near three in the morning, a different sound: thin, furious, alive. The midwife said something he did not hear. Rebecca said something he did, because she had been saving it, had carried it up out of the pain on purpose to give to him. "First baby of the year, she says. Born on the one. Before anybody else's." She found his eyes across the lamplight, exhausted past the far side of exhaustion, and she was smiling. "First, Cirilo. He's first at something already."

He crossed the cold floor and took the small hands in his — both of them folded into one of his, the gesture his abuelo had made over him thirty-six years and three thousand miles ago, on a floor that smelled of lime and hide — and found he could not speak, so he said the only words the family had. "Already big," he managed. "Already strong."

He went to the chair by the window where the old glove always sat, his father's leather and his great-grandfather's tanning, soft now past softness, and carried it back and laid it in the crook of the cradle beside his son, the way another man in another family might have laid down the deed to a thousand acres. "There," he said. "Now you own the only thing we never lost."

Outside, over the black river, a few horns and a few drunk voices were still seeing the new year in. Cirilo Méndez stood in the cold with his wife and his son and his father's glove, a thirty-six-year-old rookie with a tannery stain still under his nails, and for the length of one held breath he was not second to any man alive.

### Chapter — The Christening

They christened the boy at a Brooklyn church with a name three thousand miles long and no one of his own blood to hold him at the font.

It should have been the rancho's chapel, the one Don Joaquín built, the one the family no longer owned. It should have been Holy Cross with the whole flats packed in, Dolores's hands and the old man's blessing and the brothers loud in the back. Instead it was a strange cold church in a strange cold borough, a busy Irish priest who mangled the surname twice and gave up, and a congregation of exactly three: Cirilo, Rebecca, and Aoife Doyle, who had appointed herself godmother by the simple expedient of arriving in her good black dress and daring anyone to say otherwise.

They named him Joaquín — Joaquín Méndez, for the great-great-grandfather who had wept signing the grant, the king of the lost kingdom, a name with a whole century of California folded into it. The Brooklyn priest could not pronounce it and the Brooklyn clerk misspelled it on the certificate, *Joachim Mendez,* anglicizing the boy in the first hour of his official existence exactly as the papers were anglicizing his father across the river. Cirilo looked at the misspelled certificate a long moment and did not correct it, because he had learned the lesson of his own life too well: they will name you what they can pronounce, and the only answer is to make the name mean something anyway, by what you do, until one day they are obliged to learn to say it right. He folded the certificate into the lining of the old glove, where it would still be when the boy was a grown man, soft with handling.

Don Aurelio's blessing came by telegram, because the old man was too frail to travel now and they both knew he was dying and neither said so. It was the longest telegram Cirilo ever received, and must have cost the family a week of bread to send, and it read, in the clipped telegraphic Spanish of a man counting every word at the going rate: HE IS FIRST CHILD OF THE YEAR STOP TELL HIM THAT WHEN HE IS OLD STOP TELL HIM HIS NAME IS A KING STOP TELL HIM WE WERE NOT FINISHED STOP. Rebecca kept it her whole long life. She would read it to the boy on his birthdays, every New Year's, all the years after, until he had it by heart and could say it back to her — *first child of the year, your name is a king, we were not finished* — three generations of refusal folded into a yellow telegraph form: the cheapest and the most expensive thing the family ever bought.

### Chapter — The Weeks Between

There were a few weeks, that winter, between the birth and the train to Boston, that were the closest thing to peace Cirilo Méndez was given in his whole driven life — and he did not recognize them as peace until they were over, which is the usual way of it.

The baby did not sleep, and so Cirilo did not sleep, and he found that he did not mind: that there was something in walking the two cold rooms at three in the morning with the small furious weight of his son against his shoulder that undid, a little, the held thing he had carried since the pier — the silence, the chain. He could not say the soft words to Rebecca; he had tried, across the years, and the words died somewhere below his throat where his father's fear had built its wall. But he found that he could say them to the baby, in the dark, in Spanish, the language his mother had kept alive in his own mouth the very same way — could say all of it, the whole flood: *mijo, mijo, you are the king's namesake and the first child of the year, and you will not be second, I swear it to you, I will make them say your name* — could pour out, to a creature who could not yet understand a word of it, the entire dammed reservoir of a lifetime's unsaid tenderness. And Rebecca, lying awake in the next room and hearing the low murmur of it come through the wall, learned more about the man she had married in those few weeks than in all the years before, and never told him she had heard — the way she never told him about the warmed bed — so that he would not stop.

He held the boy's hands and counted the fingers and turned them in the gray Brooklyn light, the way the old man had turned his, the way he would turn them again at the end. He did the small useless adoring arithmetic that new fathers do — whose nose, whose chin, how big will the hands get — and underneath it, always, the cold thing he would not look at directly: that he was thirty-six, and the door east had finally opened, and a man does not get many springs at thirty-six; that whatever he was going to make them say, he would have to make them say it now, this year, while the arm still answered; that the peace of the two cold rooms was borrowed, like everything the family had ever held, and that the lender was already, faintly, knocking. He thought it was only time that was knocking. It was the flu. It was both. It is always, in this family, both.

On the last morning he held the baby a long while at the window and showed him the gray river and the gray city, and told him, in Spanish, that his father was going away to do a thing, and would come back, and would teach him to throw — *I will teach you the dying ball, mijo, while they still let us throw it; everything they give us has a clock on it; you will have to learn to throw fast* — and then he handed his son to Rebecca, and took up his valise and his father's glove, and went down to the train. He would see the boy exactly once more, in October, for four days, before the end. He did not know that, either. A man never does. He went down to the train thinking only that he was running late, and that the season was waiting, and that *first,* after a whole lifetime of *second,* was finally close enough to reach out and take.

### Chapter — The East

He had never left California until the want and the war drove him east at thirty-six, and so the East, when he finally reached it, struck him the way it strikes everyone born on the far coast: as a foreign country that happened to fly the same flag.

The train came down into Boston through three days of country he had no map for — the dry West, the flat middle, then the crowded green East, older and tighter and grayer than anything in his California: a land worn smooth by people over three hundred years, where his own coast still had the raw new-cut look even in its oldest corners. Boston itself was a stone city, close and vertical and cold in a way the dry cold of a Sierra winter had never prepared him for — a wet cold that got into the old tannery ache in his hands and stayed. And the people were courteous and closed, nothing like the loud democratic carnival of Rec Park; a reserve he mistook at first for hostility and came to understand was only the East being the East, holding everyone at the same arm's length — which was, he decided, its own strange mercy. In Boston he was held at arm's length like everybody else, and there was a cold equality in that he had never once felt at home, where the distance they kept from him had always been a different and more pointed distance.

He found two rooms in a part of the city where the rents were what a rookie's pay could bear, among the Irish and the Italians and the Jews — the great immigrant tide of the East that had come in through Boston and New York the way his own people had simply always *been there,* before the border crossed them — and there was an odd comfort in being, for once, only one more newcomer among newcomers, in a city so crowded with arrivals that no one had the energy to make a project of him. And the first time he walked into Fenway Park — three years old then, a brick bandbox with its strange high wall in left and its odd green angles — he stood a while in the empty stands in the gray morning, before anyone else had come: an old man at the end of a road that had run from a tannery floor in Santa Cruz through the bushes and the fog to *this,* the top, the actual top, the major leagues of the actual United States. He thought of his abuelo, three thousand miles and a few months from death; of the silver dollars shining in the Rec Park dirt; of a girl's laugh on a pier. He did not weep — he had business to attend to, and the weeping could wait — but he let the whole impossible distance of it settle into him a moment, and then he went down to the clubhouse and found his locker, the dead boy's locker, and hung up his glove, and got to work.

### Chapter — The Oldest Rookie

He reported to Boston in the spring of a year the whole country was trying not to look at directly. The war had been on long enough to have eaten the part of every town that did the dying, and *work or fight* had boiled the rosters down to a thin gruel of old men and boys and the few young stars the army had not yet reached. Ed Barrow, who managed the Red Sox the way a man drives a wagon down a grade with the brake smoking, took one look at the thirty-six-year-old spitballer he had bought for the price of a train ticket, said "God help me, I'm starting graybeards" more to himself than to anyone, and put him in the rotation — because there was no one else, and because the graybeard could still make a baseball die in the air a foot in front of the plate.

The clubhouse was a wonder and a loneliness both. There was a boy in it named Ruth, enormous and loud and twenty-three, eating as though food were about to be rationed — it was — and hitting baseballs distances that made grown sportswriters revise their religion. Ruth liked Cirilo in the careless way the very young like the very old, and drifted down between his own starts to watch the spitball drop, shaking his big head. "They oughta outlaw that," he said once, grinning. "They're going to," Cirilo said, and Ruth laughed like it was a joke and not a clock.

And there was Hooper. Harry Hooper, the gentleman of the outfield, who had grown up one county over from the Beachcombers on Soquel ground, and who had carried Cirilo's name in his pocket for years before he finally said it to the right man. They did not talk much about Santa Cruz. They did not have to. Twice a week, two boys off the same sliver of the same coast looked at each other across a major-league clubhouse three thousand miles from it, and that was conversation enough.

What the city could not do was say his name. The Boston papers tried for about a week and then gave up and made him a new one, the way the country had been making his people new names for seventy years — too foreign to be spoken, so corrected into something the tongue could hold. They called him El Cid: a Spanish conqueror, for a Mexican tanner's son, the joke of it so total that no one in the bleachers ever heard it.

The man who hung the name on him was a sportswriter named Danny Halloran, of the *Post,* and he was not a cruel man — which is the only kind of man who could have done it so thoroughly without once noticing he had. Halloran was Irish, two generations off the boat and the *No Irish Need Apply* cards, close enough to the bottom to remember the smell of it; and he had clawed his way up to a press-box seat and a byline and a barstool where the other writers now bought *him* the first drink, and he had done it, in part, the way the recently-despised so often do — by finding the next man down the ladder and setting a boot on his head to climb. He genuinely loved the way Cirilo pitched; he said so, in print, in prose that soared. He simply could not be troubled to spell *Cirilo Méndez* twice in one week, and "El Cid" scanned, and conjured a cheap flash of Spanish romance that sold papers; and if some part of Halloran knew that turning a Mexican into a Spaniard was a small daily act of erasure — the very act the courthouse had worked on the boy's grandfather in a slower key — he kept that knowledge where he kept the rest of what he knew about himself, which was below the waterline, where a working newspaperman with a mortgage does not go looking. He would write Cirilo's obituary, too, that October, and it would be the finest thing he ever published, and he would weep composing it, and he would spell the name right that once — at the very end, when it could no longer do its owner the slightest good.

On the days he pitched, Fenway Park stood and chanted the name of a man eight centuries dead and a country he had never been let belong to, and Cirilo took the ball and did not correct them, because he had learned the first hard law of his life at the bottom of the bushes and never unlearned it: you do not own your name any more than you own your arm. They will call you what they please. The only answer is the pitch.

### Chapter — Halloran

Danny Halloran of the *Boston Post* gave Cirilo Méndez the name that made him and erased him in the same four letters, and he did it — like most of the damage in this book — not out of malice but out of a deadline.

Halloran was a decent newspaperman of the rococo school, an Irishman two generations off the boat who had clawed up out of the same harbors of contempt that had drowned better men than him — *No Irish Need Apply* was a phrase his own father had read in shop windows — and who had, with the short memory of the recently admitted, pulled the ladder up behind him and learned to look down at the browner, newer, lower men the way the Brahmins had once looked down at him. He liked Cirilo. That was the trouble with him, the thing that made him a man and not a cartoon: he genuinely liked the quiet old rookie with the disappearing ball, admired the arm, was moved despite himself by the lateness of it all. And on a September night, on deadline, needing something with music in it to sit atop a column about a thirty-six-year-old Mexican shutting out the league, he reached — the way a tired man reaches for the nearest bright thing — and pulled down *El Cid.*

He knew what it did. That was the part he buried. He knew, being a literate man, that the Cid was a *Spanish* hero, Castilian, a Christian sword raised against the Moors — that to call a Mexican by that name was to perform, in two syllables, the exact erasure the whole country was already performing on the man: to reach past the brown, past the Indian, past the conquered Mexico of his actual blood, and hand him instead a clean white Spanish crown — a conqueror's name for a conquered man, the fog made flesh in a headline. *Spanish,* not Mexican. *El Cid,* not Cirilo. He knew it scanned because it lied, and he ran it anyway, because it was beautiful and it was late and the desk was screaming for the page; and it caught, the way the easy lie always catches, and ran in every paper in the league inside a week, and became the name the country agreed to love him by — on the strict and unspoken condition that it was not quite his.

There is a coda to Halloran that he never knew was a coda. Years on, an old man, he wrote a column about the great forgotten players, and put Méndez in it, and wrote — meaning it as the highest tribute he had — *we called him El Cid, and he carried it like a king.* And he never once saw the thing the line confessed: that *we called him,* that the naming had been theirs and not his; that a man who is carried like a king under a name not his own is not a king but a hostage, dressed in the crown of the people who took his country and applauded for how well the captured wear the colors of the captor. The honest words were right there, on the same page, in the same hand — and the man who wrote them went to his grave never reading them. Which is the most ordinary tragedy there is, and the one this whole book is finally about: not the men who hate, who are few, but the men who like you, and mean well, and erase you on deadline, and never once learn the language in which the thing they did was a wound.

### Chapter — Work or Fight

He owed his whole season to the war, and the war made him pay for it in the only coin a man like Cirilo had ever been let keep, which was his conscience.

In the spring of 1918 the Provost Marshal handed down the order the newspapers called *Work or Fight:* every man of draft age in a nonessential occupation must take up war work or take up a rifle — and baseball, it was ruled, to the genuine shock of men who had believed themselves national treasures, was nonessential. The young ones went. They went all spring and all summer, off the rosters and onto the troopships, and the clubs scrambled to fill the holes with whatever they could find: old men and boys and 4-Fs, and the occasional thirty-six-year-old Mexican spitballer the East had spent sixteen years declining to call. Cirilo had prayed his whole life for the door to open. It had opened, finally, because a generation of young men were being fed into the guns of France, and their empty lockers were his locker now, and he dressed in front of one every day and could not always meet his own eyes while he did it.

There was a kid — there is always a kid — a nineteen-year-old infielder named Pohlman, up from the bushes to fill a hole, who idolized Cirilo with the pure uncomplicated worship of the young for the old and good, and who got his draft notice in July and came to say goodbye to the old man before he shipped, shy and proud and terrified under the brave face, and asked Cirilo — because he had no father living, and Cirilo was the nearest thing the clubhouse offered — whether he thought a fellow ought to be scared. And Cirilo, who had been scared every day of his life and had learned the one thing there is to learn about it, told him the truth: *Yes. Be scared. Scared is just your body telling you the thing you're about to do matters. The brave ones are not the ones who don't feel it. The brave ones are the ones who feel it and go anyway. You've been brave since the notice came, son. The going is only the last part.* Pohlman went, and was dead in the Argonne by October — the same month the flu took Cirilo, the two of them carried off in the same autumn by the same enormous indifferent thing, the boy by its iron and the man by its breath — and Cirilo never knew it, because he died first, by a few days; and that was perhaps the only mercy in any of it, that he went to his grave still believing the kid he had blessed was somewhere alive in France, scared and brave and going anyway, exactly as he had told him to be.

He thought about it all season, under the cheering. Every game he won was a game some younger and whiter and luckier man was not at home to win, because that man was in a trench. He took the season anyway — *you take it anyway,* the family creed, the only creed they had — but he took it the way he took everything at the end, with the gratitude and the shame so tangled he had long since stopped trying to pull them apart, and had learned instead simply to carry them both, one in each hand, like a man carrying water across a long dry country: spilling a little of each with every step, and arriving, always, with just enough.

### Chapter — The Summer of the Dying Ball

He was, that summer, the best he had ever been — which is a cruel thing to become at thirty-six, in a body that has trimmed hides all winter to make the rent.

He pitched the way the old man had taught him on an overturned bucket behind the Beach Flats backstop a lifetime before, and the lessons came up out of his hands without his having to call them. He read a man's feet before he read his swing: the deep stander buying time, busted up and in where his fear could not follow; the plate-crowder who could not reach his own fists, tied up inside until he came apart. He watched their hands on the bat — the white-knuckle sluggers squeezing the sawdust out of the wood, to whom he fed the slow wet one and let their own greed swing for them; the loose, patient hands he could not fool and so out-thought instead, never showing a pair of eyes the same height twice, high and hard and then slow and low until the eyes loaded for the one and the other was already gone.

And the spitball — the dying ball, the wet one his abuelo had pressed on him with a warning folded inside it — fell off the table that whole last legal summer like a thing that had given up on flight. The American League, which would outlaw the pitch forever in two more years, had no answer for an old man who threw it with a young man's arm and a cardsharp's mind. He led them all. The most wins. The fewest runs to cross the plate behind him. By the early, war-shortened end of the season, the perennial almosts of the country had themselves a pennant, and the man who had been second his entire life stood, for the first time, one short series from first at the only thing the whole bleeding country had stopped to watch.

### Chapter — A Tuesday in July

What the record book will not tell you — what no record book ever tells you — is what it actually *felt* like, that summer, to watch Cirilo Méndez pitch a game. So here is one: an ordinary Tuesday in July, Boston against Detroit, ten thousand in the stands, the war news bad and the heat coming up off the infield in sheets.

He did not look like much warming up; he never had. A spare, gray-templed man of thirty-six with a tannery stoop he had never entirely lost, throwing easy on the side to a kid catcher who plainly could not believe he had drawn the assignment of handling the old Mexican. But Ruth wandered over to watch — Ruth always wandered over — and Hooper drifted in off the outfield grass, and by the time the first Detroit hitter dug in there were three future Hall of Famers and a dying man on a baseball field, and only one of them yet knew which was which.

He worked the way the old man had taught him on a bucket behind the Beach Flats backstop a quarter-century before, and anyone who knew what to look for could read the whole catechism in it. The leadoff man stood deep in the box, back near the catcher, buying himself time — so Cirilo busted him up and in with the fastball where the fear lived and had him jamming a weak roller to short before he had taken a full breath. The second man crowded the plate, so Cirilo tied him in a knot inside and then showed him the wet one — the dying ball, the spitter, legal for one more summer — and the man swung over the top of it by half a foot. The cleanup man was the dangerous one, a big free-swinger with his hands choked white around the bat, and Cirilo looked at those white knuckles and gave him nothing, nothing, three pitches off the plate that all looked exactly like strikes leaving his hand and all died somewhere in the last ten feet; and the big man fished at the third one and dragged it foul off his own shin and cursed, and then took the real strike three straight down the middle while he was still busy hating the spitter — because Cirilo had shown him the same eye level twice and then, the once that mattered, did not.

In the seventh, with the dying ball doing its work and the Detroit bench gone quiet and ugly, the bleachers began it — low at first, then all together, the way a congregation finds a hymn — *EL CID, EL CID, EL CID* — ten thousand Boston throats chanting the name of an eight-hundred-year-dead Castilian at a tanner's son from Santa Cruz, not one of them hearing the joke folded inside it. And Cirilo stood on the mound in the hammering July sun, taking the ball back from his catcher, not correcting them, his face giving away nothing — while somewhere under the iron of him a boy who had once swept these same strangers' planks for nickels stood very still and let the sound break over him and thought, with a clarity that frightened him: *they are saying a name. It is not my name. But they are on their feet, and they are saying a name, and I made them do it. It is the closest they will ever come, and it is not nothing, and God forgive me, after all these years, I will take it.*

He struck out the side in the eighth. Ruth, in the dugout, told anyone who would listen that the old man threw a ball that committed suicide on its way to the plate. It was, the *Post* said the next morning under Halloran's byline, a clinic — and they spelled the invented name correctly and the real one not at all. Cirilo clipped it out anyway and kept it, because his son would want it someday, and because a man takes what he is given, and is grateful, and hates that he is grateful, and is grateful still. Both things. Always both things, all the way to the end.

### Chapter — Father Fagan

Father Aloysius Fagan was an old man in 1918, retired from the coaching he had loved and confessed in equal measure, living out his years in the Jesuit house at Santa Clara with his books and his guilt and his two unreconciled appetites gone quiet at last; and one October morning he opened the newspaper and read that the pitcher the East had taken to calling El Cid — the Boston rookie, the wartime sensation, the thirty-six-year-old who had carried the Red Sox to a championship — had died of the influenza, and that his name, the paper noted in passing, in the small type at the end, had really been Cirilo Méndez.

Fagan set the paper down and sat a long time in the cold light of the cloister.

He had brought the boy to Santa Clara. He had held the door. He had told himself, for forty years, the thing all the door-holders tell themselves — that he had done the boy a kindness, given him a chance, opened a way — and it had even been true, as far as it went. But he had also, he made himself say now, in the plain merciless light an old man finally turns on his own life, brought a brown boy into a house that would use his arm to beat Stanford every spring and never once truly seat him at its table; had held the door to a country that would let the boy climb exactly high enough to be useful and then rename him, at the very summit, because his own name was too much trouble; had been, in his own small comfortable way, one more decent man holding one more door in the long corridor of decent men holding doors, down which the boy had walked his whole hard life looking for a single room that would let him simply *in,* as himself, and had found, at the end of it, only the grave.

He said the Mass for Cirilo Méndez himself, alone, in the small chapel, very early, before the house was up. He said it in Latin — the language he had taught the boy to construe, the language the boy had once turned, brilliantly and on the spot, into a weapon against a cruel professor — and at the part where the priest prays for the dead, Fagan departed from the Latin and said, in plain English, into the empty chapel, the thing he had never had the courage to say to the boy while he lived, the confession he had always owed him and always swallowed: *I am sorry, son. I am sorry I could only open the door. I am sorry I never once thought to ask why the room on the other side of it was built the way it was. You deserved better than the men who loved you, and I was one of them, and that is the worst thing I know about my own life, and I am too old now to do anything about it but say it — to a God who let it happen, and an empty room where you cannot hear me. Go with Him. He, at least, will say your name right.*

And then the old priest, who had spent a lifetime confessing the wrong sins — the small, comfortable ones — finally confessed the large one to the only one who could absolve it, and was not absolved, because some things are not ours to be absolved of; they are only ours to carry. He carried it the little while he had left, and died the next spring, and was buried in the Jesuit plot among the others who had held the doors, under his own correctly-spelled name — in a country that had always, always been willing to say his.

### Chapter — The Babe and the Old Man

Ruth could not leave him alone, and Cirilo never quite worked out why — except that the very young are sometimes drawn to the very old in a clubhouse the way they are drawn nowhere else in life, because a ballfield is the one place on earth where a man of twenty-three and a man of thirty-six are, briefly, the same age.

George Herman Ruth was, that summer, becoming the thing he would be for the rest of the century: the biggest man in the game, the biggest in the country soon, a force of nature with a child's appetite and a child's heart and a left arm and a bat that between them were quietly ending the dead-ball era single-handed. He was loud where Cirilo was silent, enormous where Cirilo was spare, careless of money and women and food and his own gift in a way the careful old Mexican — who had husbanded every ounce of his across sixteen lean years — found by turns appalling and wonderful. They should have had nothing to say to each other. They talked, instead, for hours: Ruth doing most of it, sprawled on a clubhouse bench eating somebody else's sandwich, telling stories about the Baltimore reform school where the Brothers had raised him and handed him the game, the only home he had ever really had; Cirilo listening, because he had learned across a lifetime that you find out everything worth knowing by listening, and because there was something in the big careless boy he recognized and could not at first name.

He named it, finally, one night in September. Ruth — who had caught the influenza in the spring and shrugged it off the way he shrugged off everything — had caught it again, the second time hard, sweating and gray in a hotel bed; and Cirilo, who already knew in his own bones what was coming for the city though he had not yet let himself say it, sat with him a while and laid a cool cloth on the great hot forehead, and understood at last the thing he had recognized. Ruth, too, was a man the world had thrown away once — an orphan, a reform-school case, a boy nobody wanted — who had clawed up out of it on nothing but a gift. They had the same story. The only difference between them, the whole difference, the difference that would make one of them immortal and bury the other under a borrowed name, was the single accident of which of them had been born the color the country had decided to forgive. *Get well, Jidge,* Cirilo said, using the clubhouse name; and Ruth grinned up at him gray and said *I always do, pop* — and he did; he got well twice that year and lived to be a god. Cirilo caught it once, in October, off a crowd cheering a name that was not his, and did not. There is no moral in that. Cirilo looked for one the rest of his short life and never found it. Some men the wheel forgives and some it does not, and the forgiving has nothing to do with the man — and that, not the flu and not the conquest and not the curse, is the hardest thing in the whole story to hold in your hand without flinching.

### Chapter — Comiskey

The strangest World Series anyone had ever seen opened in September — September, weeks early, the season guillotined by the war — and not even in the Cubs' own park, but in Comiskey, borrowed across town because it held more bodies, though in the end the bodies did not come. The country was too busy dying in France and in the troop camps to give its whole heart to a ballgame, and the stands at the first Series Cirilo Méndez ever pitched in held perhaps half the roar he had been promised, his whole life, the big time would be.

It opened with Ruth. Of course it opened with Ruth — the boy pitched the first game and threw a shutout, nine innings of the kind of thing that made men who had watched baseball for forty years go quiet — and Cirilo watched from the bench and felt the complicated thing he always felt watching the Babe: the pure clean joy of seeing the game played at the absolute edge of what a body can do, laid over the old buried ache of knowing that the boy doing it would be remembered for a hundred years, and the old man watching would be remembered, if at all, under a name a sportswriter had invented because his own was too much trouble. Both things. He had stopped, by then, expecting the two feelings to resolve. He had a locker and a uniform and a turn in the rotation coming, and that was more than the man in the derby had ever meant him to have, and he meant to use it.

It was a subdued and shadowed Series, played under the shadow of the thing no one would name yet — the players bound for the army, the flag draped over everything. In the seventh-inning stretch of the opener the band struck up a tune, a wartime favorite called "The Star-Spangled Banner," and the crowd, almost without meaning to, rose for it — the first time anyone present could remember a whole ballpark standing for that song; and Cirilo stood on the top step with his cap over his heart and sang the words he had been taught in a schoolroom that did not want him and on a boardwalk that swept him out at season's end, and meant them, in the complicated way he meant everything about this country: meant them as a wish more than a fact, the way the powerless always mean an anthem — singing not the country that was, but the one he had decided, against all the evidence of his life, to keep believing was still somehow possible. Then he put his cap back on, and waited his turn. It was coming. It had been sixteen years coming. And he was, at last, going to take it.

### Chapter — A Man in a Good Coat

Chuck Ashby came to the Series the way he came to everything: in the best seat money could rent, with a wife on his arm who was poised and handsome and was not Rebecca.

He had done, by 1918, exactly what he had always been going to do. He had inherited the chair at the club and the name on the building and the trick of regarding the world as a thing arranged for his convenience. He had not thought about the bottle boy in years — not really, not in any way that cost him sleep — so it was a peculiar shock to lift his field glasses over the rail of his box and find, on the highest mound in the country, the beach attendant. EL CID, the bunting said. And in the cheaper seats, in a plain coat with a baby on her hip, the only woman who had ever looked at Chuck Ashby and priced him correctly.

His wife asked whether they were friends of his. "I knew them," he said, a beat too long. "A long time ago."

Then he sat, in the most expensive seat in the park, and was given a clear and unobstructed and entirely unwanted view of the one thing his money had never been able to buy: a woman who'd had every other choice in the world, on her feet, weeping, screaming a Mexican's invented name, in love in the open with a dying man who had nothing and would, by Christmas, have less. The whole park was standing. Chuck Ashby did not stand. He had finally caught up to the bottle boy, and it had cost him eighteen years and a fortune to arrive at the precise spot from which he could watch — and not look away from — everything he had won instead of the thing that mattered.

And beside him his wife watched him watch, and understood all of it — because Eleanor Ashby, née Vane, married eleven years to the chair at the club, was nobody's fool and never had been. She had known about the other woman since before the wedding; you did not marry into a thing like the Ashbys without first reading the whole ledger, and Rebecca Whitfield's name had been an entry in it, in the column of the permanently unfinished. Eleanor had married Chuck with her eyes open, traded one loneliness for a comfortable and well-appointed version of itself, and spent eleven years in the gilded quiet learning the one lesson the cage teaches its long-term residents: that the people who win the way her husband had won are very often the emptiest people in any room, and that they know it, in the small hours, and that the knowing is the whole of their punishment and is never enough. She looked at the dying brown man on the mound and the plain-coated woman weeping for him in the cheap seats — two people with nothing who had somehow contrived to have everything — and felt no jealousy at all, only a clean, cold, almost scientific recognition, the kind one prisoner feels watching another prisoner pardoned. *So that is what it looks like,* she thought. *I had always wondered.* And she laid her gloved hand over her husband's on the rail — not to comfort him, for she did not especially love him and he had never given her cause to, but out of a strange fellow-feeling, one lifer to another. Chuck, who did not understand the gesture and never would, let it lie there; and the two of them sat in the best seats in the house and did not stand, and watched.

### Chapter — The Strike

There was an hour, in that World Series, that the record books note in a single sentence and the country forgot inside a week, and that Cirilo Méndez never forgot at all — because it was the one hour of his life he watched other men discover the thing he had known since the bushes: that they did not own themselves. And watched them try, just once, to do something about it.

It came out, between games, that the owners had quietly cut the players' share of the Series money to a fraction of what the men had been promised — diverted the gate to themselves and to a list of other clubs by a feat of accounting no player had been consulted on or could contest, the reserve clause being, as ever, a one-way street. And the players of both clubs, the Red Sox and the Cubs, broke and bound for the war and playing the first World Series anyone could remember for something close to nothing, did a thing that had never been done: they refused to take the field. They sat in the clubhouse under Comiskey with the crowd filling the stands above them and the band playing and the officials going purple, and for one hour the most powerless men in American labor — owned men, sold men, men whose contracts were traded like hides — held in their dirty hands a sliver of actual leverage: the only leverage labor has ever really had, which is the simple refusal to perform.

They lost, of course. They were always going to lose; men with families and no savings and the draft breathing on them cannot hold out long against men who have everything. But the *way* they were made to lose was the thing Cirilo carried. The owners did not negotiate. They sent in a former player, an official now, to stand in the clubhouse doorway and remind the men — gently, regretfully, with the flag all but audible behind him — that there were wounded soldiers in the stands, that the country was at war, that the public would never forgive ballplayers who struck for money while boys were dying in France. And he was right, and the men knew he was right, and they filed out and played, for the cut wages, having been beaten not with a club but with a flag — which is how the men who own things in this country have always preferred to win. Cirilo dressed in silence and went out and pitched. He understood the doorway and the flag better than anyone in that room. He had been managed by that exact instrument his whole life — *be patient, be grateful, do not make trouble, think how far you have come, think of the others worse off than you* — the soft, suffocating decency that asks the powerless to be the ones who keep the peace, always, at their own expense, forever. He threw a shutout. The owners kept the money. And somewhere in him the small cold clarity he had carried since a man in a derby decided his life in a single sentence turned over once more and settled, and he thought: *they will let us win the game. They will never, ever, let us win the argument. Remember it, on the chance you ever forget: they will hand you the trophy a hundred times before they will hand you the receipt.*

### Chapter — The Fever Game

The fever came up in the third inning of the last game, and he told no one.

He had felt it gathering for a day: the ache behind the eyes, the cold that lived under the skin and would not be warmed. He had looked at the calendar and the standings and the sixteen years behind him and decided, with the flat cold certainty of a man who has waited too long to be careful, that it could wait one more afternoon. The thing already had a name in the camps; it would have one in the city within the week. On the field that day it was only a chill, and a pitcher the color of the ball, and thirty thousand people who did not yet know they were sitting in the middle of the thing that would kill more of them than the war.

The middle innings he pitched in a kind of fever-trance he would never afterward be able to fully describe. The heat of the day and the heat under his skin had merged into a single furnace, and the ball came back from the catcher and went out again from his hand, and the world narrowed to that one line, sixty feet and six inches long, the roaring stands gone to a far-off sea-sound at the edges of it. He did not have his best stuff; he had something stranger, and for one afternoon better — a total economy, a man with nothing left to spend spending exactly enough and not one ounce more, reading the hitters now not with his quick young eyes, which were going to water and blur, but with twenty years of pure knowing, throwing on memory and faith. Twice between innings he sat on the bench and shook, and Hooper draped a towel over his shoulders and looked at him hard and said nothing — because Hooper, the decent man, understood that there are things you do not take from a man even to save him, and that this was one of them, and hated that he understood it. The bleachers chanted the wrong name. And Cirilo took the ball each inning and walked back out into the furnace and pitched, because he had been second his whole life and he could feel, the way you feel weather coming, that *first* was finally close enough to touch — and a man does not sit down sixty feet from the only thing he ever wanted, not for a fever, not for anything, not even when sitting down is the very thing that might save his life. Especially not then.

By the ninth he could barely feel the seams. Two out, the tying run aboard, a full count — one strike from the first championship of his life and his team's. Barrow came halfway to the mound, read the catcher's face and then Cirilo's, and stopped where he stood, because some things a manager is not permitted to take from a man.

"You're done, Mendez," he said anyway. "You're white as the ball. Let me bring the kid."

Cirilo shook his head. Once. "I've been second my whole life, skip," he said. "Not today."

Barrow looked at him a long moment — the kind of look a man spends the next forty years trying to forget he gave — and put the ball back in his hand and walked away.

In the box, Rebecca had their son up on her hip — alone with the baby, three thousand miles from anyone of Cirilo's blood, for the abuelo was far too frail now to have crossed the country and sat that very hour, Cirilo knew, in his good chair in Santa Cruz, the family law going round in him like a rosary: *we are not allowed to be first.* Had the old man been in these stands he would not have been watching the batter. He would have been watching his grandson's hands, and he would have been afraid of them.

Cirilo looked in. The stitched leather, an old friend. He did not wind up big; he never had. He threw the best thing he owned, and the world went silent the way it only does in memory, and the ball died in the air a foot in front of the plate, and the bat went through the place it had been.

Strike three.

And the park came apart. They reached him at the mound the way the sea reaches a rock and lifted him up — the tannery boy, the bottle boy, the man with the wrong name in the papers — up onto their shoulders and over the whole roaring country, a champion, *first*, for the first time in his life and the first time in his family's stolen century. He found Rebecca in the noise and held the ball up toward the boy like a man showing a coin he had crossed an ocean to earn. And no one in that roaring park knew — not Rebecca, weeping, holding the baby up to see — that the only soul alive who had foreseen this exact afternoon was an old man in a good chair three thousand miles west, who would have closed his eyes rather than watch it, because he alone understood what being first had always cost this family, and he had been right to.

### Chapter — What the Crowd Carried

They carried him through the streets that night, and the streets killed him.

It was the first time the city of Boston had ever opened its arms to a Méndez, and it did the thing wholeheartedly, the way a city does when it has decided you are briefly its own: forty thousand people shoulder to shoulder in the wet autumn dark, breathing the same air, passing the champion overhead hand to hand, his son held up over the crowd in a knit cap. For one night Cirilo Méndez was not second to any man in America, and the whole town loved him out loud, and the whole town was already sick and did not know it.

That was the cruelty the century had saved for him, and it was a tidy one: the embrace was the infection. The same packed, joyful, unmasked crowd that finally lifted the brown man onto its shoulders was the breath that carried the thing through the city in the days that followed — the camps already overflowing, Devens forty miles off counting its dead by the hundred, October closing its hand over the country until it became, before it was through, the deadliest month the nation would ever know. They had spent seventy years refusing to embrace his people, and the one time they did, in the one city, on the one night, it was the embrace that did what the refusal never had.

He went down four days later. He had survived the bushes and the reserve clause and the man in the derby and sixteen years of being told no, and the air of a celebration thrown in his own honor took him in under a week.

### Chapter — The Room

The influenza did in four days what sixteen years of *no* had not managed.

The flat was small and clean and full of the wrong quiet. On the sill the championship ball sat beside a guttering candle and the rosary that had come all the way from the beginning of the family, from a chapel on land they no longer owned. Rebecca sat at the edge of the bed, dry-eyed past the far side of crying, their son asleep against her. In the corner Aoife Doyle's lips moved over the beads and did not stop — the old Irish widow keeping the vigil that the family three thousand miles west could not.

Cirilo was a long way off by then, but his hand found the old glove on the blanket — his father's leather, his great-grandfather's tanning, the one thing four generations had carried through every theft — and he turned it once in his fingers, and then he did not give it to Rebecca. He folded the small fingers of his son around it instead.

"Abuelo was right," he said, and they had to lean close to hear it. "There's a price. For us. Being first." The ghost of the old smile. "Worth it. Tell him — when you take me home, tell the old man — it was worth it."

She would carry that message west, and she would stand at a graveside in Santa Cruz and hear the old man's broken answer to it; but here, now, she did not spend her husband's last clear minutes on a reply he could no longer keep. What she gave him instead was the thing she had carried up out of her grief on purpose, the way she had once carried his name up out of her labor, to give him in time. "You weren't second," she said into the quiet. "Not for one day of your life."

He heard that. And his hand went still on his son's hand on the glove. And Aoife did not stop praying; and Rebecca, through her tears, was looking at a Méndez's hands. Small ones now. Already strong.

### Chapter — Siempre Primero

They took him home to Santa Cruz, because Rebecca understood that a man belongs to the ground that made him even when that ground was stolen, and she carried him across the whole width of the country he had never quite been allowed to belong to and gave him back to it.

And the town came. That was the thing none of them had thought to expect. The tannery shut its gates for the afternoon — the first time in its history it had shut them for a Mexican — and Esteban's old crew stood at the grave with their cracked, stained, leather-colored hands folded in front of them. The fishermen came up from the wharf. The families came down out of the flats and in from the orchards, the people who spoke the language only behind closed doors, and they stood in the open October light and spoke it — because the boy in the box had made it so that for one season the whole country had been forced to learn to say a name like theirs, even the wrong, invented, conqueror's version of it, and to say it with its hat in its hands.

Don Aurelio stood at the edge of land that had once run from the river to the redwoods, all of it theirs and none of it theirs, and understood at last that he had read the family's curse exactly backward for ninety years.

Because a trophy is a cup in a glass case in a city three thousand miles away, and the men who carried Cirilo Méndez on their shoulders would be old men and then no men, and the cup would gather the indifferent dust of strangers. That first had been real, and glorious, and borrowed, and brief — the way being first in their world always is. If it had been the only first the boy ever reached, then the curse was true and it had won, and the old man could have lain down beside his grandson and let the century have the rest of them.

But there is another kind of first, and it does not gather dust. In the summers that came after, the boys of the flats played ball on the flat ground by the river where the rancho's cattle had once come down to drink, and when one of them threw a pitch nobody could touch, the others would go quiet, and then somebody would say it — *throws like Méndez* — and not one of them had ever seen the man pitch. They knew only the story: that one of theirs, a tannery son off this exact dirt, had stood on the highest mound in the country and been *first*, and had kept working when the answer was no for sixteen unbroken years, and that the working had been the man, and not the cup. The town that had spent a century trying to forget the family found, now, that it could not stop saying the name.

They had hung *El Cid* on him as a joke and an erasure — a Mexican made over into a Spanish conqueror because the papers could not bring themselves to let him be a hero as himself — and they had been more right than they could have known. In the old legend the Cid's own body, lashed upright in the saddle, had led one last charge and won a battle after his death. And so, in the only way that ever lasts, did Cirilo Méndez.

The curse, the old man finally saw, had never been that the Méndez could not be first. It was that they had believed they could not. His grandson had walked out into a country that had spent a hundred years telling him to stay second and had simply, stubbornly, *declined* — and the proof of it was sleeping now in a Brooklyn cradle three thousand miles east, a worn glove folded under his small strong hands, the leather his great-great-grandfather had tanned on land they once owned, soft now from a fifth generation of use.

He was the root. He was the legacy. He was the first that lasts.

And that — not the cup, not the autumn, not the borrowed conqueror's name — was the whole of *Siempre Primero,* which means *always first,* and which the family had had backward, like the curse, for a hundred years: that you are not made first by the thing you win at the end. You are made first by the way you keep walking toward it — second, and second, and second, every single day they tell you no.

The last thing is the boy's hands closing around the ball.

Already big.

Already strong.

### Chapter — The Widow

Rebecca Whitfield Méndez was thirty-six when she was widowed, and she lived to be eighty-one, and she never married again — not out of faithfulness to a memory (she would have told you, briskly, that she had no patience for women who made shrines of their dead husbands) but because she had already had the thing, the real thing, the being-known, and having had it she could not work up the appetite to settle for the ordinary article, the way a person who has once eaten at a great table cannot afterward take the boardinghouse fare quite seriously, however hungry.

She raised the boy alone, in California, near the Méndez, who took the Anglo widow and her half-Méndez son fully in now, in a way they had been too wary to manage while Cirilo lived — grief having burned off the last of the suspicion, as grief sometimes will. Dolores taught her the rest of the cooking and the whole of the language, and the two women — the illiterate ranchera's daughter and the disinherited heiress, who had begun a continent and a class apart — ended as something English has no clean word for and Spanish does: *comadres,* co-mothers, bound by a dead man and a living boy and the long daily work of keeping a family from disappearing. Rebecca read the telegram to Joaquín every New Year's. She kept the glove oiled. She told the boy the story — all of it, the rancho and the booth and the dying ball and the fever — not as a tragedy, which is how the world would have told it, but as what it was: the account of a man handed a curse and a country that did not want him and a single short season of grace, who had, with all of that, refused, every single day of his life, to be less than he was. *Your father was not a sad story,* she would tell the boy, fierce, when he came home from a schoolyard where some other child had tried to make him one. *Do not let them make him a sad story. Sad is what they wanted. He would not give it to them, and neither will you.*

She had her own griefs and kept them by the Dolores method: a tended hour and not one inch more. She missed him at the strange times — not the anniversaries and the empty side of the bed, but the small ones, a turn of phrase she wanted to tell him, a thing the boy did with his hands. She lived through the boy's growing and the second war and the slow turning of the century, clear-eyed to the end; and she had understood, long before the radio said *Robinson* in 1947, the thing it would take the country another half-century even to begin to learn — that the people it threw away were never its waste but its conscience, and that everything it would one day be proud of having become, it had been dragged to, kicking, by the very ones it had ordered to wait. She had married one of the ones who would not wait. She had never been sorry. Not for one day of her life.

### Chapter — The Two Mothers

The two women went up the hill together, the first Sunday after they buried him, and that was where the friendship that had begun over a ruined pot of beans became the thing that carried them both the rest of their lives.

Dolores had been doing this for thirty years — the climb, the weeds, the tending of the ones the world wanted disappeared — and she did not slow her pace for the younger woman, because she had decided, without discussing it, that the way to save Rebecca was not to coddle her grief but to put a tool in her hands. So she handed the heiress — the Stanford woman, the disinherited daughter of a banker — a pair of work gloves and a hand trowel, and set her to clearing the grass from around a new grave beside two old small ones; and they worked in silence in the wind off the bay a while, two widows of a kind, for Dolores had been widowing her sons to that hill for thirty years and was an expert in the thing Rebecca was only beginning.

*You will want to make it a shrine,* Dolores said at last, in the slow careful English she used for the things that mattered most, the English she had learned late and wore like a second good dress. *Anglos make shrines. You will want to come up here and weep and leave flowers and feel the grief like a fire that warms you. Do not.* Rebecca looked up, stung, and Dolores met the look without softening. *I buried two before him. I know the fire. The fire feels like love and it is not love; it is the thing that eats the ones who are left. This* — she gestured at the trowel, the cleared grass, the plain tended ground — *this is love. Not the feeling. The tending. You come up, you pull the weeds, you say the names, and then you go back down and you feed the boy and you live. The feeling you give one hour, here — and then you put it down and walk back to the living, because the living are still here and they need a mother whose hands do not shake. That is the whole of it. That is everything I know. My mother taught it to me and her mother to her, all the way back to women whose names even I have lost; and now I am teaching it to an Anglo, which God in His humor never warned me I would do — and you will teach it to that boy's wife someday, whoever she is, and that is how we do not disappear.*

And Rebecca Whitfield, who had been raised to believe that grief was a feeling you displayed and love a transaction you negotiated, knelt in the California dirt beside her mother-in-law and learned, in her thirty-seventh year, the actual shape of both. The two of them, the ranchera's daughter and the banker's, finished clearing the three graves together, and said the three names aloud into the wind — Inés, and Joaquín, and Cirilo — and then walked back down the hill to where Joaquín the younger, the fourth name, the living one, was waiting to be fed. And they fed him. And that, repeated ten thousand times across the decades — the climb and the weeds and the names and the walk back down to the living — was the whole of how the Méndez family, against everything, did not disappear.

### Chapter — The Boy's Hands

Joaquín Méndez was twelve the summer Edward Purse came to watch him pitch, and he did not understand for many years what he had been given that afternoon — which is the way of the most important things given to twelve-year-olds.

The boy had his father's hands. Everyone said so — the few who had known Cirilo, and the many who knew only the story. He had taught himself to pitch with his father's glove, on a sandlot in the California town where Rebecca had settled them, near enough to the Méndez that Dolores could spoil him and far enough that at school he could be merely a quiet boy with an odd name and not the orphan of a legend. He did not throw the dying ball — they had outlawed the spitter the year his father died, 1920, the clock his abuelo had warned of finally run all the way out — but he threw hard and clean and could already, at twelve, make the older boys look foolish, and he loved it the simple way a boy loves the thing he is good at, before the world has explained what it will cost him.

The old Black man came on a Saturday and stood a long time at the fence, alone, in a good worn suit, watching. Rebecca knew him at once — she had read his letters, had kept the one folded in the glove — and she went and stood with him, and the two of them watched the boy throw and did not speak for a while. Then Edward Purse, who was near the end of his life though no one yet knew it, who had a year of breath left and a lifetime of baseball stored behind a wall the country had built and never once let him cross, said, in a voice like dry paper: *He's got his father's hands.* And after a moment: *Your husband threw a ball that died in the air, ma'am. This boy won't — they took that pitch away. But he's got the hands, and he's got the other thing, the thing you can't teach, the thing that lets a man take the world's worst and not swing back stupid. I can see it from here.* And then he gave Rebecca the words she carried the rest of her life and handed to the boy when he was old enough — the words the boy carried his whole life after that, all the way to a radio over a tanning bench in 1947: *Somebody has to make them practice saying the name. His father did his part. This one will do his. It's all one long game, ma'am, and not one of us is going to live to see the end of it, and we play anyway. That's the whole secret. You teach him that. You teach him to play anyway.*

He shook the boy's hand at the fence before he left — the great copperplate hand that had kept eleven years of score books and never once been let onto the grass, closing around the small strong hand that would be allowed, a little, more than its grandfather's had been, because the door was coming off its hinges one slow inch per generation and Joaquín had been born one inch further along than Cirilo. The boy did not know who the old man was. He remembered, all his life, only the dry voice and the firm grip and the words he did not understand at twelve and understood completely at twenty-nine with the radio going: *you play anyway, son.* And then Edward Purse tipped his hat to Rebecca and walked away up the road, a man going home — and was dead inside the year, and buried in Oakland under a stone his ballplayers paid for, and given, by the white world that had spent his whole life pretending he did not exist, exactly the obituary it had always given him, which was none.

### Chapter — Eleanor

Eleanor Vane had been the most sought-after girl of her San Francisco season, and she had chosen Chuck Ashby with her eyes wide open, and she would have told you — had you been the kind of person she trusted, which almost no one was — that it had not been a mistake so much as a transaction she had understood perfectly at the time and had simply outlived her appetite for.

She was clever in a way her world had no use for in a woman and had therefore taught her to hide; she read everything, saw everything, and said almost nothing, having learned early that a clever woman in a gilded house survives by being underestimated and is destroyed by being known. She had married the chair at the club because the alternatives were worse and fewer than people imagined for a girl of her time and class — a woman could be a wife or an aunt, an ornament or a burden — and Eleanor had looked at the menu of her one short life and ordered the least bad thing on it, with full knowledge, and without self-pity, which she despised. She did not love Chuck. She had never pretended to, to herself; the pretending was for the dinners. But she had made him a fair bargain and she kept it, scrupulously, for forty years, which is more than love and rarer: she was loyal to a man she saw through completely, out of a kind of cold honor, because she had given her word in a church, and because breaking it would have meant becoming a person who broke words, and that she would not do — not even for happiness, especially not for happiness, which she had decided early was a thing for other people and beneath her serious attention.

And yet. There was a thing she did, once, that no one ever knew, that did not fit the cold clear ledger of her, that she herself could not entirely explain. Years after the Series — long after the brown man had died and the plain-coated woman had vanished back into whatever life she had chosen — Eleanor Ashby, going through the accounts her husband left entirely to her because the details bored him, came upon the name Méndez in a list: a widow and a boy, in California. And she did a small quiet thing with a portion of money that was hers to move and that Chuck would never miss or notice — an anonymous scholarship, administered through a Catholic charity so that no string led back to her, that paid, for years, for the schooling of a boy named Joaquín. She never met him. She never wrote. She told no one, least of all her husband, whom she had watched, at a ballgame eleven years into their marriage, fail to look away from everything he had not won. She did it, she decided afterward, not out of guilt — that was Chuck's to carry, not hers — and not out of pity, which she found vulgar; but out of something closer to fellow-feeling extended across an impossible distance: one prisoner, holding the keys to the commissary, quietly sending a little something to the cell of a woman who had at least, unlike Eleanor, once been free. *So that is what it looks like,* she had thought, watching Rebecca stand and weep in the cheap seats. She spent the rest of her life unable to forget what it looked like — and once, with a stroke of a pen on an account her husband never read, she had reached across the entire width of the cage, and touched it.

### Chapter — Mr. Ashby's Country

Chuck Ashby got everything, and lived a long time, and that was his whole sentence.

He inherited the bank and the chair at the club and the house on the hill, and he added to all of it, because he was not stupid and the country was built to reward men like him with compound interest. He married Eleanor, who saw him clearly and never once said so, and they had children raised exactly the way he had been raised — measured against an impossible standard and found wanting — so that he passed down, intact, the one thing his own father had truly given him, which was the certainty of not being enough. He served on boards. He gave to the right charities. He had his name carved on a building at the university where a Mexican had once humiliated him in Latin, and he never made the connection, because he had long since filed the bottle boy away in the place where he kept the few memories that did not fit the story of a successful man.

But it would not stay filed. That was the thing Eleanor knew and Chuck would never admit: that the one afternoon of his life he could not get rid of was the World Series afternoon — the brown man on the mound and the woman in the cheap seats on her feet — and that some nights, old now, rich beyond any need, he would surface from sleep with the image of it and lie in the dark and feel, in the place where other men keep their souls, the cold draft of the thing he had spent a fortune and a lifetime never having. He had won. He had won everything there was to win in the only game his father ever taught him to play. And he had watched, once, for nine innings, two people who had lost that game completely and were richer than he would ever be — and he never recovered from it, and never told a living soul, and *that,* not poverty, which he never tasted, not failure, which he never met, was the price the country exacted from Chuck Ashby for being one of the men it was built to reward: that he got everything, and knew, in the watches of the night, that he had bought it with the only thing that turned out to matter, and that the receipt was non-returnable.

He read about Robinson in 1947, too — the same April afternoon Joaquín Méndez set down the tanning knife in Oakland. Chuck read it in his study, in a good chair, an old man with a glass of good whiskey, and felt nothing in particular: a vague distaste, the reflexive resistance of his kind to any door coming open. And then, unbidden, the old image rose — the mound, the cheap seats, the wrong-colored name the whole park was chanting — and Chuck Ashby, who had everything, set down the paper and looked out the window at the bay for a long while, and could not have told you why his eyes stung, and would not have admitted it if he could. He had been on the wrong side of the long game. He had always been on the wrong side of it. And the cruelest thing the country ever did to Chuck Ashby was to let him win, so completely, on the wrong side, that he lived his whole long life with just enough of a soul left to occasionally suspect it.

### Epilogue — *Siempre*

Joaquín Méndez was twenty-nine years old, and a tanner — for the trade had come down to him after all, the way the leather always came down in that family, except that it came to him by choice and not by defeat, in a good union shop in Oakland with his own name spelled right on the time card — when he heard, over the radio on the bench on an April afternoon in 1947, that a man named Jackie Robinson had walked out onto a major-league field in Brooklyn and broken the line.

He set down the knife. He was not a sentimental man; his mother Rebecca, who had raised him alone and fierce and had read him a yellow telegram every New Year's of his childhood, had not raised a sentimental man. But he set down the knife, and he stood in the green reek of the soaking pits that his great-great-grandfather and his great-grandfather and his grandfather had all stood in before him — in their kingship and their defeat and their wage — and he found that his eyes had filled, and he let them, because there was no one watching and because some things have earned it.

He had never seen his father pitch. He had been ten months old. He had only the story, and the glove — his father's glove, his great-great-grandfather's leather, soft now past softness from five generations of hands — and the two papers folded into its lining, which he had found as a boy and read until he had them by heart: a telegram that said *we were not finished,* and a letter in a beautiful copperplate hand from a Mr. Edward Purse of Oakland, California, whom Joaquín had in fact met once — an old Black man at his father's kind of remove, who had come to a sandlot to watch Joaquín play as a boy, and had said, watching, in a voice like dry paper, *your father threw a ball that died in the air. You don't. But you've got his hands, son. God help you, you've got his hands.* And who had told him the one thing he never forgot: *Somebody has to make them practice saying the name. Your father did his part. Brooklyn is going to do the rest — you watch. It is all one long game, and not one of us is going to live to see the end of it, and we play anyway. That is the whole secret. You play anyway.*

Purse had been right. Purse had been sixteen years in the ground, and he had been right. Joaquín stood in the steam in Oakland with the radio going and his father twenty-nine years dead — though it always felt longer, it always would — and he understood the thing the whole long story had been trying to say, the thing his great-grandfather had had backward for ninety years and his grandfather had spent a life proving and his mother had folded into a yellow telegram for him to carry: that you are not made first by reaching the top, because the top is borrowed and brief and they take it back; you are made first by the refusing, the working, the playing-anyway — the standing in the steam and the standing on the mound and the standing in the wrong-colored seats and the standing at the deathbed, *siempre,* always, world without end. The line his father had not been allowed to cross had just been crossed, in his father's own Brooklyn, by another man with the wrong kind of hands; and it had been one long game all along, exactly as the old porter said; and the Méndez family, against ninety years of its own hard evidence and a whole country's determined weight, had turned out, in the only way that finally lasts, to have been first.

He picked the knife back up. There was a hide on the beam and the living still needed shoes and the dead did not, and his mother had taught him — who had it from a woman named Dolores she had loved and buried — that you tend what is given for exactly as long as it is given. That night he went home and took down the glove and oiled the old leather, and told his own son — for there was a son now; there is always, in this family, a son — the story again from the beginning, the way it had been told to him, the way it would be told after him:

*Before this was a country for us, mijo, it was ours. All of it. Listen. Your great-great-great-grandfather signed a paper and wept. And we were not finished. We have never once been finished. Remember the hands.*
