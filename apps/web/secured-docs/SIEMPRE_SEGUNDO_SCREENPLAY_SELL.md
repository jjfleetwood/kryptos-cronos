# SIEMPRE SEGUNDO — Screenplay (Sell Draft)

*Lean, industry-standard spec version of the literary screenplay
(`SIEMPRE_SEGUNDO_SCREENPLAY.md`). Same scenes, beats, and dialogue; action lines
stripped to what's on screen — for agents, contests, and coverage. The literary
draft remains the "read." Loosely based on Frank Arellanes, Santa Cruz's own —
among the first Mexican-American men to reach the major leagues, and the first
Latino to play for the Boston Red Sox.*

```
FADE IN:

EXT. SANTA CRUZ MOUNTAINS -- HIGH RIDGE -- DAWN (PRESENT DAY)

Fog on a wall of redwoods. A RED-TAILED HAWK lifts off the
highest fir and rides the first thermal of the morning.

We go with her.

Below: redwoods, switchback roads, water towers, the sprawl of a
California beach town waking on a June day.

EXT. STEAMER LANE -- THE POINT -- CONTINUOUS

Cold morning surf. A SURFER, 18, drops down a wave, tucks under
the lip -- gone inside the barrel -- and is flung out the end in
spray and light. He whoops. On the cliff, TWO GIRLS watch,
pretending not to.

EXT. THE WHARF -- CONTINUOUS

Sea lions bellow on the pilings. On the wet sand, a FATHER and
his SMALL BOY throw a ball back and forth. The glove is too big
for the boy's hand. The soft THOCK of the catch.

EXT. THE BOARDWALK -- CONTINUOUS

A hundred-year-old wooden boardwalk. The GIANT DIPPER hauls its
first cars up the lift, breasts the summit, and PLUNGES. The
scream goes up. A girl grabs a boy's arm.

EXT. THE MIDWAY -- CONTINUOUS

Carnival games. The BASEBALL THROW -- iron bottles, cloth balls.
The KID working it is sixteen, brown, poor. A knot of richer
BOYS his age stop, don't play, work him over -- the accent, the
shoes. One flicks a balled-up dollar at his chest and laughs
when he flinches to catch it.

The kid keeps his eyes down. Waits it out.

EXT. HARVEY WEST PARK -- FIELD 1 -- CONTINUOUS

A little-league playoff. Fresh lime lines, banners on the
backstop, parents in lawn chairs. On the mound, a BOY toes the
rubber and looks in for the sign.

The hawk slides over it and climbs the river ravine.

EXT. A STORAGE YARD -- A LOW RISE -- CONTINUOUS

Asphalt, roll-up doors, chain-link. One OLD REDWOOD stands over
the lot. The hawk drops, takes the high limb, fixes the gold eye
on the ground below.

CLOSE ON -- THE HAWK'S EYE.

And the world thins. The asphalt, the fence, the doors fade.
SMOKE rises over a cluster of poor houses.

EXT. THE TANNERY FLATS -- A DIRT YARD -- MORNING (1881)

A CHILD, 4, a water bucket on his hip, stops. A shadow crosses
him. He looks up -- and finds the hawk.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
El halcón ha venido a mirarte, mijo. The
hawk has come to look at you. And you
should know what that means.

SMASH TO BLACK.

TITLE: SIEMPRE SEGUNDO
"always second"
```

## PROLOGUE — *California Banknotes*

```
DON AURELIO (V.O.)
Before this was a town for strangers, it
was ours. All of it.

EXT. THE MENDEZ RANCHO -- COASTAL HILLS -- DAY (1840s)

Gold hills, no fences. Cattle move across them like a slow
brown river.

EXT. THE MATANZA -- DAY

Hundreds of cowhides staked to the ground to dry.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
A cowhide was as good as coin. They called
them California banknotes.

INT. RANCHO TANNING SHED -- DAY

YOUNG ESTEBAN, 10, watches his father draw a knife down a hide.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
Our leather. Our herds. Our ground.
Remember it was ours first.

INT. U.S. LAND COMMISSION, SAN FRANCISCO -- DAY (1850s)

ANGLO COMMISSIONERS. An old Californio lays a bundle of Mexican
land grants on the table. No one reads them.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
The conquest came with a courthouse and a
clerk and a language we did not speak.

EXT. THE RANCHO -- DAY (1860s) -- DROUGHT

White bones in white grass. A squatter's fence. A new sign:
another man's name.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
We sold it the way a man bleeds -- a little
at a time. And we learned our place.
Second. Always second.

EXT. VOLKOV'S TANNERY, SANTA CRUZ -- WINTER (1881)

ESTEBAN MENDEZ, grown and graying, walks through the gate and
takes up another man's knife, another man's hide. At a curtained
window, owner FYODOR VOLKOV watches the yard.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
They left us our hands. So we gave them to
the leather.

INT. TANNERY BACK ROOM -- CONTINUOUS

A CRY. DOLORES holds a NEWBORN, wet and red. DON AURELIO,
ancient, takes the small hands in his.

DON AURELIO
Already big. Already strong.

MATCH CUT TO:

A wet hide off the beam -- then a BASEBALL dropping into a small
brown palm.
```

## ACT I — *The Boardwalk* (1900)

```
EXT. THE NEW PLEASURE PIER -- UNDER CONSTRUCTION -- DAY (1900)

Men drive pilings into the surf. CIRILO MENDEZ, 18, swings a
maul beside NUNZIO SABATINI, 20s, who sings as he works.

NUNZIO
You know what I figured out, Mendez? A man
like us can have anything in this country.
Anything we want.

CIRILO
Yeah?

NUNZIO
Long as we don't mind being hated for it.

He laughs. Cirilo doesn't.

EXT. THE PIER -- BASEBALL BOOTH -- DAY (MIDSUMMER)

The pier is open now. Cirilo works the booth -- iron bottles,
cloth balls, a banner: TEST YOUR ARM -- 3 BALLS A NICKEL. Eyes
down. He doesn't look at the summer crowd.

CHUCK ASHBY, 19, white flannels, money, throws three and misses
three. The bottles don't move. On his arm: REBECCA WHITFIELD,
18 -- beautiful, bored. In the fall she goes up to Stanford,
where the bluff sends its daughters to be settled for good.

CHUCK
(for his friends)
It's rigged. And the greaser's part of the
rig.

Cirilo's eyes stay down. A beat. Then he picks up a ball --

-- and without a windup, barely looking, THROWS.

Six bottles blow off the shelf and ring across the planks.

Silence. Then Rebecca LAUGHS -- a real one, surprised out of
her. She looks at him. He looks back. Holds it.

REBECCA
Do it again.

CIRILO
Costs a nickel.

She holds his eyes a beat too long. Chuck clocks it -- and you
can see him start to hate the kid.

REBECCA
Then here's a nickel.

She hands him a coin in a soft kid-leather glove. He takes it.
Gives her a ball. Their fingers don't touch.

EXT. THE PIER -- THE BOOTH -- DAY (LATER THAT WEEK)

Rebecca drifts back -- a friend in tow, a reason. Doesn't throw.
Doesn't speak past "three for a nickel." But she comes, and lets
him see she comes.

Paying, she leaves a folded scrap of paper in his palm with the
coin, and is gone before his hand closes.

He opens it. Four words: WHEN THE LIGHTS GO OUT.

His brother LUIS goes over the bluff fences after rich girls for
sport and comes back laughing. Cirilo knows the game secondhand
-- and knows that for him, this note could get him killed.

He closes his hand around it. He doesn't drop it in the bay.

EXT. THE TANNERY FLATS -- THE YARD -- DUSK

Poor houses under tannery smoke. Across the dirt, the owner's
house: curtained, shut. FYODOR VOLKOV at the window.

Out of that house comes his daughter, ANASTASIA VOLKOVA, 16 --
black hair, green eyes. Two boys hauling nets stop to stare. She
ignores them, watching for someone.

CIRILO crosses with a water bucket. Old friends -- raised across
this yard, near as brother and sister.

ANASTASIA
(low; accented)
He thinks I'm at my lessons. Marcella's
taking me to the festa.

CIRILO
Your father finds out --

ANASTASIA
My father wants me in the dark, Cirilo.
(smiling)
I've decided against the dark.

EXT. THE BREGANTE LANE -- NIGHT (THE MADONNA'S FESTA)

Loud, warm, crowded. The Genovese BREGANTE clan fills the lane
-- long tables, wine, focaccia, the men singing TRALLALERO with
no instruments. MARCELLA BREGANTE, 16, brown-armed and fearless,
has Anastasia by the hand.

MARCELLA
Eat. You're too sad and too thin. Here --
gut the fish like this --

Anastasia gets fish to the wrist, SWEARS in Ligurian. The table
ROARS. And she throws her head back and LAUGHS.

Cirilo watches the two girls from the edge of the light.

At the dark rim: bluff boys. ROY CALDER and his set. They've
seen Anastasia. They circle. Across the lane, ABEL MENDEZ, 20,
Cirilo's brother -- gifted, half a ruin, alive tonight -- sees
them see her. Cracks his knuckles.

EXT. UNDER THE PIER -- NIGHT

Boardwalk lights out, surf up. In the black wedge under the
pilings, CIRILO and REBECCA stand close.

REBECCA
Tell me something true. You never do.

CIRILO
My family owned this. Not the pier -- the
land. All of it, the river to the redwoods,
before there was a Santa Cruz to own it.
(beat)
Now I sell nickels on a plank built on top
of it.
(beat)
And I'd rather be here, like this, with
you, than have a single acre of it back.

She takes his hand.

REBECCA
Then we'll just have to take the rest of it
back ourselves.

A WHISTLE. A WATCHMAN'S LAMP swings over the rail. They freeze.
The beam passes over them -- and moves on. PEREIRA, the old
Portuguese night man, shuffles off, humming. He saw. He says
nothing.

EXT. THE CAROUSEL HOUSE -- OFF HOURS -- NIGHT

The carved flying horses, still. Old BRAUER, the carver, throws the
switch. Alone in the dark, only the sea for sound, CIRILO rides
-- around and around, the one hour of it that's his. Brauer
watches from the shadow and says nothing.

EXT. THE DARK UNDER THE PIER -- NIGHT (THE NIGHT BEFORE)

Surf and shadow. CIRILO has told her: the arm, the college, the
morning train. REBECCA does not say the brave thing.

REBECCA
Don't go. Stay, be the bottle boy, let me
pay my nickel all summer. I was raised on
those men -- the higher you climb, the harder
they hold the line. Not softer.

CIRILO
I'm going up so that one day I can stand
with you in the daylight and no man alive
has the standing to say no. I don't want the
rancho. I want a family. A wife who sees me,
a child to carry the name, a door I don't
have to stand afraid behind. Every loud
thing I'm chasing is the long road back to
that. With you.

A watchman's lamp swings. He crouches, stands the SMALL FIRST
GLOVE -- the child's one his family made him -- upright against a
piling, in her keeping. Says nothing. Lets the leather say it: a
man does not leave that with a girl unless he means to come back.

Gone toward the morning. REBECCA could run after him. She does
not. She stands alone with the small glove in her two hands, and
the waiting begins.

EXT. THE LITTLE DEPOT -- MORNING (1900)

A train waits. CIRILO in his one good suit, a college letter in
his pocket. The family stops at the END OF THE LANE -- not the
platform; the platform isn't theirs.

ABEL hugs him too long, drunk at nine a.m.

ABEL
Don't come back like me.

He's gone up the lane before Cirilo can answer. DOLORES takes
her son's face in both hands, murmurs a blessing in the old
language. Then she presses the worn olive-wood ROSARY into his pitching hand and folds his fingers over it.

DOLORES
(low)
No lo sueltes, mijo. Don't let go.

ESTEBAN works the brim of his hat.

ESTEBAN
They'll try to make you small up there. A
politer way than here. But the same.

Then -- against his whole careful life -- he folds his son's
pitching hand -- his mother's beads already shut inside it -- into a fist inside his own two stained ones, holds
it, lets go, and walks up the lane without looking back.

On the platform, against the rules: REBECCA.

REBECCA
(low, fierce)
Go. Become a man so loud they can't pretend
not to hear your name. And come back for me
when you have.

She presses the kid-leather glove into his hand.

REBECCA (CONT'D)
So you remember whose hand you're playing
for.

The train CALLS. He boards. Does not look back.

EXT. THE MOVING TRAIN -- NORTH -- CONTINUOUS

The platform slides away. Rebecca makes herself turn and walk.

A RED-TAILED HAWK rises off the ridge and runs with the train --
over the water, the nets, the gold hills. DOLORES, in the lane,
stops and watches it.

The hawk tilts at the county line, holds a wing, and slides back
south -- letting the train carry the boy on north, alone.

CIRILO (V.O.)
I would climb. I would not stop climbing.
And whatever it cost me, I would never, for
any price, agree to be second to the men
who decided I was born it.

SMASH CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT I.
```

## ACT II — *The Rematch & the Long Wait* (1903–1917)

```
INT. SANTA CLARA COLLEGE -- LECTURE HALL -- DAY (1903)

A public oral exam. CIRILO, 20, construes Latin at the front.
PROFESSOR PRUETT runs him long and hard -- traps, questions he
asks no one else. Cirilo answers every one.

PRUETT
Tell me, Mr. Mendez -- don't you think your
talents might be better spent in manual
work? Your people have such ... aptitude.

The room stills. Cirilo looks at him a long moment. Answers in
Latin -- we hear it under, in English:

CIRILO
"It is the mark of a small mind to mistake
the accident of birth for the measure of a
man."
(beat)
Cicero. The passage we were construing,
Professor.

It is not in the passage. He made it up, in correct Latin,
betting Pruett doesn't know the text. Pruett goes red. Ends the
exam. The other students -- startled -- APPLAUD.

EXT. SANTA CLARA BALLFIELD -- DAY (SPRING)

Orchards in blossom, petals on the infield. STANFORD, in cardinal
red, down for the oldest rivalry in the state -- come to see the
Santa Clara ACE, which is what CIRILO has become in two seasons
up the line. In the stands, half-risen, a glove forgotten in her
lap: REBECCA -- down from Stanford with her set, to a game she
never meant to watch, until she knew the motion. At the plate, an
S on his chest: CHUCK ASHBY.

CHUCK
(loud, for the red seats)
The bottle boy! Three balls a nickel,
fellas!

The Stanford side laughs. Cirilo finds Rebecca's eyes. Sets.

STRIKE ONE, past Ashby before the laugh ends. STRIKE TWO buckles
his knees. He holds the ball, looks once at Rebecca -- and
throws the best thing he owns. STRIKE THREE.

Rebecca, on her feet, not caring who sees.

CIRILO (V.O.)
I thought beating them on the square would
open the door. It took me years to learn
the square was the only place the door was
ever open at all.

EXT. STANFORD LANDS -- A REDWOOD GROVE -- NIGHT (FORTNIGHTS, 1903--)

A stand the loggers missed. He comes up on the milk train and
walks the last two miles in the dark; she's told her housemother
she's at her devotions. Two hours once a fortnight, spent
TALKING -- the one thing neither was let do: to be known by one
person in a world that knows them only as types. He tells her the
rancho, the courthouse, his father's silence; she tells him about
her mother, and the long conversion into an ornament. HOOVES pass
close; he covers her mouth with his hand; they breathe each
other's breath till the riders fade. They both know discovery
would cost them differently. She climbs to the grove anyway. So
does he.

EXT. SAN FRANCISCO -- OCEAN BEACH -- DAY (ONCE)

Once, they steal a whole day in the one city big enough to
swallow them: under the Ferry Building clock, down Market Street
side by side in open daylight. The Cliff House over the surf;
chowder they can't afford. The wind takes her hat -- CIRILO runs
it down the wet sand like an outfielder under a fly and brings it
back, and she laughs the real one, out loud, with no one to tell
her not to. The wonder is how ORDINARY it is -- the daylight
other couples never think about; contraband, to these two.

INT. A NOB HILL DRAWING ROOM -- NIGHT (THE BREAK)

Gaslight, good furniture. HORACE WHITFIELD -- self-made,
terrified of falling back down -- addresses his daughter like a
board that voted wrong. By the window: CHUCK.

CHUCK
He throws a baseball, Rebecca. That's the
whole of him. An arm and a borrowed suit.

REBECCA
And what's the whole of you? Your father's
name, money, and chair at the club.
(almost kindly)
At least his arm is his.

HORACE
Walk out that door and you walk out of this
family. No allowance. No name. No coming
back when being poor stops being romantic.

She looks around the warm, lit cage of the room she grew up in --
and goes, down the wide curving stair, past the long pier glass,
not checking it, not looking back; out with a single valise and
nothing of the house's in it.

REBECCA (CONT'D)
(turning back, to Chuck)
Find someone who wants the chair at the club.
They're the only people you've ever met.

It will be fourteen years before she climbs that stair again.

EXT. CLASS-D BALLFIELD -- VALLEY TOWN -- DAY (MONTAGE, 1904--)

A cinder infield under a white sky. Cirilo, learning his trade
in front of nobody. His catcher: DEACON MULQUEEN, 38 -- a ruin,
a gut, a thirst, a fingertip gone to a foul tip.

MULQUEEN
(crouched, spitting)
The battery's a conversation, kid. Not a
command. I'll know you better than you know
yourself -- and you're gonna let me.
(a grin)
That's the one. That's the one, you old
greaser. Throw me that one again.

EXT. A DEPOT CAFE -- VALLEY TOWN -- NIGHT

The club, dead off a doubleheader. The COUNTERMAN looks Cirilo
over.

COUNTERMAN
Can't serve the Mexican. Rest of you boys
are welcome.

The ballplayers shuffle, study their feet. Nobody moves. Then
MULQUEEN fills the doorway.

MULQUEEN
Well, then. I guess none of us are hungry.

He doesn't move. He looks at the boys, one by one, until --
grumbling -- they get up. Every one. And file out.

EXT. LIM'S CAFE -- BY THE TRACKS -- MIDNIGHT

The only other light on. LIM, Chinese, feeds them all -- the
brown one included -- at a back table, for cash, without a word.

EXT. BEACH FLATS -- THE BEACHCOMBERS' GROUND -- A SUNDAY

The climb lets him home. His county's club, behind the bath
houses, surf for an outfield wall; his brothers behind him; a
thousand of the flats along the foul ropes. At the end of the
bench, in street clothes, keeping the score book in a copperplate
hand finer than any banker's: EDWARD PURSE -- the finest
ballplayer on the field, the best baseball mind in the county,
and everyone knows it. He's called the MASCOT and kept off the
grass, because the one line in American baseball that does not
bend is drawn clean around a Black man, with no gate in it.

A visiting club comes up short. For ten minutes it's floated that
Purse fill in -- just once. The field goes quiet. A thousand
people in their Sunday best decide, without a word, that it will
not happen. It does not. Purse licks his pencil and enters the
next batter, his face not changing -- which is the worst of it.

ON CIRILO: he's shown the exact dimensions of his own permission
-- let onto this grass only so long as the papers keep calling
him Spanish. He throws a two-hit shutout in a cold fury and
enjoys none of it. He and Purse ride the trolley home -- the
let-on and the kept-off -- in a silence, for once, shared.

EXT. RECREATION PARK -- PACIFIC COAST LEAGUE -- DAY (~1910)

Big crowds. Cirilo, graying, two-hits the Angels and walks off
to a ROAR.

INT. A GRANDSTAND BOX -- CONTINUOUS

A MAJOR-LEAGUE SCOUT in a derby lowers his glasses to his bird
dog, VANCE GREEVY -- flask, dog-eared notebook.

SCOUT
Best arm I've seen all year. Spitter falls
off a cliff. Reads hitters like a cardsharp.

GREEVY
(already knowing)
So we take him.

The scout does the second sum -- not the arm, the gate.

SCOUT
He's twenty-seven. Junk-baller -- no
fastball for a poster, no name a marquee will
carry, a tannery story that's no story.
Crowds want youth and a man they can chant.
Ownership won't spend the ink.
(raising the glasses)
Write down the kid in left. Younger. Easier
to sell.

The boy on the grass is already gone from his mind. But GREEVY
writes three words under the name -- CAN'T USE HIM -- mutters
"best arm on the field," and underlines it, the way he will
season after season: a debt the game won't pay.

ANGLE -- THE GRASS. Cirilo tips his cap to the only crowd
allowed to love him. He never knows a man in a derby just priced
his whole life in a sentence.

EXT. A COAST LEAGUE CLUBHOUSE -- DUSK

DORGAN, a Coast League manager, takes Cirilo aside after a game.

DORGAN
Best arm in this league and you'll rot in it.
The bigs would take it tomorrow -- except for
the one thing. And here's what you don't know,
son: it's fixable. Be Spanish. Castilian. A
don's grandson -- which you are. Lose the
Mexican. Let them make you exotic instead of
cheap. One vowel. A ticket east.

Cirilo doesn't argue.

CIRILO
My mother keeps a name on a hill they could
never take, because she wouldn't let them. I
won't be the one who hands it over. Not for the
train. Not for any of it.

Dorgan nods. He's sorry. He never raises it again.

EXT. THE FLATS -- THE LANE -- SATURDAY NIGHT

Home between seasons. Three countries' suppers down one lane.
NUNZIO on a crate with a guitar; old CIPRIANO's fiddle.

OLD MAN
You, Beppe? You couldn't have caught a girl
standing still.

They dance in the dirt. ABEL, the most alive man in the county
tonight. LUIS eats three plates. Young CIRILO on a step, handed
a warm tortilla by a mother who won't, tonight, send him to bed.

The lane sings.

EXT. A VALLEY TOWN -- A RENTED ROOM -- NIGHT (YEARS ON)

A dollar room, a brick-wall view. REBECCA has made it theirs --
a chapel photo on the sill, poppies in a glass. CIRILO comes in
dark, sits on the bed, says nothing.

REBECCA
They wouldn't serve you.

CIRILO
No.

REBECCA
Then they're fools, and their coffee's
terrible. And you'll eat better than any of
them tonight -- I've got half a cold chicken
and a very serious tomato a Portuguese lady
gave me for nearly nothing.

The corner of his mouth goes.

CIRILO
The tomato is from the Portuguese lady.

REBECCA
The tomato is from the Portuguese lady.

CIRILO
Then it's a good tomato.

They eat with their fingers off a chair, laughing low. Later, in
the dark, to the ceiling:

CIRILO (CONT'D)
I'm glad you left the bluff.

REBECCA
I know.

INT. HOLY CROSS CHURCH -- SANTA CRUZ -- DAY (1916)

After the years in the margins, they make it law. Half empty, a
fault line down the center aisle: the MENDEZ family on one side,
worn Sunday clothes; on the other, an empty pew where the
Whitfields would have sat. She walks the aisle alone, in a plain
dress -- no father to give her, so she gives herself. The priest
speaks Spanish; REBECCA answers in it. DOLORES watches, face
composed, still waiting for the hook in the gift.

EXT. HOLY CROSS -- THE STEPS -- CONTINUOUS

DON AURELIO takes his grandson's arm. Quiet. Afraid.

DON AURELIO
First the game. Now an Anglo man's daughter.
You keep reaching over your head, mijo. Our
family was not built to be first.

CIRILO
Then I'll be the one it stops costing.

Behind them, REBECCA laughs the real one -- the one from the
booth, sixteen years on, still knocking itself loose out of
people who'd forgotten how.

EXT./INT. THE EAST -- LEAN YEARS -- MONTAGE (1916--1917)

A gray big-league cup of coffee in BROOKLYN -- a midseason
nobody, an aging journeyman with a foreign name. Off-season: a NEWARK
TANNERY bench, a major-leaguer who can't make the rent. His
Brooklyn landlady, AOIFE DOYLE -- an Irish widow, gold-star flag
in the window -- lets the rent slide and won't admit it.

INT. COLD BROOKLYN ROOMS -- DAY (WINTER 1917)

Snow. Cirilo, gray, stamps his boots in the doorway. A TELEGRAM
in his hand.

REBECCA
What -- what is it --

CIRILO
Boston bought my contract. The Red Sox.
(she gasps -- he stops her)
They want a thirty-five-year-old spitballer
for the price of the train. The war took the
young arms to France, and a cheap old
foreigner fills the spot. Sixteen years --
and the door opened because they ran short.
Not because they wanted me.

She takes his face in both hands.

REBECCA
It doesn't matter why the door opened. It
matters that you walk through it.

CIRILO (V.O.)
Hooper put my name in. A kid from Hollister,
one ridge over from my brothers. After
everything -- the man who finally remembered
me was a boy from up the road.

He's going east.

SMASH CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT II.
```

## ACT III — *Siempre Primero* (1918)

```
INT. COLD BROOKLYN FLAT -- NIGHT (NEW YEAR'S, 1918)

Drunk horns outside. A cry -- a NEWBORN. REBECCA holds him.
CIRILO crosses and takes the small hands, both folded into one
of his. He can't speak. So:

CIRILO
(barely)
Already big. Already strong.

A thirty-six-year-old man with his first real shot at the top in
front of him at last, a tannery stain still under his nails, the old
glove his own family made him on the sill. For one held breath he is
not second to any man alive.

EXT. FENWAY PARK -- A TUESDAY IN JULY (1918)

Heat off the infield. A spare, gray-templed man warms up easy.
BABE RUTH wanders over to watch. HARRY HOOPER drifts in. Three
future Hall of Famers and a dying man on a ball field -- and
only one of them knows which is which.

Cirilo reads their feet. Busts the deep stander up and in. Ties
the crowder in a knot. Floats the dying ball past three swings.

In the seventh, the bleachers find it -- low, then all together:

CROWD
EL CID -- EL CID -- EL CID --

Ten thousand Boston throats chanting the grand borrowed name at a
tanner's son, every one of them on his feet for him. Cirilo takes
the ball back, face blank.

CIRILO (V.O.)
They're on their feet. For me. The bottle boy.
Roaring a name -- and the name means me. I made
them do it, with the thing the old man put in
my arm. After all these years -- let it come.

EXT. FENWAY PARK -- A GRAY AFTERNOON (THE DUEL)

The Senators have WALTER JOHNSON. The Big Train -- the fastest
arm in the game, pure speed, no deception. Barrow gives the ball
to Cirilo. Power against the craft.

Nine innings, no score. The crowd dawns to it: the old brown
junk-baller is matching the Big Train pitch for pitch, ZERO for
ZERO.

The eighth breaks it -- on craft. Hooper walks, steals second,
takes third on a grounder, scores on a shallow sac fly. ONE run.
The only run.

Cirilo sets them down in the ninth. Over.

He has beaten Walter Johnson. One to nothing.

The crowd that didn't want to love him stands and ROARS. He
doesn't whoop. He looks once at the flat gray sky -- out of an
old habit -- for a bird that isn't there.

CIRILO (V.O.)
This is the thing the old man gave me, off a
bucket. I didn't get here alone -- I carried
all of them up here with me.

INT./EXT. THE 1918 WORLD SERIES -- TWO STARTS

GAME TWO. Cirilo throws the best game of his life and LOSES it -- a
bad-hop grounder, a run on dirt and luck, 2 to 1, not an earned run
against him. He sits with the warm-up ball, an old man down a game in
the only Series the arm may ever give him, certain this is just how it
ends for a man like him: the right work, the wrong bounce. Second,
once more.

HOOPER
(sitting down beside him)
There's more games, old man.

GAME SIX. THE CLINCHER. Bases loaded, two out, the whole season on the
next pitch -- and the Cubs' FRED MERKLE digs in. SCHANG crouches.

Pitch one -- fastball IN, under the hands. (Ball.) Pitch two --
the change, same arm, nothing behind it; Merkle's swing dies out
front. (Strike.) A wasted curve. Pitch four -- fastball in again;
Merkle fouls it off his fists, late, guarding inside now.

Which is the trap.

Pitch five -- the dying ball. Fat, waist-high, right where he
wants it. He swings from his heels --

-- and it drops off the table. The bat goes through empty air.
Schang's mitt takes it at the shin. The umpire's arm goes up.

The crowd sees a strikeout. Schang sees a man out-thought, sixty feet
at a time, on the one fair square -- and winning. The park comes
apart. They lift him off the mound a champion -- FIRST -- and ten
thousand throats roar the name they gave him, EL CID, in pure love,
and the Game Two loss washes clean.

EXT. THE STREETS OF BOSTON -- NIGHT (THE PARADE)

They carry him through the streets. FORTY THOUSAND people in the wet
dark, passing the champion overhead; REBECCA and the baby up on a
wagon bed; his SON held high in a knit cap, laughing because his
father is laughing. For one night Cirilo Méndez is not second to any
man in America, and he lets the whole grateful city close around him,
and is -- there's no smaller word -- happy.

EXT. ALONG THE RIVER -- A CLEAR COLD AFTERNOON (DAYS LATER)

CIRILO carries his son, naming the world for him in Spanish -- agua,
pájaro, cielo -- REBECCA's arm through his. An ordinary family on an
ordinary afternoon.

Then -- between one streetlamp and the next -- his arm goes heavy.
Sweat on his face in the cold. A hard shiver takes the whole of him.

CIRILO
(working to sound light)
Rebecca. Take him. My hands have gone strange.

She takes the baby. He makes the last blocks home on will alone, the
fever climbing in him like water up a piling.

INT. THE COLD ROOMS -- NIGHT (OCTOBER 1918)

Four days. That is all it takes the strong, that autumn. On the
fourth, the fever steps back to leave him a few clear minutes.

The wrong quiet. On the sill: the championship ball, a candle, a
rosary, a small Ohlone basket. REBECCA on the edge of the bed, their
son awake against her. In the corner, AOIFE prays.

Cirilo's hand finds the old glove on the blanket. He turns it
once -- and folds his son's small fingers around it.

CIRILO
(they lean close)
Abuelo was right. There's a price. For us.
Being first.
(the ghost of a smile)
Worth it. Tell him -- when you take me home --
it was worth it.

REBECCA
(into the quiet)
You weren't second. Not for one day of your
life.

His hand goes still on his son's hand on the glove. Aoife
prays. Rebecca looks at a Méndez's hands.

Small ones now. Already strong.

SMASH TO BLACK.
```

```
EXT. THE SANTA CRUZ FLATS -- OCTOBER 1918

The news comes twice in one month.

FIRST: they WON -- the Boston club -- and one of ours pitched
them to it. Men who can't read have the box scores read aloud in
the cantina. NUNZIO closes the fruit stand, his face working.
Uno de nosotros. One of ours.

Then the SECOND wire. The influenza. He's dead.

DOLORES takes it at the stove. Finishes cooking -- people have to
eat. Then climbs the hill in the rain.

EXT. THE HILL ABOVE THE BAY -- LATE OCTOBER 1918 -- THE BURIAL

They bring him home. And the town comes. The TANNERY SHUTS its
gates -- the first time ever, for a Mexican. Esteban's old crew
at the grave, cracked hands folded. The fishermen up from the
wharf. The flats families, speaking the old language out loud in
the open.

DON AURELIO stands at the edge of land that was once all theirs.

As they lower the box, a RED-TAILED HAWK comes up off the ravine
and hangs over the open grave, motionless against the gray. No
one takes it for a coincidence.

DOLORES (V.O.)
El Halcón. The hawk. The world loved him by the
grand name it gave him. The family knew him by
the truer one. Now we take him back -- and give
it to him again.

The hawk tilts and slides off north along the ridge.

EXT. THE LIVING COAST -- THE SAME DAY -- MONTAGE

A lone SEA OTTER rides the kelp off the point, cracks an urchin
on a stone over its heart. GRAY WHALES track south. STEELHEAD
climb the rain-swollen river. A coyote on a deer trail.

The sun comes up over the water the next morning the way it
always has -- and asks the ground that holds Cirilo Méndez no
question about his name, or his skin, or his place in any line.

EXT. THE SAME GROUND -- TIME FORWARD -- PRESENT DAY

The houses thin. A cannery rises and falls. A field. Then the
ASPHALT comes back up, the chain-link, the storage yard. And
over it, the OLD REDWOOD -- and on the high limb, the RED-TAILED
HAWK, gold eye on the ground below.

EXT. HARVEY WEST PARK -- FIELD 1 -- CONTINUOUS (PRESENT DAY)

The playoff from dawn. The BOY on the mound throws a pitch that
drops off the table. The hitter swings a hand's width over it.

OLD MAN
... throws like Méndez.

The boy doesn't hear it. Doesn't know the name. He just got

EXT. UP THE SAN LORENZO -- A FOLD OF THE RIDGE -- TIMELESS

A REDWOOD the loggers missed, old before Rome, stands in its fog

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
Not primero. Not segundo.

Siempre.
```
