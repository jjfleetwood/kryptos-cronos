## Approach

A continuous spec script built from the current novel (not the older selected-scenes
draft buried in `SIEMPRE_SEGUNDO.md`, which predates the present-day frame and the
Volkov rename). Spare, image-driven, dead-ball-era restraint. The hawk is the film's
witness and its only narrator of the frame; **Don Aurelio's V.O.** carries the
dispossession prologue, then recedes — the rest plays in scene.

**The no-spoiler rule holds on screen too:** the dread is elegiac and vague; the plot
beats (the Red Sox, the Series, the influenza, the death) detonate fresh in Act III.

### Sequence map (to build, act by act)
- **COLD OPEN** — the hawk over modern Santa Cruz → match-dissolve to 1882. *(drafted below)*
- **PROLOGUE — California Banknotes** — rancho → courthouse → tannery → the birth. *(drafted below)*
- **ACT I — The Boardwalk (1900)** — the booth; Rebecca; Anastasia & Marcella; the pier; the leaving. *(next)*
- **ACT II — The Rematch & the Long Wait (1903–1917)** — Stanford/Santa Clara; the bushes & Mulqueen; the road & Lim; the Coast League & the man in the derby; Saturday Night; the marriage (A Rented Room); the call.
- **ACT III — Siempre Primero (1918)** — New Year's & the boy; the season; El Cid; the Duel; the Series & the strikeout; what the crowd carried; the room.
- **CODA** — the news home; the widow; the burial & the hawk; Under the Sun; the redwood.

## COLD OPEN

```
FADE IN:

EXT. SANTA CRUZ MOUNTAINS -- HIGH RIDGE -- DAWN (PRESENT DAY)

Fog burning off a wall of redwoods. A RED-TAILED HAWK unmoors
from the highest fir and tips onto the first thermal the sun
pries off the ridge.

We go with her. We will not leave her until she has shown us
everything.

The forest gives way beneath her -- oak, chaparral, switchback
roads, a high school, water towers, the loud bright sprawl of a
California beach town on a June morning. To a hawk a century is
a season and a town is a kind of weather.

EXT. STEAMER LANE -- THE LIGHTHOUSE POINT -- CONTINUOUS

She comes out over the water. Long peeling walls of cold morning
surf. A YOUNG SURFER (18), burnt brown by a whole summer, drops
down the face of one --

-- tucks under the lip. GONE inside the spinning blue-green
barrel, the whole Pacific arched over his head --

-- and is flung out the open end in a burst of spray and light.
He throws his head back. WHOOPS -- thin as a gull to the hawk.
On the cliff, TWO GIRLS watch him and pretend, with great care,
not to.

He will remember this wave when he is an old man. He doesn't
know that yet. That is the one mercy of being him this morning.

EXT. THE WHARF / THE WET SAND -- CONTINUOUS

Sea lions stacked and bellowing on the pilings. First tourists
nursing coffees. And down on the wet sand -- a FATHER and his
SMALL BOY, throwing a ball back and forth in the early light.
The glove is still too big for the boy's hand.

The soft THOCK of the catch carries up, thin, to the hawk.

EXT. THE BOARDWALK -- CONTINUOUS

A hundred-year-old wooden boardwalk at the river's mouth. The
GIANT DIPPER hauls its first cars up the long, clattering lift.
A car breasts the summit -- a knot of kids, a BOY and a GIRL
shoulder to shoulder in the front seat -- tips, and PLUNGES.

The scream goes up off the whole train at once. The girl seizes
the boy's arm, in earnest now. Neither will admit, after, that
this was the moment.

It was. It always is.

EXT. THE BOARDWALK -- ALONG THE RAIL -- CONTINUOUS

The ones the hawk does not see the way they see themselves:

A MOTHER who worked doubles all spring for these three days,
watching her daughter shriek down the Dipper -- and thinking,
plainly, on her face: THAT. That is what it was all for.

A FATHER priced half out of the town he was raised in, holding a
corn dog he can't quite afford, watching his kids be happy --
and, for one afternoon, given the thing he wanted.

The boardwalk runs on that. It always has.

EXT. THE BOARDWALK -- THE MIDWAY -- CONTINUOUS

The hawk drops over the games. Ring toss. Water race. And the
oldest one: the BASEBALL THROW -- stacked iron bottles, worn
cloth balls.

The KID working the booth is poor. It shows in the shoes. Brown,
sixteen, a local on a summer job. A knot of OTHER BOYS his own
age -- boat-and-club money -- has stopped. They aren't buying
throws. They work him over, lightly, grinning: the accent, the
shoes. One flicks a balled-up dollar at his chest and laughs
when the kid flinches to catch it.

The kid keeps his face still and his eyes at the level of their
collars -- the level the poor learn to keep -- and waits for it
to be over.

The hawk judges nothing. She has watched this small mean weather
cross this same ground more summers than the planks have been
repainted. It passes beneath her like the tide, on a turning
older than anything with a name. She rides on.

EXT. UP THE RIVER -- CLIMBING -- CONTINUOUS

She banks inland, up the river's ravine. The town dwindles
beneath her.

EXT. HARVEY WEST PARK -- FIELD 1 -- CONTINUOUS

In the green bottomland under the lime-kiln hills: a ballfield
dressed for a playoff. Fresh lime foul lines. Banners snapping on
the backstop chain-link. The snack bar steaming. A tinny PA
reading out a lineup of nine-year-olds as though they were the
'27 Yankees. Parents folded into lawn chairs along the fence,
holding their breath.

And on the mound -- small in all that green geometry -- a BOY
toes the rubber and looks in for the sign.

For one held instant the whole bright morning belongs to him --

-- the way it has belonged, on that exact patch of grass, to a
hundred years of boys before him.

The hawk slides over it, and on.

EXT. A LOW RISE ABOVE THE RIVER -- CONTINUOUS

A storage yard. Asphalt, steel roll-up doors, chain-link fence.
A lot no one looks at twice.

But one thing stands over it: an OLD REDWOOD -- the last great
tree on that ground, somehow spared the saw.

The hawk sets her wings, drops, and takes the high limb. She
fixes the round gold eye on the asphalt below -- the fence, the
nothing -- and holds her station over the exact patch of ground
where, once, a family lived.

CLOSE ON: THE HAWK'S EYE. Gold. Ancient. Patient enough that it
does not trouble to tell a century from an afternoon.

And under that gaze, the world begins to thin --

-- the asphalt, the roll-up doors, the chain-link fade --

-- SMOKE comes up, low, over a close poor cluster of houses --

-- the morning becomes another morning: earlier, harder, and
still entirely ahead of itself --

EXT. THE TANNERY FLATS -- A SWEPT DIRT YARD -- MORNING (1882)

-- and down in the swept dirt, a CHILD (4) too small for the
water bucket riding his hip stops dead. Feels a shadow cross
him. Tips his face up to find it.

He finds the hawk.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
(old, unhurried; a whole lifetime in it)
El halcón ha venido a mirarte, mijo.
The hawk has come to look at you.
(beat)
And you should know what that means.

SMASH TO BLACK.

TITLE OVER BLACK:   SIEMPRE SEGUNDO

A beat. Then, small and white, beneath it:

                    "always second"
```

## PROLOGUE — *California Banknotes*

```
DON AURELIO (V.O.)
Before this was a town for strangers...
it was ours. All of it.

EXT. THE MENDEZ RANCHO -- COASTAL HILLS -- DAY (1840s)

Hills the color of a lion. No fences anywhere -- the land just
goes. A herd of cattle moves across it like a slow brown river.

EXT. THE MATANZA -- KILLING GROUND -- DAY

HIDES -- hundreds of them -- staked to the earth to dry, dark
and gleaming: a field of black windows.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
A cowhide was as good as coin. Men
called them California banknotes. And
they laughed when they said it -- and
they meant it.

INT. RANCHO TANNING SHED -- DAY

Oak bark, lime, the honest stink of the work. YOUNG ESTEBAN (10)
watches his father draw a knife the length of a hide.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
Our leather. From our herds. On our
ground. Remember that part, mijo.
Remember it was ours first.

INT. U.S. LAND COMMISSION, SAN FRANCISCO -- DAY (1850s)

A cold room. ANGLO COMMISSIONERS murmuring in English. An old
Californio -- DON AURELIO'S FATHER -- lays a bundle of Mexican
land grants on the table with shaking hands.

No one leans forward to read them.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
The conquest did not come with a sword.
It came with a courthouse, and a clerk,
and a language we did not speak.

EXT. THE RANCHO -- DAY (1860s) -- DROUGHT

White bones in white grass. A SQUATTER'S FENCE cuts the old
range in two. A WEATHERED SIGN, newer than everything around it:
another man's name.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
We sold it the way a man bleeds. A
little at a time. Until there is no more.
(quieter)
And we learned the family's place.
Second. Always second.

EXT. VOLKOV'S TANNERY, SANTA CRUZ -- WINTER MORNING (1882)

Smoke. The long wharf beyond. ESTEBAN MENDEZ (grown now, gray
coming in) walks through the gate, takes up another man's knife,
another man's hide. Behind a curtained office window, a figure
watches the yard: FYODOR VOLKOV -- the Russian who owns the
place, and his own long history of being left and taken from.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
They left us our hands. So we gave them
to the leather.

INT. TANNERY BACK ROOM -- CONTINUOUS

A CRY. DOLORES, exhausted and fierce, holds a NEWBORN -- wet,
red, roaring. DON AURELIO -- ancient, doing sums behind his eyes
that come out, every way he adds them, to a cost -- takes the
small hands in his.

Already big. Already strong.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
The family was not finished. Not while
there was a Mendez with hands.

MATCH CUT TO:

A WET NEW HIDE coming off the beam --

-- and then a BASEBALL, white and new, dropping into a small
brown palm.

```

## ACT I — *The Boardwalk* (1900)

```
EXT. THE NEW PLEASURE PIER -- UNDER CONSTRUCTION -- DAY (1900)

Fresh-cut planks, white paint, a casino strung with a thousand
unlit bulbs. Men drive pilings into the surf-line -- Mexican,
Italian, Portuguese, brown backs in the cold spray. CIRILO
MENDEZ (18) swings a maul beside NUNZIO SABATINI (20s), an
Italian with a good voice and hands already shiny-burned from
the lime kilns. Nunzio sings as he works.

NUNZIO
(grinning, breathless)
You know what I figured out, Mendez? A
man like us -- we can have anything in
this country. Anything we want.

CIRILO
(not looking up from the maul)
Yeah?

NUNZIO
Long as we don't mind being hated for it.

He laughs like it's a joke. Cirilo doesn't.

EXT. THE PLEASURE PIER -- THE BASEBALL BOOTH -- DAY (MIDSUMMER)

The pier is open now -- bright, loud, the band going and the bath-house crowds out.
Cirilo, promoted from the pilings to the planks, works a game
booth: a pyramid of iron milk-bottles, worn cloth balls. A
banner: TEST YOUR ARM -- THREE BALLS A NICKEL.

He has mastered the one skill the job requires: he does not
look at the summer people. Eyes down. Stay small.

A knot of money stops at the booth -- bluff kids, City money,
white flannels. At its center CHUCK ASHBY (21), home from his
first year at Stanford, certain as weather. Beside him: REBECCA
WHITFIELD (18) -- beautiful, poised, armored -- the most
looked-at girl on the pier and the least content, though no one
has ever looked close enough to see it.

Chuck lays his nickel down and throws to win it for her. First
ball -- clears the top. Second -- knocks two more rocking. But
TWO iron bottles still stand: the low corners, the hard ones.
He throws his last as hard as a college arm can throw -- clips
the left one. It spins, settles back UPRIGHT, mocking.

The little crowd goes quiet. A man who was supposed to win, in
front of everyone, has not.

CHUCK
(too loud, covering)
It's rigged. The whole booth's rigged --
weighted bottoms, a dead ball. And the
greaser's part of the rig.

He gets his laugh. Cirilo keeps his eyes at the level of their
collars. The slur he lets pass. The RIGGED he can't.

CIRILO
(low, even)
It isn't rigged, sir. The bottles are
true. You throw it low. And square.

A brown boy plainly, quietly RIGHT -- worse to Chuck than any
insult. Cirilo turns to reset the pyramid. Bends to the shelf.
Back turned.

Chuck picks the spun ball off the planks and THROWS it -- catches
Cirilo square between the shoulder blades. Not hard enough to
call a blow. Too hard to call a joke.

CHUCK
You come up here and try it, then.
Since you know so much.

The planks go still. Down the rail the FLATS have gathered --
Italians off the kilns, Mexican women, NUNZIO -- watching the
way you watch a thing that costs blood either way.

And then -- for one heartbeat -- something Cirilo has kept on a
short chain his whole life slips it.

He comes around the end of the counter. Sets the whole pyramid
up himself -- all six, square on the shelf. Steps to the line.
Picks up a ball. No windup. Barely a glance.

THROWS.

SIX iron bottles blow clean off the shelf and ring across the
planks. Down the line the flats come APART -- Nunzio loudest,
something joyful and obscene in Italian.

Silence. And then REBECCA LAUGHS -- not the bright tin laugh
the young ladies keep in their purses; a real one, sideways and
surprised, knocked loose out of her like the bottles.

She looks at him. He has, for the first time in front of these
people, looked back. He has been SEEN. And she has been seen
seeing him.

REBECCA
Do it again.

CIRILO
(flat; no smile)
Costs a nickel.

She holds his eyes a beat too long. Longer than is safe.
Chuck watches her do it -- and there it is, the exact instant
he begins to hate the bottle boy: the hatred of a man learning
that the thing he believes he owns has a mind of its own, and a
preference, and the preference is not him.

REBECCA
Then here's a nickel.

She holds out a coin -- in a glove of kid leather so soft it
might have been sewn by his own father, in another century,
when leather still belonged to the Mendez.

He takes the coin. Gives her a ball. Their fingers do not
touch, and somehow that is worse.

EXT. THE PIER -- THE BOOTH -- DAY (LATER THAT WEEK)

Rebecca drifts back -- a friend in tow, a parasol, a reason. She
doesn't throw. Doesn't speak past "three for a nickel." But she
comes, day after day, and lets him see that she comes -- which on
the boardwalk is the only language two forbidden people are
allowed.

Paying her nickel, she leaves a folded scrap of paper in his
palm with the coin, and is gone up the planks before his hand
closes on it.

He opens it. Four words, in a tutored hand gone unsteady:
WHEN THE LIGHTS GO OUT.

His brother LUIS -- the charmer -- goes over the bluff fences
after rich men's daughters for sport, and always comes back
laughing. Cirilo knows the game secondhand. He also knows that
for him, this note is the most dangerous thing a man his color
can hold in this county.

He closes his hand around it. He does not drop it in the bay.

EXT. THE TANNERY FLATS -- THE YARD -- EARLY EVENING

A close, poor cluster of houses under tannery smoke. Across the
dirt from the Mendez house stands the owner's house: curtained,
shut. A figure at the window -- FYODOR VOLKOV -- watching the
street as though it might take something from him.

And out of that shut house, into the gold evening, comes his
daughter: ANASTASIA VOLKOVA (16). Black hair. Green eyes. A
beauty so arresting that two boys hauling nets stop dead to
look. She doesn't notice them. She is watching for someone.

CIRILO crosses the yard with a water bucket. They are not
sweethearts -- they grew up across this dirt, the worker's son
and the owner's daughter, and became, before either had a word
for it, brother and sister.

ANASTASIA
(low, accented; a shared secret)
He thinks I'm at my lessons. Marcella's
taking me to the festa.

CIRILO
Your father finds out --

ANASTASIA
My father wants me in the dark, Cirilo.
(fierce, smiling)
I have decided against the dark.

EXT. THE FLATS -- THE BREGANTE LANE -- NIGHT (THE MADONNA'S FESTA)

The opposite of Volkov's shut house: an enormous, deafening,
warm Italian world. The BREGANTE clan -- Genovese, off the
Ligurian coast -- has spilled into the lane. Long tables. Wine.
Focaccia, and the smell of basil. The men come up from the
boats singing the old way -- TRALLALERO, five throats weaving a
high wild polyphony with no instrument at all.

MARCELLA BREGANTE (16) -- brown-armed, fearless, laughing from
the belly -- has Anastasia by the hand and will not let the
beautiful lonely Russian girl flinch from one bit of it.

MARCELLA
(hauling her into the light)
Eat. You're too sad and too thin and I
won't have it. Here -- you gut the fish
like this, see --

Anastasia, who has spent her life behind a curtain, gets fish
to the wrist, SWEARS in startled Ligurian -- and the whole
table ROARS -- and, for the first time we have seen, she throws
her head all the way back and LAUGHS, the way her mother behind
the curtain was never free enough to.

Cirilo watches the two girls from the rim of the lantern light.
For one night the flats is not a place the county uses and
forgets. It is a feast. And it is winning.

But at the dark edge of it: bluff boys. ROY CALDER and his set,
who keep a particular appetite for a beautiful girl they take to
be beneath them and therefore fair game. They have seen
Anastasia. They circle.

Across the lane, ABEL MENDEZ (20) -- Cirilo's oldest brother,
the gifted one, already half a ruin, and the most alive man in
the county on a night like this -- sees them see her. Sets down
his wine. Cracks his knuckles, almost glad of it.

Trouble coming. Not tonight, though. Tonight the lane sings.

EXT. UNDER THE PLEASURE PIER -- THE BLACK WEDGE -- NIGHT

The boardwalk lights are out; the surf comes up to fill the
silence. In the one place in that whole bright town where a
brown boy and a white girl can stand together without it costing
blood -- the black wedge of shadow beneath the pilings -- CIRILO
and REBECCA stand close.

REBECCA
Tell me something true. You never do.

He lets sand run through his fingers a while.

CIRILO
My family owned this.
(off her look)
Not the pier. The land. All of it -- the
river to the redwoods -- before there was
a Santa Cruz to own it.
(beat)
Now I sell nickels on a plank built on
top of it.

He looks at her in the dark.

CIRILO (CONT'D)
And I'd rather be here. Like this. With
you. Than have a single acre of it back.

She takes his hand -- the pitching hand, the unmarked one -- and
holds it like a thing she has decided to keep.

REBECCA
Then we'll just have to take the rest of
it back ourselves.

A WHISTLE up on the boardwalk. A WATCHMAN'S LAMP swings out over
the rail. They freeze, pressed together, breathing, as the beam
sweeps the sand above their heads.

It is old PEREIRA, the Portuguese night man -- a widower who
buried a wife and three of four children, who walks the empty
pier till dawn because the rooms he goes home to are louder, in
their silence, than the whole Pacific.

He sweeps the lamp out of habit, not zeal. The beam passes over
the black wedge where they stand --

-- and moves on. Pereira shuffles away up the planks, humming
low in a language neither of them knows. A man with no wish left
in him to ruin anybody.

They breathe again. They do not let go.

EXT. THE CAROUSEL HOUSE -- OFF HOURS -- NIGHT

The hand-carved horses of the old flying-horses gallery, still. Old BRAUER, the German
carver who tends them like a watchmaker, jerks his head at the
machine -- go on -- and throws the switch. Band organ OFF.

Alone, in the dark, only the sea for sound, CIRILO rides -- a
poor brown boy on a rich child's toy, around and around, in the
one hour of it that is his. Brauer watches from the shadow and
says nothing. Among the boardwalk people, the kindness that
counts is the kind you don't bill for.

EXT. THE DARK UNDER THE PIER -- NIGHT (THE NIGHT BEFORE)

Surf filling the silences. The one black wedge of the lit town
where a brown boy and a white girl can stand together. CIRILO has
just told her: a Jesuit, an arm, a place up the line, the morning
train. REBECCA does not say the brave thing she rehearsed.

REBECCA
Don't go.
(it surprises them both)
Stay. Be the bottle boy. Let me pay my
nickel all summer and let it be this. I was
raised on those men -- the higher you climb,
the harder they hold the line. Not softer.

CIRILO
Look at where we have to do this. I'm not
staying down here so they'll let me keep you
in a shadow. I'm going up so that one day I
can stand with you in the daylight and not a
man alive has the standing to say no --
because I'll have made the one thing they
can't argue with.

REBECCA
And if it takes you from me a rung at a time
and calls it your success?

CIRILO
(quiet; the whole truth)
I don't want the rancho back. I don't want
any of it. I want a family. A wife who sees
me. A child to carry the name. A roof with a
door I don't have to stand afraid behind.
Every loud thing I'm chasing is just the long
road back to that quiet one. With you.

She has no answer. He is right and so is she, and that is the
whole tragedy of it.

A watchman's lamp swings along the rail. Time.

He crouches. Sets down -- against a piling, where the lamp won't
find it -- the SMALL FIRST GLOVE, the child's one his family made
him. Stands it upright. Says nothing about it.

He does not say HOLD THIS. He does not say I'LL BE BACK. He lets
the leather say it: a man does not leave the truest thing his
family ever made him in a girl's keeping unless he means to come
back for it.

Then he is up out of the dark and gone toward the morning.

ON REBECCA: she saw. She could run -- the planks are empty -- catch
him, press it back, say the brave thing at last. She does not. She
lets him go, and stands alone with the small outgrown glove in her
two hands; and the waiting that will be the whole middle of her
life, quietly, begins.

EXT. THE LITTLE DEPOT -- EDGE OF THE FLATS -- MORNING (1900)

A train waiting. CIRILO in his one good suit, a college letter
buttoned in his breast pocket, a valise that need not be large.
A Jesuit at Santa Clara has seen the arm.

The family comes to the END OF THE LANE -- but not onto the
platform. The platform belongs to the other Santa Cruz, the one
with the money and the timetables. They have learned exactly
where their welcome ends.

ABEL hugs him too long, a little drunk at nine in the morning.

ABEL
(low; the terrible clear sight of the ruined)
Don't come back like me.

He laughs it off and is gone up the lane before Cirilo can
answer. DOLORES takes her son's face in both hands and gives him
the language one last time -- low, fast, a blessing older than
the Mission. Then she presses into his pitching hand the worn olive-wood ROSARY that came north in her grandmother's fist out of Sonora, and folds his fingers down over it.

DOLORES (CONT'D)
(low)
No lo sueltes, mijo. Don't let go.

And ESTEBAN -- gray, ruined tanner's hands working
the brim of his hat, who decided in the night not to say the
thing he wants to say, which is DON'T --

ESTEBAN
They will try to make you small up there.
A politer way than here. But the same.

-- and then, against his whole careful life, the old man folds
his son's pitching hand -- his mother's beads already shut inside it -- into a fist inside his own two stained
ones -- the gesture his own father made, the one he swore in his
fear he never would -- holds it -- lets it go -- and turns up the
lane after his sons without looking back.

ON THE PLATFORM ITSELF, against every rule, a beat past safety:
REBECCA.

REBECCA
(fierce, low, tears refusing to fall where
the platform can see)
Go. Go and become a man so loud they
cannot pretend not to hear your name. And
come back for me when you have.

She presses something into his hand: the kid-leather glove --
the soft one, from the booth.

REBECCA (CONT'D)
So you remember whose hand you're playing
for.

The train CALLS. He closes his fist around the leather and steps
up into the car. He does not look back -- because he learned
that very morning, from his father, that there are partings a
man survives only by refusing to witness them.

EXT. THE MOVING TRAIN -- SWINGING NORTH -- CONTINUOUS

The platform slides away. Rebecca stands in the wrong place a
moment too long -- then makes herself turn and walk, alone, into
a life that has just, in one train whistle, become a waiting.

And UP off the ridge, on the morning wind, comes a RED-TAILED
HAWK. It runs with the train a little way -- out over the bright
water, the drying nets, the hills going gold -- holding station
off the car window the way the first one hung over a dirt yard,
eighteen years before.

ANGLE: DOLORES, walking back up the lane, stops. Sees it. Says
nothing of what she knows -- that it has come to see him out, and
will go only to the edge of the country that is theirs, and not
one wingbeat farther.

The hawk tilts, at the county line, on a held wing. Slides back
south, toward the hill and the bay --

-- and lets the train carry the boy on, alone, north, into the
clear hard country that keeps no such birds.

CIRILO (V.O.)
I would climb. I would not stop climbing.
And whatever it cost me --
(beat)
-- I would not, ever, for any price they
named, agree to be second to the men who
had decided I was born it.

SMASH CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT I.

```

## ACT II — *The Rematch & the Long Wait* (1903–1917)

```
INT. SANTA CLARA COLLEGE -- LECTURE HALL -- DAY (1903)

A public oral examination: a boy alone at the front, construing
Latin before the assembled class. PROFESSOR PRUETT -- a layman
who resents teaching rhetoric to the likes of this one --
presides with a thin courteous smile.

CIRILO (20) construes the Cicero. Pruett runs the examination
long and hard -- doubling back, setting traps in the ablative,
questions he puts to no one else, the room invited to enjoy the
scholarship Mexican exposed at last as a fraud.

Cirilo answers the traps. Finds the verb. Four hundred nights on
this by a borrowed lamp -- because for him there is no genial
pass; he must be BETTER, simply to be allowed equal.

Pruett, furious beneath the courtesy, abandons rhetoric for the
open blade.

PRUETT
Tell me, Mr. Mendez -- do you not think your
talents might be better spent in manual work?
Your people have shown such ... aptitude.

The room goes still. Cirilo -- who took the slap on the pier and
did not swing -- looks at him a long moment. Then answers. In
Latin. Quietly. (We hear it under, in English:)

CIRILO
"It is the mark of a small mind to mistake
the accident of birth for the measure of a
man."
(beat)
Cicero. The passage we were construing,
Professor.

It is NOT in the passage. He composed it on the spot, in correct
Ciceronian Latin, and bet everything that Pruett does not know
the text half so well as the boy who lived four hundred nights
inside it.

Pruett opens his mouth. Closes it. Dares not challenge a citation
he cannot place. Goes red. Ends the examination.

And the gentlemen's sons -- not bad fellows, young enough to love
a winner -- break into a startled APPLAUSE the professor cannot
stop.

EXT. SANTA CLARA BALLFIELD -- DAY (SPRING)

Cardinal red in the grandstand: STANFORD has come down, loud and
certain. And in the sea of red -- half-risen, a kid-leather glove
forgotten in her lap -- REBECCA. Two years since the depot. He
had not expected to know a soul on this field. He was wrong.

At the plate, an S on his chest, tapping the corners as though he
bought them: CHUCK ASHBY.

CHUCK
(down the line, loud for the red seats)
The bottle boy! Three balls a nickel, fellas!

The Stanford side laughs -- the easy laugh of people who never
had to wonder whether the laughter was about them.

Cirilo looks in. Finds Rebecca's eyes across two years. He sets.

The first pitch is past Ashby before the laugh has finished.
STRIKE ONE. The second buckles his knees -- a wet ball that dies
in the air. STRIKE TWO. The bench goes quiet.

He holds the ball a moment. Looks once more at the woman in the
wrong-colored seats. And throws the best thing he owns.

STRIKE THREE.

Silence in the cardinal seats. And one woman, on her feet, not
caring who sees.

CIRILO (V.O.)
I thought beating them on the square would
open the door.
(beat)
It took me years to learn the square was the
only place the door was ever open at all.

EXT. A CLASS-D BALLFIELD -- VALLEY TOWN -- DAY (MONTAGE, 1904--)

A cinder infield baking under a white sky. The bottom of
professional baseball -- Cirilo learning the trade by failing at
it in front of nobody. His catcher: DEACON MULQUEEN (38), a ruin
-- a gut, a thirst, the top of one finger gone to a foul tip.

MULQUEEN
(crouched, spitting)
The battery's a conversation, kid. Not a
command. I'll know you better than you know
yourself -- and you're gonna let me.
(a grin)
That's the one. That's the one, you old
greaser. Throw me that one again.

He loves the boy without ever once saying so -- the way men of
that kind and that era loved. Through the work. Through the
crouch.

EXT. A DEPOT CAFE -- VALLEY TOWN -- NIGHT

Past eleven, the club dead off a doubleheader. The COUNTERMAN
gives Cirilo the quick flat verdict and says it the reasonable
way:

COUNTERMAN
Can't serve the Mexican. Rest of you boys are
welcome.

The young ballplayers shuffle, study their feet. Nobody walks
out -- they're hungry, it isn't their fight, tomorrow there's a
game. Then MULQUEEN fills the doorway. Looks at the counterman.
At the boys. At Cirilo, alone in the heat outside the screen.

MULQUEEN
(a voice built to carry over a crowd)
Well, then. I guess none of us are hungry.

He does not move. He does not stop looking at the boys, one by
one, until -- shamed, grumbling, learning something they did not
come in to learn -- they get up. Every one. And file back out.

EXT. LIM'S CAFE -- BY THE TRACKS -- MIDNIGHT

A mile and a half away, the only other light on. LIM -- a Chinese
man with his own long acquaintance with the quick flat verdict --
feeds all of them, the brown one included, at a back table, for
cash, without a word and without a flicker.

EXT. RECREATION PARK -- THE COAST LEAGUE -- DAY (MONTAGE, ~1910)

Bigger crowds. Real ball. The PACIFIC COAST LEAGUE -- major in
everything but permission. Cirilo, gray coming in at the temples,
two-hits the Angels and walks off to a ROAR.

INT. A GRANDSTAND BOX -- CONTINUOUS

A MAJOR-LEAGUE SCOUT in a derby lowers his field glasses to the
BIRD DOG beside him.

SCOUT
Best arm I've seen all year. Spitter falls
off a cliff. Reads hitters like a cardsharp.

BIRD DOG
(licking his pencil)
So we take him.

The scout says it easily -- the way a man notes a bad hock on a
horse he has decided not to buy, without heat, without the
smallest suspicion that he might be doing a wrong worth pausing
over:

SCOUT
He's thirty next year. And he's a -- you
know. Crowds back east won't warm to it.
Ownership won't spend the ink.
(raising the glasses again)
Write down the kid in left. The blond one.
Half the ballplayer. But he's the right kind
of half.

And already the boy on the grass is gone from his mind --
weather, noted and moved past.

ANGLE: DOWN ON THE GRASS. Cirilo tips his cap to the only crowd
allowed to love him -- and never knows, will never be told, that
a man in a derby just decided his whole life in a sentence, for a
reason no one would ever put on paper.

EXT. A COAST LEAGUE CLUBHOUSE -- DUSK

DORGAN, a Coast League manager, thirty years in the game, takes
Cirilo aside after a game -- the way you do to tell a man
something for his own good.

DORGAN
Best arm in this league and you're going to
rot in it. So I'll say the thing nobody else
will to your face. The bigs would take that
arm tomorrow -- except for the one thing.
(a hand at the whole of him)
And here's what you don't know, son: it's
fixable. Be Spanish. Castilian. A don's
grandson -- which you actually are. Lose the
Mexican. Never mention the tannery. Let them
make you exotic instead of cheap.
(gently)
It isn't even a lie. Nobody's asking you to be
ashamed of your mother. Just to be sensible.
One vowel. A ticket east.

Cirilo watches the dark come down over the outfield. Doesn't
argue.

CIRILO
I'm obliged to you -- you're the first man in
this league to tell me the truth to my face.
(beat)
But my mother keeps a name on a hill the
people who took everything else from us could
never take, because she wouldn't let them. I
won't be the one who hands it over. Not for
the train. Not for any of it.

Dorgan nods. He's sorry. He never raises it again.

EXT. THE FLATS -- THE LANE -- NIGHT (SATURDAY)

Home, between seasons. Six days the flats belong to the kilns and
the canneries. SATURDAY NIGHT the flats belong to itself.

Three countries' suppers down one dirt lane -- beans and chiles
off the comal, Concetta's gravy, linguica spitting in a pan.
NUNZIO on a crate, guitar against his big chest; old CIPRIANO's
fiddle. A party nobody called and nobody could stop.

The old men against the wall, total conviction and no
information:

OLD MAN
You, Beppe? You couldn't have caught a girl
standing still.

They dance in the dirt. ABEL -- a ruin six days of seven -- is,
tonight, the most alive man in the county, dancing the way he
fields. LUIS eats three plates and laughs with his whole body.
Young CIRILO on a step, handed a tortilla folded warm around
beans by a mother who will not, this one night, tell him to go to
bed.

Nobody on the lane owns a thing the county counts. They own this.
And tonight -- it does not turn to sorrow -- the lane just sings.

EXT. A VALLEY TOWN -- A RENTED ROOM -- NIGHT (YEARS ON)

A dollar room, a window that looks at a brick wall. REBECCA --
who walked out of a white house and six servants for this -- has
made it theirs in an hour: the tinted chapel photograph on the
sill, a jar of poppies in a water glass.

CIRILO comes in dark from the park -- a two-hitter in a town whose
cafe wouldn't serve him after. Sits on the bed. Says nothing. The
silence comes off him like cold off a window. She nearly lets the
old fury fly -- and puts it down. Not the feeling. The throwing of
it.

REBECCA
They wouldn't serve you.

CIRILO
No.

REBECCA
Then they're fools, and their coffee is
terrible. And YOU are going to eat better
than any man in that cafe, because I have --
(a magician's flourish, from the washstand)
-- half a cold chicken and a very serious
tomato a Portuguese lady gave me for nearly
nothing, because I admired her grandbaby for
ten honest minutes.

The corner of his mouth goes, despite him.

CIRILO
(grave; their oldest joke)
The tomato is from the Portuguese lady.

REBECCA
The tomato is from the Portuguese lady.

CIRILO
Then it is a good tomato.

Which means nothing to anyone alive but the two of them, and is
the funniest thing either has heard all day. They eat chicken
with their fingers off a chair, laughing low so the next room
won't hear. Later, the dark; he says it to the ceiling, as close
as he can come:

CIRILO (CONT'D)
I'm glad you left the bluff.

REBECCA
(not making him say more)
I know.

EXT./INT. THE EAST -- THE LEAN YEARS -- MONTAGE (1913--1917)

The dream, arrived, tastes of ash. A gray big-league cup of
coffee in BROOKLYN, before a crowd that reads its programs
through his warmups -- a midseason nobody, an old rookie with a
foreign name. And the wage the reserve clause allows an aging
junk-baller will not cover a cold-water flat AND a winter -- so
we find him at a NEWARK TANNERY bench in the off-season, a major-
leaguer who cannot make the rent. His Brooklyn landlady, AOIFE
DOYLE -- an Irish widow with a gold-star flag in the window --
lets the rent slide the hard months and would die before
admitting it.

INT. COLD BROOKLYN ROOMS -- DAY (WINTER 1917)

Snow. Cirilo, gray, in the doorway stamping his boots, his face
doing something with no name on it. A TELEGRAM in his hand.

REBECCA
(rising, the joy already breaking)
What -- what is it --

CIRILO
Boston bought my contract. The Red Sox.
(she gasps -- he stops her, gently, the truth
coming with it)
They want a thirty-five-year-old spitballer
for the price of the train. Because the young
arms are in France. Sixteen years I waited --
and it came because the country ran out of
white arms to send up.

She takes his frozen face in both her hands.

REBECCA
It does not matter why the door opened. It
matters that you walk through it. You have
waited your whole life for a door to open at
all.

CIRILO (V.O.)
Hooper put my name in. Harry Hooper -- a kid
from Hollister, one ridge over from my
brothers. After everything, the man who
finally remembered me was a boy from up the
road.

He looks at the telegram. At Rebecca. At the snow that is not his
snow, in a country that has no hawks.

He is going east.

SMASH CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT II.
```

## ACT III — *Siempre Primero* (1918)

```
INT. COLD BROOKLYN FLAT -- NIGHT (NEW YEAR'S, 1918)

Outside, faint, drunk horns seeing the new year in. Inside: a cry --
a NEWBORN, wet and red. REBECCA, spent and fierce, holds him.

CIRILO crosses the cold floor and takes the small hands -- both
folded into one of his -- the gesture his abuelo made over him
thirty-five years and three thousand miles ago, on a floor that
smelled of lime and hide. He cannot speak. So he says the only words
the family has.

CIRILO
(barely)
Already big. Already strong.

He stands in the cold with his wife, his son, and his father's glove
on the sill -- a thirty-six-year-old rookie with a tannery stain
still under his nails -- and for the length of one held breath he is
not second to any man alive.

EXT. FENWAY PARK -- BOSTON -- A TUESDAY IN JULY (1918)

Heat coming up off the infield in sheets, the war news bad. A spare,
gray-templed man warms up easy -- and does not look like much. But
BABE RUTH wanders over to watch (Ruth always wanders over), and HARRY
HOOPER drifts in off the grass, and by the first hitter there are
three future Hall of Famers and a dying man on a ball field, and only
one of them yet knows which is which.

Cirilo reads their feet. Busts the deep stander up and in. Ties the
plate-crowder in a knot, then floats the dying ball past his swing.
Gives the white-knuckled slugger nothing -- three pitches that leave
his hand looking like strikes and die in the last ten feet.

In the seventh, the bench gone quiet, the bleachers begin it -- low,
then all together, the way a congregation finds a hymn:

CROWD
EL CID -- EL CID -- EL CID --

Ten thousand Boston throats chanting the name of an eight-hundred-
years-dead Castilian at a tanner's son from Santa Cruz, not one of
them hearing the joke folded inside it. Cirilo takes the ball back,
face giving away nothing. But under the iron of him, a boy who once
swept these strangers' planks for nickels stands very still:

CIRILO (V.O.)
It is not my name. But they are on their feet,
and they are saying a name, and I made them do
it. It is the closest they will ever come. And
God forgive me -- after all these years -- I
will take it.

EXT. FENWAY PARK -- A GRAY AFTERNOON (THE DUEL)

The Red Sox against the Senators -- and the Senators have WALTER
JOHNSON. The Big Train. The fastest arm the game has produced: he
beats you with pure speed, the way weather beats you, without
deception and without apology, because he has never once needed any.

And Barrow gives the ball to Cirilo Méndez. The era's purest power
against the old man's purest lie.

For nine innings, nothing scores -- and the not-scoring is the most
thrilling thing those people have seen in years. The crowd comes to
it slowly and then all at once: the dawning understanding that the
old brown junk-baller is matching the Big Train pitch for pitch,
ZERO for ZERO.

It breaks in the eighth -- on craft, not power. Hooper walks, steals
second clean, takes third on a grounder, scores on a sacrifice fly so
shallow they nearly catch him at the plate. ONE run. The only run.

Cirilo sets the Senators down in the ninth. And it is over.

He has beaten Walter Johnson. One to nothing.

And the Boston crowd that walked in that afternoon not wanting to
love him stands, every last one of them, and ROARS.

He does not whoop. He takes the ball back and looks once at the flat
gray New England sky -- out of a habit older than the East -- scanning
it for a bird that is not there and never will be. What rises in him
is not triumph. It is heavier. Vindication -- and under it, grief.

CIRILO (V.O.)
I was this good the whole time.

INT./EXT. THE WORLD SERIES -- THE STRIKEOUT

The box score will flatten it to one letter: K. What it is: bases
loaded, two out, the Cubs' FRED MERKLE digging in. SCHANG crouches.
And the two of them run Merkle through a five-pitch sentence the old
man has been composing since he dug in.

A fastball IN, under the hands -- felt, not hit. (Ball. Merkle
doesn't like it.) The change -- same furious arm, nothing behind it --
and Merkle's whole body commits to a fastball that never comes; his
swing dies a foot out front. (Strike. The flush of shame up a
slugger's neck.) A wasted curve; let the anger work. The fastball IN
again, closer -- Merkle fouls it off his fists, a half-second late,
the inside pitch confirmed now in his fear --

-- which is the whole trap. A hitter guarding the inside fastball has
stopped looking down and away.

Pitch five: the dying ball. Fat, waist-high, full of false promise,
exactly where Merkle's furious guarded eyes want it. He swings from
his heels --

-- and it falls off the table at the last instant like a thing that
gave up the ghost. The bat goes through empty air a hand's width
above it. Schang's mitt takes it at the shin. The umpire's arm goes
up.

The crowd sees a strikeout. The two men who can read -- Schang, and
an old man three thousand miles west in his good chair -- see the
truth: the lying is a kind of genius, and the genius has just, on the
one fair square, in front of everyone, WON.

EXT. THE STREETS OF BOSTON -- NIGHT (THE CELEBRATION)

And then they carry him through the streets. And the streets kill
him.

The first time the city of Boston ever opens its arms to a Méndez --
and it does the thing wholeheartedly: FORTY THOUSAND people shoulder
to shoulder in the wet autumn dark, breathing the same air, passing
the champion overhead hand to hand, his SON held up over the crowd in
a knit cap. For one night Cirilo Méndez is not second to any man in
America.

And the whole town is already sick, and does not know it.

CIRILO (V.O.)
They spent seventy years refusing to embrace my
people. The one time they did -- the one city,
the one night -- it was the embrace that did
what the refusal never had.

He goes down four days later.

INT. A SMALL BOSTON FLAT -- THE ROOM -- NIGHT (NOVEMBER 1918)

Small, clean, full of the wrong quiet. On the sill: the championship
ball, a guttering candle, a rosary from a chapel on land they no
longer own, and a tight little Ohlone basket. REBECCA sits on the
edge of the bed, dry-eyed past crying, their son asleep against her.
In the corner, AOIFE DOYLE's lips move over the beads and do not stop
-- keeping the vigil the family three thousand miles west cannot.

Cirilo is a long way off. But his hand finds the old glove on the
blanket -- his father's leather, his great-grandfather's tanning, the
one thing four generations carried through every theft -- turns it
once in his fingers, and does not give it to Rebecca.

He folds his son's small fingers around it instead.

CIRILO
(they have to lean close)
Abuelo was right. There's a price. For us.
Being first.
(the ghost of the old smile)
Worth it. Tell him -- when you take me home,
tell the old man -- it was worth it.

She does not spend his last clear minutes on a reply. She gives him
instead the thing she carried up out of her grief on purpose, to give
him in time.

REBECCA
You weren't second.
(into the quiet)
Not for one day of your life.

He hears that. His hand goes still on his son's hand on the glove.
Aoife does not stop praying. And Rebecca, through her tears, is
looking at a Méndez's hands.

Small ones now.

Already strong.

SMASH TO BLACK.
```

## CODA

```
EXT. THE SANTA CRUZ FLATS -- DAY (OCTOBER 1918) -- TWO WIRES

The news comes home twice in one October, on the same wire, two weeks
and one whole world apart.

FIRST it runs through the flats like current: they WON -- the Boston
club -- and one of ours pitched them to it, all the way at the top of
the world. Men who cannot read have the box scores read aloud in the
cantina and weep among the bottles. NUNZIO closes the fruit stand and
stands among the crates with his face working. For two weeks the
poorest neighborhood in the county walks an inch taller. Uno de
nosotros. One of ours. All the way to the top.

And then the SECOND wire comes. And it is the influenza. And he is
dead.

DOLORES takes it at the stove, standing. Finishes what she is
cooking, because there are people who have to eat. Then climbs the
hill in the rain.

EXT. THE HILL ABOVE THE BAY -- DAY (NOVEMBER 1918) -- THE BURIAL

They bring him home across the whole width of the country he was
never quite allowed to belong to, and give him back to the ground
that made him.

And the town comes -- the thing none of them expected. The TANNERY
SHUTS ITS GATES for the afternoon, the first time in its history it
has shut them for a Mexican, and Esteban's old crew stand at the
grave with their cracked, leather-colored hands folded. The fishermen
come up from the wharf. The flats families stand in the open October
light and speak, out loud, the language they speak only behind closed
doors -- because the boy in the box made the whole country learn, for
one season, to say a name like theirs, with its hat in its hands.

DON AURELIO stands at the edge of land that once ran from the river to
the redwoods -- all of it theirs and none of it theirs -- and
understands at last that he read the family's curse exactly backward
for ninety years.

And as they lower the box, a RED-TAILED HAWK comes up off the ravine
on the wet wind and hangs over the open grave, motionless against the
gray.

Not one person in that small soaked grieving family takes it for a
coincidence. They have never once believed the world is the kind of
place where that would be one.

DOLORES (V.O.)
(soft; the old knowledge)
El Halcón. The hawk. They called him every name
but his. Now the family takes him back -- and gives
him the right one.

The hawk tilts, at last, on a held wing -- and slides off north along
the ridge, into the cloud, unhurried, having seen what it came to
see.

EXT. THE LIVING COAST -- THE SAME DAY -- MONTAGE (UNDER THE SUN)

And all around the small black knot of grieving, the coast does the
one thing it has always done, which is to live:

A lone SEA OTTER -- one of the last of them -- rides the kelp off the
point, cracks an urchin on a stone over its own heart, eats in the
rain, warm in its fur. GRAY WHALES track south on the old road in the
water, ten thousand years older than the hill or the country that dug
it. Up the canyon the first rains run the river brown and loud and the
STEELHEAD nose in off the tide to climb. A coyote on a deer trail,
nose down, indifferent and entirely alive.

The sun comes up over the water the next morning the way it has come
up over that water always -- and asks the ground that holds Cirilo
Méndez no question at all about his name, or his skin, or his place in
any line.

The one homecoming no man and no country could ever bar him from.

EXT. THE SAME GROUND -- TIME RUNNING FORWARD -- TO PRESENT DAY

The hill, the houses, the smoke -- and then time runs forward over all
of it: the houses thin, a cannery rises and falls, a vacant field, and
finally the ASPHALT comes back up, the chain-link, the storage yard,
the lot no one looks at twice.

(We have been here. This is where we began.)

And over it -- still -- the OLD REDWOOD. And on its high limb, the
RED-TAILED HAWK, the gold eye fixed on the ground below.

EXT. HARVEY WEST PARK -- FIELD 1 -- CONTINUOUS (PRESENT DAY)

And down on the green, the playoff we saw at dawn. The BOY on the
mound toes the rubber, looks in, and throws a pitch that drops off the
table like a thing that gave up the ghost -- and the hitter swings a
hand's width over it.

Behind the backstop, an OLD MAN at the fence watches, and says it to
nobody -- the way it has been said on that ground for a hundred years,
by people who never once saw the man pitch:

OLD MAN
... throws like Méndez.

The boy does not hear it. Does not know the name. He just got somebody
out, and the morning belongs to him.

The relay, unbroken. The name, still said.

EXT. UP THE SAN LORENZO -- A FOLD OF THE RIDGE -- TIMELESS

A REDWOOD the loggers somehow missed -- a sapling when Rome was still a
town worth conquering -- stands in its own fog and drinks the sea's
breath. And to the tree the whole of it (the conqueror and the
conquered, the rope and the prayer and the box score, every furious
human sorting of who was first and who was second) is one short wet
afternoon's weather, passing over roots that were old before the
question was ever asked.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
Not primero. Not segundo.
(beat)
Siempre.

The fog combs down through the great tree, and is given to the creek,
and the creek to the sea, and the sea back up as fog -- world, and
world, and world.

FADE TO BLACK.

TITLE:   SIEMPRE SEGUNDO

FADE OUT.

THE END.
```
