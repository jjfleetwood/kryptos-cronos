# SIEMPRE SEGUNDO

*"He was born second. He died first."*

A multigenerational saga — feature screenplay + companion novel — loosely inspired by the life of Frank Arellanes, the Californio pitcher from Santa Cruz. **Fiction. Names and events invented.** A motivational small-town-Chicano-to-hero story with a tragic ending — and a victory deeper than any trophy.

---

## The thesis — *Siempre Segundo*

> **This is what the picture is about.** It is an argument for the dignity of the runner-up — that a life is measured not by the trophy you lift but by how you keep working when you are *always second*: second-class, second-looked-at, second-chosen, the second of your blood ever to reach the top. Frank spends thirty-five years second and a single autumn first, and then he dies. The trophy was never the point. **The worth was in the striving through all the years no one was watching — and the real victory is the root he planted and the people he lifted, which outlive him.** Keep working. Especially when you're second. *Siempre.*

Two things make that thesis land hard:

- **The Brooklyn club is the perfect vessel.** Baseball's eternal also-rans — "Wait Till Next Year," the lovable losers who lose the Series and show up again anyway. The team's whole identity *is* the theme; Frank's one season makes the second-place team first, and it costs him everything.
- **It is the wound of the Mexican-American.** A people who had their world taken and were told to keep their heads down and keep working — made second-class on the very land that was first theirs. *Siempre segundo* is at once a virtue to live by and an injustice to rage against, and the film holds both without resolving them.

And the win is real. He **wins the championship** — the literal trophy — so the audience gets the triumph every good story earns. But the *true* victory, the one the film closes on, is the **root of life** he established (a son, born on the first day of the year) and the **legacy to his community** — a dispossessed Chicano town that learns from one of its own that you reach anyway, you work anyway, and the name they tried to erase becomes the name the kids shout on the sandlot for a hundred years.

---

## Themes & the leather thread

**Leather is the visual through-line** — the single object the camera (and the prose) returns to across three centuries, the way we keep the thesis in frame:

- **Hides as wealth.** On the rancho, sun-dried cowhides are stacked like money — "the banknotes of California" — and the family tans its own leather on its own land.
- **Leather as wage.** After the land is taken, the same hands that owned the hide trade trim another man's hides for a Prussian's wages at Kron's tannery. The craft survives; the standing is gone.
- **Leather as escape.** A baseball is stitched cowhide; a glove is tanned leather. The boy's way out of the tannery is the same material that trapped his father — reshaped into a ball and a mitt.
- **Leather as legacy.** The film opens and closes on a single worn glove. The last image is that glove in the small hands of his son.

Underneath the leather, the saga is about **dispossession & reinvention**, **the rooted treated as foreign**, the cruel arithmetic of **always second**, and **the body that betrays** — first the breaking labor, then the fever in the air.

## Principal characters

- **FRANK SOTO** — our pitcher (b. 1882). Quiet, proud, watchful; a cannon for a right arm. Born on the tannery floor; raised on the boardwalk. The last hope of a fallen family. *(Working surname "Soto" — placeholder.)*
- **DON AURELIO SOTO** — the abuelo. Last patriarch of a rancho that no longer exists. Keeper of the Mexican past and the family's conscience; our prologue narrator.
- **ESTEBAN SOTO** — Frank's father. A hide-trimmer at Kron's tannery. Wants his son safe and small; love expressed as fear.
- **DOLORES SOTO** — Frank's mother. The rosary, the language, the kitchen that holds the family together.
- **CLARA WHITFIELD** — Anglo, wealthy; meets Frank at the beach (~17), then goes up to **Stanford** — which puts her in the grandstand the day Chet's Stanford nine faces Frank's Santa Clara. The woman who crosses every line she was raised inside, and marries him.
- **CHESTER "CHET" ASHBY III** — old San Francisco money, Clara's intended. Plays for **Stanford**. Our antagonist — the embodiment of the class that inherited the family's land.
- **LUIS & ABEL SOTO** — Frank's older brothers; the meat market, the sandlot, the Santa Cruz Beachcombers.
- **"UNCLE" — the manager** — the gruff Brooklyn skipper who finally bets on Frank. *(Period-real option: Wilbert "Uncle Robbie" Robinson.)*

## Structure — three acts + prologue

**PROLOGUE — "California Banknotes" (1833–1882).** The Mexican golden age and its theft, told as Don Aurelio's memory: the secularization grant, the rancho at its height (vaqueros, the *matanza*, hides as money, the fandango, Branciforte rowdy across the river), the false promise of Guadalupe Hidalgo, the slow strangulation by the Land Act of 1851 and the drought, the fall into wage labor — ending on a match cut from a wet new hide to a newborn: Frank, 1882, born on the tannery floor.

**ACT I — "The Boardwalk" (~1900–1903).** Frank, eighteen, works the brand-new pleasure pier — labor in the leisure economy built on the land his family lost. The Whitfields summer at the beach; Clara and Frank collide. Chet humiliates him; Frank answers with the only thing the Anglo world can't argue with — his arm. The attraction to Clara becomes perilous. A scout (or a Santa Clara man) sees the arm. Frank leaves the beach carrying the family's buried hope of rising again.

**ACT II — "The Rematch & The Long Wait" (~1901–1916).** Santa Clara as a class threshold. The hinge: the **Stanford–Santa Clara game** — Clara in the grandstand, Chet at the plate, Frank on the mound; the boardwalk paid back with a strikeout, and Clara chooses. Then the cruelty of the long middle: a decade of being passed over, the exotic "Spanish" spitballer who can't get his call while lesser, paler men do. *Always second.* The act ends ~1917 with the call finally coming — and Frank and Clara marrying in the teeth of both their worlds.

**ACT III — "Siempre Primero" (1917–1918).** The bigs at last — at thirty-six, the oldest rookie anyone's seen, with baseball's eternal second-place club. On **January 1, 1918**, a son is born. Then the season of his life: a rookie sensation who pitches the team to the pennant and the World Series championship. For one autumn the man who was always second is *first*. Then the influenza takes him within days — but the root of life and the legacy he leaves are the real victory.

## PROLOGUE — *California Banknotes*

### Novel prose

Before this was a town for strangers — before the lights on the water and the laughing on the pier — it was ours. All of it. The old man said it the way other men said prayers, which is to say he said it until the words wore smooth and stopped meaning anything to anyone but him.

In the year the bells came down — 1833, when Mexico unlocked the mission and handed God's land back to men — a governor's pen made the Sotos rancheros. The grant ran from the river to the redwood line, hills the color of a lion in August, and on it the family put cattle and a chapel and a low white house that held the cool like a cellar. They were *gente de razón*: people of reason, people of name, Mexicans and Catholics and, above all, owners.

The wealth came on the hoof and left on the hide. Each spring the vaqueros rode the cattle down out of the hills for the *matanza*, and the killing-ground ran red and then the hides went out to dry, staked in their hundreds to the ground like a field of dark windows. The Yankee ships stood off the cove and the Sotos rowed the hides out and rowed back with silver and silk and iron, because a cowhide was as good as coin in those years — better; men called them *California banknotes*, and laughed, and meant it. And on the rancho, in a shed that smelled of oak bark and lime, the family tanned its own leather from its own herds on its own ground. Remember that. The leather was theirs before it was anyone's.

Across the San Lorenzo the pueblo of Branciforte gambled and brawled and drank — a town the Spanish had stocked, a lifetime before, with the contents of a Guadalajara jail — and on feast nights the guitars carried over the water to the rancho's own fandango, so that the sacred bank of the river and the profane bank of the river sang to each other in the dark. It seemed it would last forever. Nothing built on land ever does.

The thunder came from the north. A flag at Sonoma; a war; the stars-and-stripes run up over Monterey where the eagle of Mexico had flown that morning. And then, in '48, the paper that was supposed to save them: Guadalupe Hidalgo, which swore on the honor of the United States that the grants would be respected, that what was theirs would stay theirs. Don Aurelio's father believed it. That was his only crime, and it cost him everything.

For the conquest did not come with a sword. It came with a courthouse. The Land Act of 1851 made every Californio prove, in English, before a commission in a city three days' ride north, that the ground under his own chapel was his — and even the families who won bled to death paying the lawyers who won it for them. Squatters fenced the range. The cattle wandered and were not returned. Then the sky itself turned against them: the flood that drowned the valley, and after it the long drought of '64 that left the hills white with bone. They sold the rancho the way a man bleeds — not all at once, but a little at a time, until there is no more.

By the time Don Aurelio was an old man, the new Santa Cruz had risen on the grave of his: lime kilns smoking on the hill, the long wharf walking out into the bay, strangers' money everywhere and the family's name on nothing. The language went indoors. The rosary stayed out.

And on a winter morning in 1882, his son Esteban walked down off the hill and through the gate of Kron's tannery to do for a Prussian's wages the one thing the Sotos had always known how to do — turn hide into leather — only now the hide was another man's, and the leather was another man's, and all that was left to the family was the knowing. *They left us our hands,* the old man would say. *So we gave them to the leather.*

That same morning, in the back room where the women waited, a child was born — wet and red and roaring, the way a new hide comes off the beam. They wiped him clean and the old man looked at the boy's hands, already big, already strong, and did not yet know what they would do. He only knew the family was not finished. Not while there was a Soto with hands.

```
We sold it the way a man bleeds. A
```

## ACT I — *The Boardwalk* (1900)

### Screenplay — the meeting

```
EXT. SANTA CRUZ BEACH -- THE NEW PLEASURE PIER -- DAY (1900)

Fresh-cut planks, white paint, electric bulbs strung against
a blue sky. The leisure economy, newborn -- built on the very
ground the prologue mourned.

FRANK SOTO (18) hauls a crate of bottles down the boards. He
does not look at the summer people. He has learned not to.

A BANNER: "TEST YOUR ARM -- 3 BALLS A NICKEL." Frank takes his
post behind a pyramid of iron milk-bottles.

CHET ASHBY III (20), white flannels, steers CLARA WHITFIELD
(17) to the booth. She is already bored of him.

CHET
Watch. I rowed stroke at the club.

He throws three. Misses, misses, clips the pyramid -- it holds.

CHET (CONT'D)
(to Frank, not really to him)
Rigged. They weight the bottom row.
Aren't they weighted, boy?

Frank says nothing. He picks up one ball -- the stitched
leather, an old friend -- and without seeming to wind up at
all, throws.

CRACK. The pyramid does not fall over. It LEAVES, all six
bottles blown clean off the shelf.

Clara laughs -- a real one, surprised out of her.

CLARA
Do it again.

FRANK
(quiet)
Costs a nickel.

She holds his eye a beat too long. Chet sees it.

CLARA
(producing a coin)
Then here's a nickel.
```

### Novel prose — the same beat, interior

He had learned not to look at the summer people, which was a thing you learned the way you learned not to touch the lime vats — once, and never again. So he was looking at the bottles, and the worn ball in his hand, when the girl laughed.

It was not the laugh they used on the pier, the bright tin one the young ladies kept in their purses with their handkerchiefs. It came out of her sideways and surprised, as if he had knocked it loose along with the bottles. Frank had thrown a baseball through six iron bottles and felt nothing; one honest laugh from a stranger and his ears went hot.

The boy in the white flannels had felt it too, the shift in the air, the way a dog feels weather. *Aren't they weighted, boy.* Frank knew that voice. It was the voice that had bought the rancho at the courthouse and never once had to raise itself. He had been answering it with his silence his whole life, because silence was the only thing they couldn't take and couldn't use against you. But the girl was holding out a nickel and looking at him as though he were a person and not a fixture of the pier, and the old man's voice was somewhere in him saying *not while there is a Soto with hands* — and Frank understood, with the flat cold clarity of a thing already decided, that the silence was going to cost him more than it ever had.

"Then here's a nickel," she said.

He took it. Her glove was kid leather, soft as a thing his father might have made in another life, when leather still belonged to them. He gave her a ball, and tried not to think about whose hands had tanned the hide of her glove, and failed.

## ACT II — *The Rematch* (1903)

### Screenplay — Stanford at Santa Clara

```
EXT. SANTA CLARA COLLEGE -- BALL GROUND -- DAY (1903)

A college crowd. A score board: STANFORD vs. SANTA CLARA. The
Stanford side is louder, richer, sure of itself.

In the grandstand, among the cardinal red: CLARA WHITFIELD, a
Stanford woman now. She did not expect to know anyone on the
field. She is wrong.

ON THE MOUND: FRANK SOTO, 21, Santa Clara flannels gone gray
with dust.

AT THE PLATE: CHET ASHBY III, cardinal "S" on his chest,
tapping the corners like he owns them.

CHET
(grinning)
Well. The bottle boy.

Frank says nothing. He has always said nothing. He looks in --
and THERE, in the red, is Clara, half-risen, a kid-leather
glove forgotten in her lap. Their eyes meet across four years.

CHET (CONT'D)
(to his bench, loud)
Three balls a nickel, fellas!

Laughter from the Stanford side. Frank sets. The first pitch
is past Chet before the laugh is done. STRIKE ONE. The second
buckles his knees. STRIKE TWO.

Frank holds the ball a moment -- the stitched leather, an old
friend -- looks once at Clara, and throws the best thing he
owns.

STRIKE THREE. Chet swings at air and nearly screws himself
into the dirt.

The Santa Clara side erupts. And Clara is on her feet, alone
in a sea of red, clapping -- for the wrong team, for the wrong
boy, in front of everyone she knows. She doesn't care. That is
the whole story of her, right there.

FRANK
(under his breath, to no one)
Costs a nickel.
```

## ACT III — *Siempre Primero* (1918) — THE CLIMAX

He has waited sixteen years for this. The boy from the tannery floor — passed over a hundred times, the wrong color for the call that kept going to lesser arms — finally stands on a big-league mound, at thirty-six, in the deciding game of the World Series, pitching for the team that has *never* won, the eternal second-place club. On New Year's Day his son was born. Now the man who was always second is one out from first.

And he is burning alive. The fever came up in the third inning and has not let go.

### Screenplay — the fever game

```
EXT. THE BALLPARK -- WORLD SERIES, DECIDING GAME -- LATE
AFTERNOON (OCTOBER 1918)

Thirty thousand people on their feet. Bunting on the rails.
Scattered through the crowd: white gauze MASKS -- the flu is
already in the city, though no one yet calls it what it is.

ON THE MOUND: FRANK, 36. Soaked through. Gray under the brown.
The ball shakes a little in his hand. Bottom of the ninth, two
out, the tying run on, a full count. One strike from the first
championship this team has ever known.

The MANAGER ("UNCLE") comes halfway out, sees Frank's face,
and stops.

UNCLE
You're done, son. You're white as the
ball. Let me bring the kid in.

Frank shakes his head. Just once.

FRANK
I've been second my whole life, skip.
(beat)
Not today.

Uncle looks at him a long moment -- then puts the ball back in
his hand and walks away.

In the box seats: CLARA, the ten-month-old BOY on her hip in a
knit cap. DON AURELIO beside her, ancient now, hat over his
heart. He is not watching the batter. He is watching his
grandson's hands.

Frank looks in. The stitched leather, an old friend. He
breathes. He does not wind up big. He never did.

He throws the best thing he owns.

The world goes silent the way it does only in memory.

STRIKE THREE.

And the ballpark comes apart. The bench empties. They reach
Frank at the mound the way the sea reaches a rock, and lift
him -- the dispossessed boy, the bottle boy, the spitballer
nobody called for sixteen years -- up onto their shoulders,
above the whole roaring city, a CHAMPION.

He finds Clara in the noise. Holds up the BALL toward the boy
like a man showing a coin. Then his eyes roll, his knees go --
and the lift becomes a CATCH. They lower him gently, gently,
into the dirt he just conquered.

DON AURELIO (V.O.)
He was first. For one afternoon, in
front of everyone, my grandson was
first.

SMASH TO BLACK.
```

### Screenplay — the room, days later

```
INT. SOTO APARTMENT, BROOKLYN -- NIGHT (DAYS LATER)

Small. Clean. A championship BALL on the sill beside a
guttering candle and the rosary from the prologue. The

FRANK in the bed, far away now. CLARA at its edge, dry-eyed

Frank's hand finds the old worn GLOVE on the blanket -- the

in the BOY'S -- folds the small fingers around it.

FRANK
(barely)
Tell him... it was never the trophy.
(beat)
Tell him we keep working. Even second.
(the ghost of a smile)
Especially second.

CLARA
(whisper)
You weren't second. Not for one day of
your life.

He hears it. That is the gift she came all this way to give
him, and she gives it in time. His hand goes still on the
boy's hand on the glove.

DON AURELIO does not stop praying. But he is looking, again,
```

### Novel prose — the real victory

They buried him in Santa Cruz, because Clara understood that a man belongs to the ground that made him even when that ground was stolen, and she carried him home on the train across the whole width of the country he was never quite allowed to belong to.

And the town came. That was the thing none of them expected — the town came. The tannery shut its gates for the afternoon, the first time it had ever shut them for a Mexican, and Esteban's old crew stood with their cracked leather hands folded. The fishermen came up from the wharf. The families came down out of the flats and in from the orchards, the people who spoke the language only indoors, and they stood in the open and spoke it, because the boy in the box had made it so that for one season the whole country had to learn to say a name like theirs and say it with its hat off. Don Aurelio stood at the grave on the edge of land that had once run from the river to the redwoods, all of it theirs, none of it theirs, and he did not weep for the land anymore. The land was never the victory. He had been wrong about that his whole long life.

Because here is what a trophy is: it is a cup in a case in a city three thousand miles away, and the boys who carried Frank Soto on their shoulders will be old men and then no men, and the cup will gather the dust of strangers. That was the *first*. It was real, and it was glorious, and it was borrowed, and it was brief, the way being first always is.

And here is what a root is. In the summers after, the boys of the flats played ball on the flat ground by the river where the rancho's cattle had once come down to drink, and when a kid threw one nobody could hit, the others would go quiet and then somebody would say it — *throws like Soto* — and not one of them had ever seen him pitch. They only knew the story: that one of theirs, a tannery son off this exact dirt, had stood on the highest mound in the country and been *first*, and that he had kept working when the answer was no for sixteen straight years, and that the working had been the man, not the cup. A town that had spent a century trying to forget the family could not, now, stop saying its name. *Siempre segundo,* the old man had taught them all — and then taught them the rest of it, the part that mattered: you reach anyway. You plant anyway. You hand the glove to the small strong hands and you go.

The boy grew up on that ground with his father's glove, the leather his great-grandfather tanned, soft now from a third generation of use. He was the root. He was the legacy. He was the victory that does not gather dust — the one thing the courthouse and the conquest and the fever in the air could never staple a stranger's name to.

The last image is his hands closing around the ball.

Already big. Already strong.
